# Research Dump: La Marmotte Granfondo Alpes

## Quick Facts
- 174-177km / 108-110mi, 5,000-5,530m / 16,400-18,143ft climbing (route varies slightly by year)
- 4 major HC/Cat 1 climbs: Col du Glandon (or Croix de Fer), Col du Telegraphe, Col du Galibier, Alpe d'Huez
- Start: Bourg d'Oisans (720m) -> Finish: Alpe d'Huez (1,860m)
- Sunday 28 June 2026 (44th edition). Typically late June / early July
- ~7,500 entries. 88% international participation (~6,600 riders from abroad). 60+ nationalities
- Founded 1982 in the Oisans region of the French Alps. Named "La Doyenne" (the old lady) in France -- borrowing the nickname of Liege-Bastogne-Liege
- Named after the alpine marmot (ground squirrel) that inhabits the slopes of Alpe d'Huez
- EUR 110-130 tiered entry fee (first 500 at EUR 110, next 1,000 at EUR 120, next 2,000 at EUR 125, final spots EUR 130). Day licence ~EUR 15 extra if no racing licence. Rando des Marmottes (2-day option): EUR 85
- Wave start format: Wave 1 departs ~06:30, Wave 2 ~06:50, subsequent waves at intervals. Riders assigned to start pens with designated entry and start times
- Mandatory: approved helmet, medical certificate (doctor's note within 1 year or UCI-recognised racing licence). No e-bikes
- Route options: (1) La Marmotte Granfondo (full 174-177km single day), (2) La Rando des Marmottes (same route split over 2 days: Day 1 Bourg d'Oisans to Valloire 97km/2,500m, Day 2 Valloire to Alpe d'Huez 80km/2,500m; conventional or e-bike allowed)
- Road closure: NOT fully closed roads. Partial road closures with rolling closures for the lead group. Unlike L'Etape du Tour, motorized traffic is present on some sections
- Cutoff: Must pass the foot of Alpe d'Huez in Bourg d'Oisans by ~18:15 (varies by year, sometimes 18:00 or 19:00, published in advance). Broom wagon follows last rider on Alpe d'Huez
- Glandon descent is neutralized (timing frozen) since 2010 to discourage dangerous descending
- Medal system: Gold, Silver, Bronze based on age/sex categories. Example: men 18-29 gold <8h29m, silver <10h35m; women 18-34 gold <10h03m, silver <12h04m (neutralized descent time excluded)
- Fastest finishers: ~5h45m. Slowest finishers: up to 13 hours
- Organiser: Local organisation committee in the Oisans region. Title association: Marmotte Granfondo series (also runs Marmotte Granfondo Pyrenees, Marmotte Granfondo Valais)
- Entry includes: signposted route, breakdown assistance, first aid, feed stations, timing chip (on handlebars), medical service, souvenir, general + category rankings, diploma (Gold/Silver/Bronze)

## Course Profile

**Overview**: The Marmotte is widely regarded as the hardest single-day sportive in Europe. The route starts in Bourg d'Oisans at 720m, immediately climbs the Col du Glandon (or Croix de Fer), descends into the Maurienne Valley, climbs the Col du Telegraphe and Col du Galibier back-to-back (2,000m of climbing over 35km), then descends 48km via the Col du Lautaret back to Bourg d'Oisans, before the final brutal ascent of Alpe d'Huez's 21 hairpin bends. Four HC/Cat 1 climbs in a single day, replicating some of the most legendary Tour de France mountain stages.

### The Climbs

#### 1. Col du Glandon (from Bourg d'Oisans / Allemond) -- HC
- **Distance**: 21.3km (from the Maurienne side, La Chambre) / ~25-26km from Bourg d'Oisans via Allemond
- **Elevation gain**: 1,367-1,472m (depending on start point)
- **Average gradient**: 6.5-6.9%
- **Maximum gradient**: 12% (final 2km)
- **Summit altitude**: 1,924m (6,312ft)
- **Category**: HC (Hors Categorie)
- **Profile**: The first major challenge. A long, steady climb from the valley floor. The gradient is moderate through the lower and middle sections but increases significantly in the final 2km, where stretches reach 10-12%. The road passes through alpine pastures and forest, with the Lac de Grand'Maison reservoir visible en route.
- **Road surface**: Generally good French departmental road. Some rougher patches on the upper sections.
- **Note**: The Col du Glandon replaced the original Col de la Croix de Fer around 2006 due to roadworks. The two climbs share the same road from Bourg d'Oisans, diverging only near the summit. In 2021 and 2023, the route reverted to the Croix de Fer. The 2023 edition also added the Col du Mollard (extending distance to 186km/5,530m). The specific first climb varies by year.

#### Col de la Croix de Fer (alternative first climb) -- HC
- **Distance**: ~30km from Bourg d'Oisans (via Allemond and Lac de Grand'Maison)
- **Elevation gain**: ~1,505m
- **Average gradient**: 5.4% (overall from Bourg d'Oisans)
- **Maximum gradient**: 13%
- **Summit altitude**: 2,067m (6,781ft)
- **Category**: HC (Hors Categorie)
- **Profile**: Same road as the Glandon approach until near the summit, then continues a further 3km and 143m higher to the Croix de Fer summit. Features several descents mid-climb that chill the legs and add additional climbing. Iron cross monument at summit. Has featured in the Tour de France 19+ times since 1947.

#### 2. Col du Telegraphe (from Saint-Michel-de-Maurienne) -- Cat 1
- **Distance**: 11.8km
- **Elevation gain**: 846m
- **Average gradient**: 7.1-7.2%
- **Maximum gradient**: 9.8% (summit section)
- **Summit altitude**: 1,566m (5,138ft)
- **Category**: Cat 1
- **Profile**: A steady, consistent climb through wooded switchbacks. The gradient rarely drops below 6% and sustains 7-8% for most of the ascent. Seven hairpins cover the final 1.3km at 9.3%, followed by 400m straight at 10.6% to the summit. Named for the telegraph/relay towers visible from a distance. Acts as the "gateway" to the Galibier -- a short descent to Valloire (1,403m) separates the two climbs, but mentally and physically they form one continuous block.
- **Road surface**: Wide, well-maintained ski-road quality asphalt. Good visibility, well-surfaced corners.

#### 3. Col du Galibier (from Valloire) -- HC
- **Distance**: 18.2km (from Valloire at 1,403m)
- **Elevation gain**: 1,239m
- **Average gradient**: 6.9-7.0%
- **Maximum gradient**: 9.5-10% (some sources cite steeper ramps up to 12.1% near the summit)
- **Summit altitude**: 2,642m (8,668ft)
- **Category**: HC (Hors Categorie)
- **Profile**: The highest point on the route and one of the most famous climbs in cycling history. From Valloire, the road climbs steadily through Plan Lachat and past the old tunnel (2,556m) to the summit. The final 4-5km above 2,200m are the hardest -- altitude effects become significant, the gradient spikes to 10%+, and weather conditions can be extreme. Henri Desgrange memorial stele near the summit commemorates the Tour de France founder. First used in the Tour in 1911, featured 63+ times. Panoramic views of the Meije, Ecrins massif, and Mont Blanc on clear days. The 2,642m summit is the third-highest paved pass on the Route des Grandes Alpes.
- **Road surface**: Variable. Generally acceptable on the main road, but the top section (final 2km) has notably poor surface quality with narrow roads and no guardrails. The descent from the summit is technical and exposed.
- **Weather**: A wind jacket is MANDATORY for the descent, even in midsummer. Temperatures at the summit can be near-freezing even in late June/July. Snow patches possible. Wind exposure severe.
- **Combined Telegraphe + Galibier**: From Saint-Michel-de-Maurienne, the combined ascent is 34.8km gaining 2,120m at 6.1% average -- one of the longest continuous climbing blocks in European cycling.

#### 4. Alpe d'Huez (from Bourg d'Oisans) -- HC
- **Distance**: 13.8km
- **Elevation gain**: 1,143m
- **Average gradient**: 8.1%
- **Maximum gradient**: 13% (early hairpins near La Garde)
- **Summit altitude**: 1,860m (6,102ft) (some sources: 1,841-1,880m depending on exact finish location)
- **Category**: HC (Hors Categorie)
- **21 hairpin bends** (lacets), numbered in reverse from 21 at the bottom to 1 at the top. Each bend is named after a Tour de France stage winner on Alpe d'Huez (e.g., Bend 21: Fausto Coppi, Bend 1: Lance Armstrong -- now struck from the record).
- **Profile**: The final and most iconic climb. After ~48km of descending from the Galibier via the Col du Lautaret to Bourg d'Oisans, riders face 13.8km at 8.1% average with devastated legs. The first 6 bends up to La Garde are the steepest, averaging around 10-11%. The gradient is remarkably consistent for the first 12km, rarely straying from 8-10%. The road briefly eases through Huez village before a final kick to the resort. 77.5% of the climb is at 5-10% average grade.
- **Road surface**: Excellent. Wide, well-maintained road built for ski resort traffic. Smooth asphalt, good visibility, well-marked corners. One of the best-surfaced major climbs in the Alps.
- **Context**: Alpe d'Huez has hosted 33 Tour de France stage finishes since 1952. The Dutch have a special affinity -- 8 Dutch riders have won stages here, making it "the Dutch mountain." On Tour de France days, up to 500,000 spectators line the 21 bends. The Marmotte finish on Alpe d'Huez captures a fraction of this atmosphere, but the setting is unmistakable.

### Descent Sections

**Glandon descent (21km, neutralized)**: The descent from the Glandon summit to Saint-Etienne-de-Cuines in the Maurienne Valley is technical, narrow, and dangerous. The first four bends are particularly treacherous. Timing is frozen for this section since 2010, and riders are instructed to descend carefully. Crashes are commonplace when riders descend recklessly.

**Galibier/Lautaret descent (48km to Bourg d'Oisans)**: The long descent from the Galibier summit back to Bourg d'Oisans via the Col du Lautaret. The first few kilometers from the summit are extremely dangerous -- narrow roads, very poor road surface, no guardrails, steep drop-offs. After the Col du Lautaret (2,058m), the descent becomes faster and more open, rolling down the Romanche valley for ~42km. The lower section is a great reward -- fast, not too technical, wide road. But riders must manage energy and nutrition here, as the final climb of Alpe d'Huez begins immediately at the bottom.

### Road Surface Summary
- Overall road surface quality: Good to very good on the main routes. French departmental and national roads are generally well-maintained
- Exceptions: Glandon upper sections (rougher), Galibier summit descent (poor surface, narrow, no barriers), some mid-mountain sections with patchy repairs
- No gravel, no unpaved sections. 100% asphalt
- Road markings and signage: Well-signposted event route

### Feed Zones
- **Col du Glandon summit**: First major feed station
- **Valloire** (between Telegraphe and Galibier): Second feed station
- **Col du Galibier** (~2km below summit, at the tunnel): Third feed station
- **Bourg d'Oisans** (foot of Alpe d'Huez): Fourth feed station
- Stations provide water, sports drinks, energy food, fruit
- Many riders carry additional nutrition given the 7-13 hour duration

## Logistics

### Nearest Airports with Drive Times
- **Grenoble-Isere Airport (GNB)**: 139km, ~1hr 10min. Closest airport but limited flight options (mostly seasonal/budget airlines from UK)
- **Lyon Saint-Exupery (LYS)**: 145km, ~2hr. Major international hub. TGV station in terminal. Best for long-haul connections
- **Geneva (GVA)**: ~220km, ~3hr. Major international hub. Most popular for UK/Northern European riders due to extensive low-cost flight options. Cross-border (Switzerland/France)
- **Chambery (CMF)**: ~130km, ~1hr 40min. Small regional airport
- **Turin Caselle (TRN)**: ~250km, ~3hr. Alternative for Italian approach via the Frejus tunnel
- **Grenoble train station**: TGV from Paris (3hrs), then bus/shuttle to Bourg d'Oisans (~1hr). Regular bus service links Bourg d'Oisans and Grenoble

### Hotel/Lodging Strategy
- **Bourg d'Oisans**: Start village. Hotels, gites, campsites, Airbnbs. Base elevation 720m. THE place to stay for race morning convenience. Limited supply -- book 6-12 months in advance. Budget: EUR 80-150/night hotels, EUR 50-100 Airbnb. Camping options: Camping La Cascade (50m from the Alpe d'Huez start), A la Rencontre du Soleil campsite (4-star with mobile homes and pitches), Camping Colporteur
- **Alpe d'Huez**: Finish village. Ski resort with plentiful accommodation at premium pricing (EUR 120-250/night). Riders staying here must descend 13.8km to the start in Bourg d'Oisans early on race morning -- cold descent, bring layers. Advantage: cross the finish line and walk to your hotel
- **Vaujany / Oz-en-Oisans / Villard-Reculas**: Nearby satellite villages with additional accommodation options, typically cheaper than Bourg d'Oisans or Alpe d'Huez
- **Tour operator packages**: Multiple operators (Sportive Breaks, Sports Tours International, Cycle Classic Tours, Campo Bicicleta, SportActive) offer packages from EUR 299 (entry only) to EUR 800-2,500+ (3-5 night packages with hotel, transfers, guided rides, guaranteed entry). Hotels commonly used include Hotel Alp Azur and Hotel Camigane in Alpe d'Huez

### Race-Day Transport
- Start is in Bourg d'Oisans, finish is Alpe d'Huez (13.8km and 1,143m higher)
- After finishing, riders descend the 21 bends back to Bourg d'Oisans to collect cars/return to accommodation
- No official event shuttle from finish to start (riders ride down)
- Broom wagon: coach with bike trailer takes DNF riders to finish village at Alpe d'Huez
- Baggage transport available through tour operators
- Parking in Bourg d'Oisans (limited -- arrive very early)

### Bib Pickup / Registration
- Race village/expo opens the day before the event (typically Saturday) for bib and timing chip collection
- Bib pickup mandatory in person with ID and medical certificate/racing licence
- Timing chip attached to handlebars
- Start area opens early morning; arrive 45-60 minutes before your wave start

## History

### Founding & Origin Story
- **1982**: First edition held. Created by a local organisation committee in the Oisans region of the French Alps. Conceived as a challenging amateur ride replicating legendary Tour de France ascents. One of the oldest cyclosportive events in the world
- Named "La Marmotte" after the alpine marmot (Marmota marmota), a large ground squirrel that inhabits the slopes around Alpe d'Huez and the surrounding high alpine valleys. The marmot's characteristic whistle is a familiar sound to cyclists climbing these passes

### Route Evolution
- **Original route (1982)**: Bourg d'Oisans -> Col de la Croix de Fer -> Col du Telegraphe -> Col du Galibier -> Col du Lautaret -> Alpe d'Huez
- **~2006**: Roadworks on the Croix de Fer forced a switch to the nearby Col du Glandon. The Glandon branch diverges a few kilometers before the Croix de Fer summit, adding a short valley loop to maintain approximate distance and elevation. Route stabilized at ~174km / 5,000m
- **2010**: Glandon descent neutralized (timing frozen) due to safety concerns. The descent is technical, narrow, and crashes were frequent
- **2021**: Route returned to the Col de la Croix de Fer (replacing Glandon)
- **2023**: Two weeks before the event, roadworks on the Glandon forced another change. Route included both Glandon and Croix de Fer plus the Col du Mollard. Distance increased to 186km / 5,530m -- one of the hardest editions ever

### Growth Milestones
- **1982-2002**: For the first 20 years, it was still possible to register on the morning of the race itself. Participation grew steadily from a few hundred to several thousand, driven by the iconic reputation of the climbs and the prestigious Alpe d'Huez finish
- **Early 2000s**: Popularity exploded dramatically. The 7,500 places began selling out rapidly
- **2012**: The event entry website received more than 6,000 individual online applications in a single day. Places now sell out within hours of opening
- **Present**: 7,500 participants annually. 88% international. One of the most popular cyclosportive events in Europe. The Marmotte brand has expanded to include Marmotte Granfondo Pyrenees, Marmotte Granfondo Valais (Switzerland), and Marmotte Granfondo Ecosse (Scotland)

### Notable Moments
- The event's reputation as "the hardest sportive in Europe" has made it a bucket-list challenge and rite of passage for amateur cyclists worldwide
- The Marmotte regularly features in "Top 10 Sportives in Europe/the World" lists from cycling media (CyclingWeekly, Sportive.com, GranFondoGuide)
- Dutch cyclists have a particular affinity with the event due to the Alpe d'Huez finish -- 8 Dutch riders have won Tour de France stages at Alpe d'Huez, making it "the Dutch mountain." The Netherlands is consistently one of the top participating nations

## Climate (Late June -- French Alps)

### Typical Race-Day Weather (Late June)
- **Valley floor (Bourg d'Oisans, 720m)**: Highs 22-28C (72-82F). Early morning start (06:30) can be 10-15C (50-59F). Generally warm and pleasant by mid-morning
- **Mid-altitude (Telegraphe/Valloire, 1,400-1,570m)**: 15-22C (59-72F)
- **Galibier summit (2,642m)**: Can be 2-10C (36-50F) even in late June. Severe wind chill. Snow patches possible near summit. Cold, windy, and potentially wet even on "good" days. A wind jacket is compulsory for the descent
- **Alpe d'Huez (1,860m)**: 15-22C (59-72F). Moderate temperatures by finish time (afternoon/evening)

### Bourg d'Oisans June Climate Averages
- Average high: 18-20C (64-68F)
- Average low: 7-9C (45-48F)
- Rainfall days: ~17 per month in June
- Average monthly precipitation: ~89mm (3.5in)
- Relative humidity: ~87% (high, Alpine)
- Chance of rain on any given day: ~48%
- Clear/sunny hours: ~14 per day (59% of daylight)

### Precipitation Risk
- Late June sits at the boundary of spring and summer weather patterns in the Alps
- Afternoon thunderstorms are a significant risk (convective storms build rapidly over mountain ridges from midday)
- Morning starts are typically clearer, with cloud buildup through the day
- Mountain summit conditions can be dramatically different from valley conditions on the same day
- Historical precedent: multiple editions have featured rain, cold, and even snow on the Galibier

### Specific Weather Challenges
- **Temperature swing**: Riders can experience a 20-25C+ temperature differential in a single day -- warm valleys to near-freezing summit conditions. This is one of the most extreme temperature ranges in any single-day cycling event
- **Galibier exposure**: The final 5km of the Galibier and the summit area are fully exposed to wind and weather. The road is above treeline, with no shelter. Even in summer, the Galibier can experience freak snowstorms
- **Descent wind chill**: The 48km descent from Galibier to Bourg d'Oisans at speed compounds the cold. Riders who are wet from sweat or rain at the summit can become dangerously cold during the descent
- **UV exposure**: Extended climbing at high altitude with intense Alpine UV. Sunscreen essential, especially above 2,000m
- **Dehydration**: Despite the cold at altitude, dehydration is a major risk during the long valley sections and sustained climbs in warm conditions

## Community & Culture

### Atmosphere
- Often described as "the hardest sportive in Europe" and "the ultimate amateur cycling challenge"
- The combination of four legendary Tour de France climbs in a single day creates an unmatched sense of achievement
- Carnival-like atmosphere at the start in Bourg d'Oisans with early-morning excitement and nervous energy
- Feed stations have a festive, supportive atmosphere -- volunteers cheering, music, camaraderie among riders
- The finish at Alpe d'Huez is emotional. Crossing the line after 7-13 hours of riding generates a profound sense of accomplishment. Mutual respect among all finishers regardless of time
- "There is something addictive about that part of the French Alps -- the scenery, the scale of the climbs, and the atmosphere of the event itself -- that gets a lot of participants coming back year after year" (Alpine-Cols)

### International Participation
- 88% of participants come from outside France (~6,600 of 7,500)
- Strong contingents from: Netherlands, United Kingdom, Belgium, Germany, Australia, USA, Scandinavia, Spain, Italy
- Dutch cyclists have a special affinity with the event due to the Alpe d'Huez finish. The Netherlands is consistently one of the largest national groups
- The event has become a rite of passage for Dutch cyclists specifically
- 60+ nationalities typically represented

### Tour de France Connection
- All four climbs on the Marmotte route are legendary Tour de France mountains:
  - Col du Galibier: first used in the Tour in 1911, featured 63+ times. One of the five highest points ever reached by the Tour
  - Alpe d'Huez: 33 Tour stage finishes since 1952. The most iconic summit finish in professional cycling
  - Col du Glandon/Croix de Fer: featured regularly since 1947. Both HC-rated in the Tour
  - Col du Telegraphe: regular Tour inclusion, gateway to the Galibier
- The route is described as "comparable to the most challenging high mountain stages of the Tour de France"
- Riding the Marmotte is the closest an amateur cyclist can come to experiencing a full Tour de France Alpine stage

### Unique Traditions & Features
- The neutralized Glandon descent (since 2010) -- timing frozen to prevent dangerous racing on the technical descent
- The Gold/Silver/Bronze medal system based on age-category times
- The 21 hairpin bends of Alpe d'Huez, each named after a Tour de France stage winner -- riders count down from 21 to 1
- The event's nickname "La Doyenne" (the old lady) -- one of the oldest cyclosportives in existence
- Post-ride descent: unlike most summit-finish sportives, riders must descend Alpe d'Huez's 21 bends after finishing to return to the valley
- The marmot mascot -- the alpine marmots can actually be heard whistling on the slopes during the event

### Accompanying Events
- **Rando des Marmottes**: 2-day version of the same route for those who want the experience without the single-day brutality
- **Trophee d'Oisans**: Separate event run in conjunction with Marmotte weekend
- **Expo/race village**: Opens 1-2 days before the event with equipment exhibitors, nutrition brands, cycling apparel
