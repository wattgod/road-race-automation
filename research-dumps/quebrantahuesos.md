# Research Dump: Quebrantahuesos (Marcha Cicloturista Internacional)

## Quick Facts
- **Full name**: Marcha Cicloturista Internacional Ibercaja Quebrantahuesos by TotalEnergies
- **Discipline**: gran_fondo (marcha cicloturista)
- **Distance**: ~200km (198-205km depending on year; ~124mi). 3,500m / 11,483ft total elevation gain. 4 major climbs (Somport, Marie Blanque, Portalet, Hoz de Jaca).
- **Route options**: Quebrantahuesos Gran Fondo (~200km, 3,500m) and Treparriscos Medio Fondo (85km, 1,500m climbing, 2 passes: Alto de Petralba and Cotefablo)
- **Start/Finish**: Sabinanigo (Sabiñánigo), Huesca province, Aragon, Spain. Elevation ~780m.
- **Date**: Third Saturday of June. 2025: June 21. 2026: June 20. 2024 was the XXXIII (33rd) edition. First edition: 1991.
- **Field size**: ~8,000-10,000 riders for the Gran Fondo; ~2,000+ for the Treparriscos. Combined ~11,000-12,000. Demand far exceeds supply — 14,000+ lottery applications in peak years.
- **Entry fee**: EUR 90-108 (registration after lottery selection). Lottery entry fee: EUR 4 (non-refundable). Direct-place packages through tour operators are significantly more expensive.
- **Start format**: Mass start at 07:15. Bib numbers assigned by best time in last 3 editions (faster riders start nearer the front). Maximum arrival time at finish: 19:30 (~12h15min cutoff).
- **Entry method**: Lottery (sorteo). Pre-registration opens early December, lottery draw in January. Riders with 15+ editions of participation receive automatic entry. Direct Place packages available through affiliated tour operators (no lottery required, higher cost).
- **Roads**: Entirely closed to traffic. 100km in Spain, 100km in France.
- **UCI status**: UCI Gran Fondo World Series qualifier event. Top 25% of each age group qualifies for UCI Gran Fondo World Championships.
- **Organizer**: Peña Ciclista Edelweiss (cycling club) in collaboration with Octagon Esedos.
- **Title sponsors**: Ibercaja (Spanish bank), TotalEnergies.
- **Name meaning**: "Quebrantahuesos" = "Bone Breaker" in Spanish. It is also the Spanish name for the Bearded Vulture (Gypaetus barbatus / lammergeier), an endangered raptor native to the Pyrenees. The Treparriscos is the Spanish name for the Wallcreeper (Tichodroma muraria), a small Pyrenean bird.

## Course Profile

### Route Overview
The route forms a large loop through the Aragonese and French Pyrenees, crossing the international border twice. Starting in Sabinanigo (~780m), riders head west to Jaca, then north into the Pyrenees via the Aragon Valley, crossing into France at the Col du Somport, traversing east through the French Pyrenean valleys via Oloron-Sainte-Marie, climbing the Col de Marie Blanque, descending to Laruns, then heading south back into Spain via the Col du Portalet, descending through the Tena Valley, tackling the steep Hoz de Jaca wall, and finishing in Sabinanigo.

**Key towns on route**: Sabinanigo → Jaca → Castiello de Jaca → Villanua → Canfranc → (border) → Urdos → Etsaut → Cette-Eygun → Accous → Sarrance → Escot → Bedous → Oloron-Sainte-Marie → Bielle → Billheres → Gere-Belesten → Laruns → (border) → Sallent de Gallego → Panticosa → Hoz de Jaca → Biescas → Sabinanigo.

### Climb 1: Puerto de Somport / Col du Somport (Category: HC)
- **Distance**: ~27km from Spanish side (from Canfranc area); gradual approach begins well before the steeper sections
- **Summit altitude**: 1,632-1,640m
- **Elevation gain**: ~690-700m from the valley floor
- **Average gradient**: 3-3.5% overall (progressive gradient; final 10km average ~6%)
- **Maximum gradient**: ~7-8%
- **Character**: Very long but progressive. The first major climb serves as a warm-up — the gradients build gradually over 27km with no savage ramps. The road follows the Rio Aragon valley through Canfranc and Canfranc Estacion (site of the grand abandoned international railway station). The final 10km steepen to a steady 5-6%. Well-surfaced, wide road (N-330 / E-7 trans-Pyrenean route).
- **Historical significance**: One of the oldest Pyrenean passes, used since Roman times. The Somport tunnel (opened 2003) runs beneath it. Featured in the Tour de France and Vuelta a Espana.
- **Descent (French side)**: Fast, well-surfaced descent into the Aspe Valley toward Urdos, Etsaut, and Bedous. Approximately 25-30km of predominantly downhill through the stunning French Pyrenean valley.

### Climb 2: Col de Marie Blanque (Category: HC / Cat. 1)
- **Distance**: 9.1km (from Escot)
- **Summit altitude**: 1,035m
- **Elevation gain**: ~705m
- **Average gradient**: 7.7-7.8%
- **Maximum gradient**: 13%+ (sections in the final 4km)
- **Character**: The selection climb. Deceptively two-faced — the first half features gentle 2-5% gradients through pleasant forested roads, lulling riders into false security. At the halfway mark, the climb transforms: the final 4km average 10-11% with multiple ramps exceeding 13%. This is where the field splinters. Narrow, twisting road through dense forest. The surface is good quality French departmental road.
- **Tour de France history**: Featured multiple times in the Tour (1978, 2000, 2006, 2010, 2016, 2020). Category 1 in professional racing.
- **Descent**: Technical descent with hairpins toward Bielle and then into the Ossau Valley toward Laruns. An aid station is located mid-descent.

### Climb 3: Col du Portalet / Col du Pourtalet (Category: HC)
- **Distance**: 27.9-28.3km (from Laruns on the French side)
- **Summit altitude**: 1,791-1,795m
- **Elevation gain**: ~1,275-1,285m
- **Average gradient**: 4.6% (from Laruns)
- **Maximum gradient**: 8-9%
- **Character**: The longest and most emblematic climb of the Quebrantahuesos. A grinding 28km ascent that saps whatever energy remains after Marie Blanque. The gradient is never extreme but offers no respite — constant 4-5% with occasional steeper sections reaching 8-9%. The climb follows the Gave d'Ossau valley through spectacular Pyrenean scenery — waterfalls, glacial cirques, and towering peaks. After the town of Gabas, the road becomes more exposed with dramatic views of Pic du Midi d'Ossau (2,884m). The final kilometers are above treeline in high mountain terrain.
- **Tour de France history**: Featured regularly since 1978. Used in the 2023 Tour. The pass marks the Franco-Spanish border.
- **Descent (Spanish side)**: Fast descent toward Formigal ski station and Sallent de Gallego in the Tena Valley. Well-surfaced road. An aid station is located in the middle of the descent before the final climb.

### Climb 4: Hoz de Jaca (The Wall)
- **Distance**: 2-2.5km
- **Summit altitude**: ~1,100m
- **Elevation gain**: ~200-225m
- **Average gradient**: 9-10%
- **Maximum gradient**: 12-13%
- **Character**: The final sting in the tail. After 180+ kilometers, this short but savage wall arrives when riders are at their most depleted. The ramps are relentless — 9-10% average with sections bordering 12-13%. The last kilometer is on concrete road surface. This is the climb that earns the event its "bone breaker" name.
- **Descent**: Technical descent toward Biescas with several dangerous hairpin bends and some gravel patches. Requires extreme vigilance despite fatigue and the temptation to rush to the finish.

### Final Section: Biescas to Sabinanigo
- ~15km of predominantly flat to gently rolling terrain along the Gallego Valley. Fast group riding for those with remaining energy; survival mode for the rest.

### Road Surface Quality
- Overall excellent. The route uses major trans-Pyrenean highways (N-330, D-934, etc.) and well-maintained Spanish and French departmental/regional roads.
- All roads are completely closed to traffic during the event — a major differentiator from many gran fondos.
- The only notable surface exception is the Hoz de Jaca climb (final kilometer on concrete) and some gravel patches on the Hoz de Jaca descent.
- Both the Spanish and French road authorities maintain these routes to high standards given their importance as trans-Pyrenean corridors and Tour de France/Vuelta routes.

## Logistics

### Nearest Airports (with approximate driving times to Sabinanigo)
- **Zaragoza (ZAZ)**: ~2hr drive (~170km). Limited international connections but well-served from Spain.
- **Pau Pyrenees (PUF)**: ~2hr drive (~119km). Small French airport with some Ryanair/domestic connections. Convenient for the French side approach.
- **Huesca-Pirineos (HSK)**: ~1hr drive (~64km). Very small regional airport — minimal scheduled service.
- **Pamplona (PNA)**: ~2hr drive (~120km). Moderate connections.
- **Toulouse-Blagnac (TLS)**: ~3.5-4hr drive. Major international hub. Best for long-haul connections.
- **Barcelona-El Prat (BCN)**: ~4hr drive (~310km). Major international hub. Most international flights.
- **Madrid-Barajas (MAD)**: ~4.5hr drive (~380km). Spain's largest hub.
- **Bilbao (BIO)**: ~4hr drive. Good for UK/European connections.
- **Recommended strategy**: Fly into Zaragoza or Barcelona. Zaragoza is closest major city (~2hr) and has a high-speed rail connection from Madrid and Barcelona. Alternatively, fly into Pau (France) for the shortest drive if connections work.

### Lodging Strategy
- **Sabinanigo**: The start/finish town. Limited hotel capacity (population ~10,000). Hotels range EUR 50-120/night. Book very early — accommodation sells out months in advance.
- **Jaca** (20km west): Larger town (~13,000 pop) with more hotel options. Strong tourism infrastructure as a gateway to the Pyrenees. Better restaurant/bar scene.
- **Biescas** (15km north): Smaller but very close to the route. Popular with riders.
- **Formigal/Panticosa** (30-40km north): Ski resort towns with significant hotel capacity. Off-season rates in June can be favorable.
- **Huesca** (60km south): Provincial capital with full amenities, but a long drive on race morning.
- **Camping**: Some riders camp near Sabinanigo. The event provides parking facilities.
- **Key advice**: Book 6-12 months ahead. The entire Serrablo/Alto Gallego region fills up for the event weekend. Many tour operators offer packages with guaranteed accommodation + direct entry (bypassing the lottery).

### Race-Day Transport
- Parking available in Sabinanigo (organized by the event).
- Bus services from Jaca and surrounding towns are typically arranged for race weekend.
- The event provides a broom wagon (escoba) that sweeps the course — riders who cannot maintain the pace are collected and transported to the finish.
- Mechanical support vehicles (motorbikes and cars with professional mechanics) patrol the entire route.
- Medical services stationed throughout the course.

### Feed Zones / Aid Stations (Avituallamientos)
- Multiple aid stations throughout the 200km course (typically 6-8 stations).
- Provisions include: energy bars, cakes/pastries, bananas, dried fruit, nuts, sandwiches, sports drinks, Coca-Cola, and water.
- Notable stations: mid-descent on Marie Blanque, and mid-descent off Portalet (before the Hoz de Jaca wall).
- Finish area: food, drink, first aid, physiotherapy/massage, showers, and bike storage facilities.

### Race-Week Schedule
- **Thursday/Friday**: Bib collection/registration at the event expo in Sabinanigo. Expo features cycling brands, local tourism, nutrition products.
- **Saturday**: Race day. Start at 07:15. Finish cutoff at 19:30.
- **Post-race**: Awards ceremony, post-race party, massage/recovery facilities.

## History

### Origin Story
The Quebrantahuesos was born from inspiration. In 1988 and 1989, members of the Peña Ciclista Edelweiss — a local cycling club based in Sabinanigo — traveled to the French Alps to ride La Marmotte, one of Europe's most famous cyclosportive events. Inspired by the experience and recognizing that the Pyrenean passes surrounding their own town rivaled anything in the Alps, they resolved to create a Pyrenean equivalent. The first edition took place in 1991 with a modest field of local and regional cyclists.

### Growth Milestones
- **1991**: First edition. Small field of regional riders.
- **Late 1990s**: Participation surged as the event gained national recognition in Spain. The format of crossing between Spain and France over legendary Tour de France/Vuelta passes captured cyclists' imaginations.
- **2000s**: International participation grew significantly. The event became a bucket-list ride for European cyclists. The lottery system was introduced to manage demand as applications exceeded capacity.
- **2010s**: Established as Spain's largest gran fondo and one of Europe's most prestigious. Regularly attracting riders from 30-50 nationalities. UCI Gran Fondo World Series qualifier status added further international cachet.
- **2019**: ~9,000+ participants from 47 nationalities in the pre-COVID peak.
- **2020-2021**: COVID disruptions. The event was cancelled in 2020 and held with restrictions in 2021.
- **2022 (XXXII edition)**: Return to full normality. 10,000+ participants. Alejandro Valverde (retired WorldTour pro, former World Champion, Movistar Team) set a new course record of 5h 14m 32s — beating the previous record by 7 minutes.
- **2024 (XXXIII edition)**: June 22, 2024. UCI-sanctioned. ~10,000 riders.
- **2025 (XXXIV edition)**: June 21, 2025.
- **2026 (XXXV edition)**: June 20, 2026.

### Notable Moments
- **Alejandro Valverde's record (2022)**: The recently retired World Champion and Vuelta a Espana winner rode with the Movistar Team Gravel Squad, finishing in 5h 14m 32s — a staggering time for a 200km mountainous route. His participation underscored the event's prestige in Spanish cycling culture.
- **Pro rider participation**: Numerous current and retired professional cyclists participate regularly, including cyclists, ex-WorldTour riders, motorcycle and car racing drivers, and Spanish sports celebrities. The event has a "Hall of Fame" section on its official website.
- **International expansion**: Growth from a local Aragonese cycling club event to one of Europe's top 5 most prestigious gran fondos in just three decades.

## Climate

### Typical Race-Day Weather (Third Saturday of June)
- **Temperature at start (Sabinanigo, 780m, 07:15)**: 10-15C (50-59F)
- **Valley temperatures during the day**: 20-30C (68-86F) possible, especially on lower sections
- **Summit temperatures (Portalet, 1,795m)**: 8-15C (46-59F) typical, can drop lower in bad weather
- **Marie Blanque summit (1,035m)**: 15-22C (59-72F) typical
- **Temperature swing**: Riders experience dramatic temperature changes — from cool starts, to warm French valleys, to cold high-altitude passes, to hot finish areas. A 20C+ range within a single ride is common.

### Precipitation Risk
- June is generally favorable in the Pyrenees — early June to mid-July offers the most stable conditions.
- The Spanish side (Aragon) sits in the rain shadow and tends to be drier and sunnier than the French side.
- Average rainfall in June: 43-107mm depending on elevation, with the majority falling on the French side.
- Afternoon thunderstorms are a risk, particularly at higher elevations and on the French side. The event's early morning start (07:15) means most riders clear the high passes before afternoon storm risk peaks.
- Mountain weather is inherently unpredictable — rain, fog, and cold winds are possible on any Pyrenean pass in any month, even in summer.

### Key Climate Considerations for Riders
- **Layering is essential**: Arm warmers, gilet/vest, and rain jacket are strongly recommended. The descent off Somport into France (25-30km downhill from 1,640m) and the Portalet summit (1,795m) can be bitterly cold.
- **Sun protection**: UV intensity is high at altitude. Sunscreen mandatory.
- **Wind**: The Portalet climb is above treeline in its final kilometers and exposed to crosswinds and headwinds.

## Community & Culture

### Atmosphere
- Quebrantahuesos is widely regarded as the premier cycling event in Spain and one of the top 5 gran fondos in Europe (alongside La Marmotte, Maratona dles Dolomites, L'Etape du Tour, and Nove Colli).
- The atmosphere is described as a "fiesta del ciclismo" — a full celebration of cycling culture. The start in Sabinanigo is electric, with thousands of riders filling the streets.
- Towns along the route provide enthusiastic spectator support, particularly in the Spanish sections (Jaca, Canfranc) and French villages.
- The event generates massive economic impact for the Serrablo and Alto Gallego regions of Aragon.

### International Participation
- Riders from 30-50 nationalities participate each year. The event draws particularly strong contingents from France (geographic proximity and shared Pyrenean culture), UK, Germany, Netherlands, Belgium, Italy, and increasingly from Latin America.
- The bilingual/binational nature of the route (Spain + France) adds a unique international dimension rare among gran fondos.

### Media Coverage
- Live television coverage on Television Espanola (TVE) and streaming.
- 5 camera-equipped motorbikes + 2 drones provide comprehensive race-day coverage.
- 5+ hours of multimedia content across YouTube, Instagram Live, and Twitter.
- 60,000+ social media followers across official channels.
- Coverage in Spanish, French, and English-language cycling media (Cycling Weekly, GranFondo Guide, CycloWorld, etc.).

### Pyrenean Cycling Culture
- The Quebrantahuesos passes through some of the most storied cycling terrain in the world. The Somport, Marie Blanque, and Portalet have all featured in the Tour de France and Vuelta a Espana.
- The event is deeply embedded in Aragonese identity — it was founded by a local cycling club and remains community-rooted despite its international scale.
- The bearded vulture (quebrantahuesos) conservation connection adds an environmental/ecological dimension. The Foundation for the Conservation of the Bearded Vulture (FCQ) is closely associated with the event and the Pyrenean ecosystem.
- Riders regularly spot vultures, griffon vultures, and other Pyrenean wildlife along the route.

### Hall of Fame
- The event maintains an official Hall of Fame recognizing notable participants and contributors. Riders with 15+ editions receive permanent recognition and automatic lottery bypass.

## Scoring Dimension Notes

### distance (1-5)
200km / 124mi with 3,500m climbing. This is a serious endurance challenge — among the longest single-day gran fondos in Europe. The 12h15min cutoff confirms the severity. Comparable to La Marmotte (174km/5,000m) in overall difficulty, longer in distance but with less total climbing.

### climbing (1-5)
3,500m / 11,483ft across 4 categorized climbs including 3 HC-rated passes. The Portalet alone (28km, 1,275m) is a massive effort. Marie Blanque's final 4km at 10-13% are genuinely brutal. Hoz de Jaca at 9-10% after 180km is sadistic. This is an elite-level climbing challenge.

### descent_technicality (1-5)
Mixed. The Somport descent (French side) is fast and well-surfaced. Marie Blanque descent has technical hairpins. The Portalet descent into Spain is fast but straightforward. The Hoz de Jaca descent has dangerous curves and some gravel patches — the most technical section and arrives when fatigue is maximal.

### road_surface (1-5)
Predominantly excellent. Major trans-Pyrenean highways and well-maintained French/Spanish departmental roads. The main exception is the Hoz de Jaca (concrete on the climb, some gravel on descent). Fully closed roads throughout — a significant quality factor.

### climate_risk (1-5)
Mountain weather is always a factor. June is generally favorable but Pyrenean passes can produce rain, cold, fog, and wind at any time. Summit temperatures at Portalet (1,795m) can be 8-15C even in June. The dramatic temperature swings (valley heat to summit cold) require proper layering. Afternoon thunderstorm risk exists, particularly on the French side.

### altitude (1-5)
Highest point: Col du Portalet at 1,795m. Col du Somport at 1,640m. Neither reaches extreme altitude, but the sustained time above 1,000m across 200km is significant. No oxygen concerns at these elevations, but cold exposure risk is real.

### logistics (1-5)
Moderate difficulty. Sabinanigo is a small town in a rural mountainous region. Nearest major airports are 2-4 hours away (Zaragoza 2hr, Barcelona 4hr). Accommodation is limited and books up many months in advance. The lottery entry system adds uncertainty. However, event-day logistics are excellent — closed roads, extensive support, and well-organized aid stations.

### prestige (1-5)
Very high. Spain's biggest and most prestigious gran fondo. UCI Gran Fondo World Series qualifier. Founded 1991 with 35 editions by 2025. Consistently ranked among Europe's top 5 gran fondos. Attracts riders from 30-50 nationalities. Pro cyclist participation (Valverde record). The route crosses legendary Tour de France/Vuelta passes. Media coverage on national Spanish television.

### organization (1-5)
Exceptional. 35 years of experience. Fully closed roads (200km — enormous logistical achievement across two countries). Multiple aid stations with varied nutrition. Mechanical support patrols. Medical services throughout. Broom wagon. Post-race physiotherapy, massage, showers, bike storage. Live TV coverage with 5 camera bikes and 2 drones. The lottery system, while frustrating for applicants, effectively manages field size for safety and environmental protection.

### scenic_experience (1-5)
World-class. The route traverses some of Europe's most spectacular mountain scenery. Highlights include: the Aragon Valley approach to Somport, the French Aspe Valley, the Ossau Valley with views of Pic du Midi d'Ossau (2,884m), the Portalet's high-altitude lunar landscape, and the lush Tena Valley. The route crosses between Spain and France — different architecture, language, and landscape character on each side. Pyrenean wildlife (bearded vultures, griffon vultures) visible along the route.

### community_culture (1-5)
Deeply embedded in regional identity. Founded by a local cycling club (Pena Ciclista Edelweiss) and still community-rooted. The event is a major economic driver for the Serrablo/Alto Gallego region. Festival atmosphere in start/finish towns. Connection to bearded vulture conservation (FCQ). Riders from 50 nationalities create an international atmosphere. The binational route (Spain/France) adds unique cultural texture. Spanish cycling media treat it as a marquee event.

### field_depth (1-5)
Very strong. 8,000-10,000 riders in the Gran Fondo alone. UCI qualifier status attracts competitive riders seeking World Championship qualification. Demand exceeds capacity (14,000+ lottery applications). Pro and ex-pro participation (Valverde's 5h14m record). The field spans from elite amateurs to recreational riders given the generous 12+ hour cutoff.

### value (1-5)
Good. EUR 90-108 entry fee for 200km on fully closed roads with extensive support, feed zones, post-race facilities, mechanical service, medical coverage, and TV coverage. Comparable to or better value than La Marmotte, Maratona dles Dolomites, or L'Etape du Tour. The lottery-bypass "direct place" packages through tour operators are expensive (EUR 300-600+) but include accommodation guarantees.

### expenses (1-5)
Moderate-to-high total trip cost for international riders. Entry fee is reasonable (EUR 90-108), but accommodation in the small Pyrenean towns can be expensive during event week (EUR 50-120/night). Flights to Zaragoza or Barcelona are generally affordable from European cities. Car rental likely necessary given limited public transport. Food and drink in rural Aragon is affordable by European standards. Tour operator packages range EUR 300-1,000+ depending on inclusions.
