# Research Dump: Haute Route Alps

## Quick Facts
- 7-day multi-stage amateur cycling race through the French and Swiss Alps
- ~808km / ~502mi total distance, ~19,255m / ~63,173ft total climbing (2026 route)
- 6 full road stages + 1 uphill individual time trial
- 2026: Nice (start) to Thonon-les-Bains near Geneva (finish), August 23-29
- Route changes annually: previous editions have run Nice-Megeve, Megeve-Nice, Geneva-Nice, etc.
- 2025 edition: Megeve to Nice, August 24-30 (813km / 20,000m+)
- 2022 edition: Nice to Megeve, 792km / 21,300m (368 starters, 302 finishers)
- Field size: ~500-600 riders for the 7-day event (2024 capped at 550 cyclists)
- Founded 2011 (inaugural edition: Geneva to Nice). 2026 = 15th anniversary edition
- Entry fee: EUR 2,099 (7-day, 2026 via Sportive Breaks listing). 4 price phases — entry increases as event approaches. Earlier pricing reported at EUR 1,899 (2022 registration). 3-day option also available at lower price
- Accommodation packages additional: Standard Double EUR 2,199, Comfort Double EUR 2,699, Premium Double EUR 3,299 (7-day, includes breakfast + hotel-to-hotel bag transport)
- Total cost with accommodation: EUR 4,298-5,398 for 7-day event (entry + lodging). Tour operator all-inclusive packages range USD 5,799-6,600+
- Start format: mass start each morning with timed sections (primarily climbs and flats); descents are neutralized for safety
- 3-day option also offered (stages 5-7 typically), making event more accessible
- 40+ nationalities represented. ~20% US, ~20% UK, ~10% Brazil, ~10% Canada/France
- 56% returning alumni (2024 data)
- 12% women participants
- Medical certificate or health module required
- Officially billed as "the most premium multi-day stage event for amateur cyclists in the world"

## What's Included in Entry Fee
- Haute Route branded backpack (leave at start, collect at finish each day)
- Travel bag transport (race village to race village; hotel-to-hotel if accommodation booked through HR)
- Race bag service
- Detailed roadbook with stage profiles and GPX files
- Marshals along the route, motorcycle escorts, security vehicles
- Broom wagon ("End of Race Car")
- "Red Lantern" — a personality rider offering encouragement to struggling participants
- Mavic mechanical support (race village + on-course)
- Medical assistance
- Live timing system with daily rankings
- 3-5 feed stations per stage (water, drinks, snacks, fruit)
- Secure bike park at each race village
- Bike wash area
- Post-stage hot meal
- Daily ranking and awards ceremony
- Daily safety rider briefing (mandatory, evening before each stage)
- Drinks party / social events
- 15-minute recovery massage after each stage
- Daily professional photos and video content
- Finisher medal and apparel at final finish line
- Closing ceremony and awards

## What's NOT Included
- Accommodation (separate package or book independently)
- Breakfast (included only if accommodation booked through HR)
- Dinners (some tour operators include)
- Airport transfers (available as add-on, ~EUR 59)
- Bike rental (available as add-on)

## 2026 Course Profile (Nice to Thonon-les-Bains)

### Stage 1: Nice to Cuneo (Italy)
- 182km, 4,020m+ elevation gain
- La Colmiane (1,513m), Col de la Lombarde (2,340m)
- The most brutal opening stage in recent memory. 4,000m of climbing on day one. La Colmiane warms gently before La Lombarde tips riders into the unknown. Crosses into Italy. No warm-up, no compromise.

### Stage 2: Cuneo to Col d'Izoard / Briancon
- 141km, 3,610m+ elevation gain
- Col d'Agnel (2,744m) — Italian side: rougher, longer, wilder. 31km from Sampeyre, last 9-10km average 10%
- Col d'Izoard (2,360m) — Tour de France legend. Casse Deserte section: dramatic orange scree landscape in final km
- Col d'Agnel is the highest paved pass in the event at 2,744m

### Stage 3: Briancon to Alpe d'Huez
- 79km, 2,375m+ elevation gain
- Col du Lautaret (2,058m), Col de Sarenne (1,999m)
- Compact but sensational. Lautaret sets the rhythm. Sarenne is raw and difficult. Finishes on the iconic Alpe d'Huez — the Tour de France holy ground

### Stage 4: Alpe d'Huez Time Trial
- 16km, 1,125m+ elevation gain
- Individual uphill time trial up Alpe d'Huez
- 21 legendary hairpin bends. Pure time trial — no peloton, no drafting
- One of the most iconic climbs in professional cycling. Each hairpin named after a Tour de France stage winner

### Stage 5: Alpe d'Huez to Megeve
- 169km, 3,525m+ elevation gain
- Col du Glandon (1,924m) — majestic lake setting at summit
- Signal de Bisanne (1,730m) — confidential and formidable, rarely used in Haute Route history
- Col des Saisies (1,650m)
- The marathon stage. Longest day of the week

### Stage 6: Megeve to Megeve Altiport
- 100km, 2,560m+ elevation gain
- Col des Aravis — 12km climb, final 7km at 6-7%
- Col de la Colombiere — averages 5.8%, ramps up in final 3km
- Circuit stage returning to Megeve area

### Stage 7: Megeve to Thonon-les-Bains
- 119km, 2,040m+ elevation gain
- Col de Joux-Plane — 6km at 6% average, consistent punishing climb
- Final stage descends to Lake Geneva and the finish in Thonon-les-Bains
- New finish location for 2026 (first time HR has finished in Thonon)

### 2026 Totals
- 808km / 502mi, ~19,255m / ~63,173ft climbing
- 7 stages over 7 consecutive days
- Highest point: Col d'Agnel at 2,744m (9,003ft)

## Iconic Climbs Featured Across Editions
The route changes every year, but regularly features these legendary ascents:
- **Col du Galibier** (2,642m / 8,668ft) — HC. One of the highest paved passes in Europe. Tour de France icon since 1911
- **Alpe d'Huez** (1,860m / 6,102ft) — 21 hairpins, 13.8km, 7.9% avg. The most famous finish in cycling
- **Col d'Izoard** (2,360m / 7,743ft) — Casse Deserte moonscape. Coppi and Bobet legend
- **Col de la Madeleine** (2,000m / 6,562ft) — 19.3km, 7.9% avg from Aigueblanche
- **Col de la Croix de Fer** (2,067m / 6,781ft) — "Iron Cross." 29km from Saint-Jean-de-Maurienne
- **Col du Telegraphe** (1,566m / 5,138ft) — Gateway to Galibier. 11.9km, 7.1% avg
- **Col de l'Iseran** (2,770m / 9,088ft) — Highest paved pass in the Alps (when featured)
- **Cormet de Roselend** (1,968m / 6,457ft) — HC. 20km, 7% avg. Dramatic dam and lake
- **Col d'Agnel** (2,744m / 9,003ft) — Franco-Italian border. Remote, wild
- **Col de Vars** (2,108m / 6,916ft) — Gateway to the southern Alps
- **Col de la Bonette** (2,715m / 8,907ft) — Among the highest road passes in Europe
- **Col de la Lombarde** (2,340m / 7,677ft) — Franco-Italian frontier pass
- **Col de Sarenne** (1,999m / 6,558ft) — The "back door" to Alpe d'Huez

## Road Surface & Traffic
- All roads are fully paved tarmac. These are major French departmental and national roads, generally well-maintained for the Tour de France
- Roads are NOT fully closed to traffic. Marshals, motorcycle escorts, and security vehicles manage traffic. Lead and follow vehicles provide protection
- Some sections may have rolling road closures on climbs
- 25+ motorbikes and multiple support cars on course
- Descents are neutralized (not timed) — riders descend at their own pace for safety

## Logistics
- **Start city (2026):** Nice, Cote d'Azur, France
- **Finish city (2026):** Thonon-les-Bains, France (near Geneva, Switzerland)
- **Airports:** Nice Cote d'Azur (NCE) for start. Geneva (GVA) for finish/departure. Both are major international airports with excellent connectivity
- **Airport transfers:** Available as add-on. Nice airport pickup at 12:00 PM on registration day. Group transfer from Thonon-les-Bains to Geneva airport after final stage
- **Registration day:** Day before Stage 1 (Saturday Aug 22, 2026). Bike box collection, number pickup, safety briefing
- **Accommodation:** NOT included in entry fee. Three options: (1) Book through Haute Route at 3 tiers (Standard/Comfort/Premium), (2) Book through official tour operators (Sportive Breaks, Alpcycles, La Fuga, Two Wheel Tours, etc.), (3) Book independently
- **Bike transport between stages:** Riders ride their bikes stage-to-stage. No bike transport needed — it's a point-to-point ride. Bike boxes collected on registration day, transported by truck, returned after Stage 7
- **Luggage logistics:** Travel bag transported stage-to-stage by HR logistics team (hotel-to-hotel if accommodation booked through HR). Backpack: leave at start line, collect at finish line
- **Daily schedule:** Mandatory evening safety briefing → early morning start (typically 6:30-7:00 AM) → ride stage → finish → massage/recovery → bike park → hot meal → briefing
- **Cutoff times:** Vary by stage based on distance and climbing. Exceeding cutoff = DNF for that stage. Riders may continue subsequent stages but are removed from General Classification
- **DNF/withdrawal:** Riders who miss a cutoff on one stage can start subsequent stages but are removed from GC. Full withdrawal at any time. Medical team can pull riders

## History
- **2011:** Inaugural edition. Geneva to Nice. Created by Remi Duchemin through his company ThirdPole (founded 2009), which merged with OC Group in late 2010 to form OC ThirdPole
- **2012:** OC ThirdPole evolved into OC Sport (June 2012). Alps event sold out in 8 hours. Participants from 57 countries
- **2013:** Haute Route Pyrenees added (7-day event)
- **2014:** Haute Route Dolomites added (7-day event)
- **2015:** Mavic became main partner and official clothing partner. DNF rate ~17% for Alps, ~12% for Pyrenees
- **2017:** Expanded to US with Mavic Haute Route Rockies. Also franchise events in Oman, Mexico, Brazil, China
- **2020:** New majority shareholder Michael Hartweg (independent investor/entrepreneur) acquired the brand
- **2021 (August):** IRONMAN Group acquired the entire Haute Route Series
- **2023 (late):** France Velo Evenements (FVE) — a joint venture of the French Cycling Federation and Hopscotch Sport agency — acquired the Haute Route brand from IRONMAN
- **2024:** First edition under FVE ownership. Route: Megeve to Nice. 714km, 19,405m climbing. Capped at 550 riders
- **2025:** Megeve to Nice. 813km, 20,000m+
- **2026:** Nice to Thonon-les-Bains. 808km, ~19,255m. 15th anniversary edition. First-ever finish in Thonon-les-Bains
- **Notable:** The route direction and cities change annually, giving repeat participants a fresh experience
- **Completion rates:** ~82-83% based on 2022 data (302 finishers from 368 starters). DNF rate historically ~15-20% across all 7 stages

## Climate (Late August, French Alps)
- **Valley temperatures:** 20-30C (68-86F). Nice coastal start can be 28-35C
- **Summit temperatures (2,000-2,700m):** 5-15C (41-59F). Temperature drops ~6.5C per 1,000m of altitude gained
- **Dawn starts:** Potentially single digits in mountain valleys (4-8C)
- **Rain risk:** MODERATE-HIGH over 7 days. August is one of the driest months but afternoon thunderstorms are common in the Alps, typically breaking after 3 PM. Over 7 consecutive days, encountering at least 1-2 rainy stages is likely
- **Storm risk:** Violent but short-lived afternoon thunderstorms. Lightning risk on exposed passes
- **Snow risk:** LOW but not zero on passes above 2,500m (Col d'Agnel at 2,744m, Col de l'Iseran at 2,770m when featured). Early-season snow can linger. Freak August snowfall is rare but documented
- **Wind:** Can be significant on exposed cols, especially Galibier and Agnel. Crosswinds in valleys
- **Sun exposure:** Extreme at altitude. UV intensity increases ~10% per 1,000m. Multiple hours of exposure daily
- **Mandatory gear:** Rain jacket required (varies by edition). Arm warmers, leg warmers, gilet strongly recommended for descents from 2,500m+ passes

## Community & Culture
- **The multi-stage bond:** Spending 7 consecutive days riding 100-180km per day creates deep camaraderie among participants. Described as "the Haute Route family." Shared suffering on legendary climbs bonds strangers into friends
- **Rider profile:** Serious amateur cyclists. Most participants train 10-15 hours/week for months in advance. Official 12-week training plans available. FTP of 3.0+ W/kg recommended as minimum. Many participants are experienced gran fondo riders or triathletes. Not a beginner event
- **Post-stage experience:** Massage tent, bike wash, hot meal, secure bike park. Evening safety briefing doubles as social event with daily awards ceremony, drinks party. Daily video and photo content shared. Pasta party and closing ceremony after final stage
- **International participation:** 40+ nationalities. ~20% American, ~20% British, ~10% Brazilian, ~10% Canadian/French. Also strong representation from Australia, Switzerland, Italy, Spain, Chile, China, UAE
- **Comparison to pro Grand Tours:** Deliberately modeled on the pro peloton experience. Mavic neutral service (same yellow cars as Tour de France). Motorcycle escorts. Timed stages with GC classification. Daily jerseys for classification leaders. Feed zones. Broom wagon. The closest an amateur can get to riding a Grand Tour
- **Media coverage:** Covered by Cycling Weekly, BikeRadar, Cyclist Magazine, Velo/Outside, GranFondo Guide, Rouleur. YouTube documentaries (Jasper Verkuijl: "I Tried The TOUGHEST Race In The WORLD!"). Official daily video content and professional photography
- **Returning riders:** 56% of 2024 participants were alumni — testament to the event's addictive quality
- **The "Haute Router" identity:** Finishers earn the title "Haute Router." Strong alumni community. Event cultivates a premium, aspirational brand

## Haute Route Event Family
Beyond the flagship Alps event, the Haute Route brand has expanded to:
- Haute Route Pyrenees (7-day, added 2013)
- Haute Route Dolomites (7-day, added 2014)
- Haute Route Ventoux (shorter format)
- Haute Route Crans-Montana (shorter format)
- Haute Route Rockies (US, added 2017)
- Various franchise events in Oman, Mexico, Brazil, China
- 3-day formats introduced for Alps, Pyrenees
- Under FVE ownership (2024+), focus has narrowed back to flagship French events
