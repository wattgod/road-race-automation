# Research Dump: El Tour de Tucson

## Quick Facts
- 102 miles / 164km (Century), ~2,800-3,000ft / 853-914m total climbing
- Route options: 102mi Century, 63mi/100km Metric Century (~1,600ft climbing), 32mi/50km Half Metric Century (~500ft climbing), 3mi Family Fun Ride, 1mi Family Fun Ride
- Start/Finish: Tucson Convention Center, 260 South Church Avenue, Downtown Tucson, Arizona
- Third or fourth Saturday in November (42nd edition: November 22, 2025; 43rd edition: November 21, 2026)
- ~9,000-11,000+ riders annually (2025 record: 11,000+ participants, 60% first-timers; 2023: ~9,000; typical recent years: 7,000-10,000)
- Founded 1983 by Richard DeBernardis; first edition had 197 riders raising $4,500 for the American Diabetes Association
- Entry fee: $15-$600 range depending on category and registration timing; VIP Finish Line Club: $250 additional; $100 gift certificates available for future entries; early registration strongly encouraged for lower pricing
- Wave start system: Century at 7:00 AM, Metric Century at 9:00 AM, Half Metric Century at 10:00 AM, Fun Rides at 8:15 AM
- All routes close at 4:30 PM (9.5-hour window for Century riders)
- Named USA TODAY 10Best "Best Road Cycling Event" (2023); recognized as the largest perimeter cycling event in America
- Discipline: century / gran fondo (mass-participation, not a sanctioned race; competitive field at the front)
- 2025 Century men's winner: Stephen Schaefer (Landis Cyclery/Trek), 3:39:42
- 2025 Century women's winner: Marjorie Rinaldo (Fount Cycling Guild), 3:53:02
- Notable past participants: Damiano Cunego (Giro d'Italia champion), Jens Voigt (Tour de France veteran), George Hincapie, Bobby Julich, Bob Roll, Christian Vande Velde, Mari Holden, Rahsaan Bahati (10x USA National Champion)

## Course Profile

### Overall Character
The Century route is a 102-mile perimeter loop around greater Tucson through the Sonoran Desert, starting and finishing at the Tucson Convention Center in downtown Tucson (elevation ~2,390ft / 728m). The course is predominantly flat to gently rolling with a few gradual climbs — riders describe it as "mostly flat with a gradual climb that is nothing more than a few degrees." The total elevation gain of ~2,800-3,000ft is spread across 102 miles, meaning the average gradient is negligible. There are no sustained steep climbs or mountain passes. The eastern portion of the route, heading toward Colossal Cave and through the communities southeast of Tucson, provides the most notable elevation changes as the terrain rises gradually toward the foothills.

### Key Route Segments (Century / 102mi)

1. **Downtown Tucson to Davis-Monthan AFB** — Urban start through downtown streets, heading southeast. Flat terrain through the city grid. Riders pass through or alongside Davis-Monthan Air Force Base, a distinctive military landmark.

2. **Old Spanish Trail** — The route follows the historic Old Spanish Trail southeast of Tucson, entering the Sonoran Desert landscape. Saguaro cacti line the road with views of the Rincon Mountains to the east. This section features gentle rolling terrain as the route climbs gradually away from the Tucson basin.

3. **Colossal Cave / Vail area** — The easternmost point of the route near the historic Colossal Cave Mountain Park. This is the highest elevation point on the route. The terrain rolls through desert foothills with the Santa Catalina and Rincon mountain ranges as backdrop.

4. **Corona de Tucson** — Southeast of Tucson, the route passes through this unincorporated community. Open desert terrain with long sightlines.

5. **Sahuarita / Green Valley** — The southern arc of the route passes through these communities south of Tucson. Relatively flat terrain through suburban and semi-rural desert landscape. The Santa Rita Mountains are visible to the south.

6. **Return to Tucson** — The western/northern return leg brings riders back through Tucson's south side and back to downtown. Primarily flat terrain.

### Elevation Profile
- Start/Finish elevation: ~2,390ft (728m)
- Highest point: ~3,200-3,400ft (975-1,036m) near Colossal Cave area (approximate)
- Total climbing: ~2,800-3,000ft (853-914m) per Ride with GPS and official sources
- Character: Long, gradual ascents and descents; no sustained climbs above 3-4% gradient
- The Metric Century (63mi) covers ~1,600ft of climbing
- The Half Metric Century (32mi) covers ~500ft of climbing

### Road Surface & Conditions
- Entirely paved public roads (asphalt)
- Route uses a mix of city streets, suburban arterials, and county highways
- Road quality is generally good; the organizers work closely with Pima County, City of Tucson, and other municipalities to select the best available roads and avoid construction zones
- Full road closures on portions of the route; rolling closures and police support throughout
- The route traverses public roads shared with some traffic on outer segments, though significant law enforcement and volunteer support manages intersections
- Desert conditions mean roads are dry with no drainage issues (November is extremely low precipitation)
- Chip seal and rough patches possible on some county road segments outside the city core

### Aid Stations
- Aid stations approximately every 7-12 miles along all routes
- Stocked with water, bananas, oranges, and electrolyte drinks
- Some stations also offer raisins, cookies, and/or pretzels
- Port-o-pots at each aid station location
- Staffed by volunteer groups including Girl Scouts, Boy Scouts, nonprofits, businesses, and service organizations
- Aid stations close on a rolling schedule to ensure all riders can finish by the 4:30 PM course closure
- Mechanical support (SAG) vehicles patrol the route

### Cut-off Time
- All routes close at 4:30 PM
- Century riders starting at 7:00 AM have approximately 9.5 hours to complete 102 miles (~10.7 mph average pace required)
- Individual aid stations close according to a pace schedule; slower riders may find stations already closed

## Logistics

### Getting There

**Tucson International Airport (TUS)** — 8-9 miles (15-20 min drive) from the Tucson Convention Center (start/finish). Small but well-connected regional airport with direct flights from major hubs including Los Angeles, Phoenix, Denver, Dallas, Houston, Chicago, Minneapolis, Seattle, San Francisco, and Atlanta. Airlines include American, United, Delta, Southwest, Alaska, and Frontier. Generally affordable and uncrowded compared to Phoenix.

**Phoenix Sky Harbor International Airport (PHX)** — 117 miles / ~2 hours driving from downtown Tucson. Much larger airport with more flight options and typically cheaper fares. A viable alternative for riders willing to make the drive or arrange a shuttle. Major international hub with flights from virtually everywhere in North America and many international destinations.

### Accommodation

**Near Start/Finish (Tucson Convention Center, <1 mile):**
- Leo Kent Hotel, Tucson (Tribute Portfolio) — ~$227/night
- Hampton Inn & Suites — ~$200/night
- Home2 Suites by Hilton — ~$200/night
- Ramada by Wyndham — ~$115/night (El Tour negotiated rate)

**Near Airport (7-8 miles from start):**
- Courtyard by Marriott Tucson Airport — ~$119/night
- Hampton Inn & Suites Tucson Mall — ~$139/night
- Four Points by Sheraton Tucson Airport — ~$99/night

**Strategy**: Book 2-3 months in advance for El Tour weekend. Hotels near the Convention Center fill quickly. Downtown Tucson (4th Avenue, Congress Street area) puts you within walking distance of the start. Airport-area hotels are cheaper but require a 15-20 minute drive to the start. Airbnb/VRBO options available throughout greater Tucson at various price points. Mid-November is not peak tourist season for Tucson (that is January-April), so hotel supply is generally adequate.

### Race-Day Transport
- Tucson Convention Center has parking facilities, but early arrival recommended (gates open before 6:00 AM)
- Many riders use ride-share (Uber/Lyft available in Tucson) or get dropped off
- Bike valet and secure parking typically available at the venue
- Post-race: finish is at the same Convention Center location, so logistics are simple (no point-to-point transport needed)

### Registration / Expo
- **Zenni Optical Expo & Fiesta** held at Tucson Convention Center Exhibition Hall
- Thursday: Noon - 8:00 PM
- Friday: Noon - 8:00 PM
- Saturday (race day): 6:00 AM - 5:00 PM
- All riders must pick up credential packets at the Expo; bring QR code from registration confirmation
- Expo features: merchandise, product demos, giveaways, panels, contests, music, entertainment, local food vendors, food trucks, Dragoon Brewing beer garden
- Expo is free and open to the public
- Post-ride festivities at the same venue: food, drink, live entertainment, awards ceremony, finish line beer garden

### Prologue Camp
- Multi-day training camp offered before El Tour (the "Holualoa Companies El Tour de Tucson Prologue Camp")
- Features rides with professional cyclists and legends (Jens Voigt, etc.)
- Separate registration and fees
- Held in the days leading up to the event

## History

### Origin Story
Richard J. DeBernardis, a Harvard graduate and dedicated touring cyclist, moved to Tucson in the early 1980s after remarkable cycling adventures including being the first person to bicycle the entire perimeter of the continental United States (1979) and riding from Alaska to Mexico (2,218 miles in 40 days). In Tucson, DeBernardis was inspired by the city's cycling-friendly climate and terrain. In 1983, he founded the Perimeter Bicycling Association of America (PBAA) and organized the first El Tour de Tucson — a 100-mile perimeter ride around the city that began and ended at Sabino Canyon Recreation Area. The concept blended two cycling traditions: "perimeter rides" (circumnavigating a city) and the "century challenge" (riding 100 miles, a benchmark personal accomplishment).

### First Edition (1983)
- 197 riders participated (some sources say 185)
- Raised $4,500 for the American Diabetes Association
- Route was approximately 109 miles
- Single distance option only

### Growth Timeline
- **1983**: 197 riders, $4,500 raised
- **1991** (9th edition): 1,854 riders from 32 states plus Canada, Mexico, and Taiwan; $160,000 raised for the Arthritis Foundation. Course record set at 4:22:21 (109 miles). Featured in Sports Illustrated ("Riding on the Rim," March 9, 1992).
- **1990s-2000s**: Steady growth into thousands of riders; multiple distance options added
- **2010s**: Regularly attracting 7,000-9,000+ riders
- **2018**: David Salomon won the Century; Damiano Cunego (2004 Giro d'Italia champion) finished 2nd (3:57:42)
- **2019**: Milestone of $100 million cumulative charity fundraising reached
- **2022** (39th edition): 20-year-old Sean Christian defeated Damiano Cunego by 1.2 seconds for the Century win
- **2023** (40th edition): ~9,000 riders, 43% first-timers; $6 million raised for nonprofits
- **2024** (41st edition): Charities raised nearly $8 million for 85 nonprofits; cumulative total approached $130 million
- **2025** (42nd edition): Record 11,000+ participants, 60% first-timers; Stephen Schaefer won Century in 3:39:42; Jens Voigt and Rahsaan Bahati competed in the Metric Century

### Notable Moments
- **1983**: Inaugural ride with 197 cyclists established what would become the nation's largest cycling event
- **1992**: Sports Illustrated feature article "Riding on the Rim" brought national media attention
- **2018-2022**: Grand Tour champion Damiano Cunego became a regular participant, elevating the event's prestige
- **2019**: Cumulative charitable fundraising crossed the $100 million mark
- **2023**: Named USA TODAY 10Best "Best Road Cycling Event in America," defeating RAGBRAI, Mount Washington Hillclimb, and others
- **2025**: Record participation (11,000+); Jens Voigt, Rahsaan Bahati, and other legends competed; event described as "the largest bike race in the U.S. and the longest-running bike event in Arizona"
- **COVID era**: The event continued operating through the pandemic years (unlike many peers that cancelled for 1-2 years), though with reduced participation

### Organizational Structure
- Produced and directed by Perimeter Bicycling Association of America (PBAA), founded by DeBernardis in 1983
- DeBernardis has been president of PBAA since founding
- PBAA also produces El Tour de Zona (March) and El Tour de Mesa (April)
- The organization's mission centers on "celebrating cycling through world-class events while empowering our community and encouraging charitable giving"

## Climate (November in Tucson)

### Temperature
- Average high: 71F (22C)
- Average low: 47-50F (8-10C)
- Race-day range: typically 49-71F (9-22C)
- Dawn start (7:00 AM) temperature: ~45-55F (7-13C) — arm warmers and a vest recommended
- Midday: 65-75F (18-24C) — comfortable riding conditions
- Late afternoon (4:30 PM cutoff): ~65-70F (18-21C)

### Precipitation
- November average: 0.75 inches (19mm) total rainfall
- Average rainy days: 3.5 per month
- Probability of rain on any given November day: ~12%
- Tucson's monsoon season (July-September) is well past by November
- Dry desert air; average relative humidity: ~37%
- Rain is very unlikely on race day, but when it does rain in the desert, roads can become slippery due to oil accumulation on normally dry pavement

### Sun & UV
- Average sunshine: ~69% of daylight hours (16.5 hours of clear/partly cloudy skies)
- UV index remains moderate-high even in November at this latitude (32N) and altitude (2,400ft)
- Sunscreen, UV-protective clothing, and sunglasses are strongly recommended
- Sunrise ~6:30 AM, sunset ~5:25 PM in late November

### Wind
- Average wind speed: 12.6 mph (20.3 kph)
- Generally light to moderate; not typically a major factor
- The desert can produce gusty conditions, especially on exposed segments outside the city
- Wind direction is variable; no dominant prevailing pattern comparable to Chapman's Peak at Cape Town
- 2025 race was notably windy, described as challenging conditions

### Overall Assessment
November in Tucson offers near-ideal cycling weather: warm (not hot), dry, sunny, and generally calm. The climate is one of the event's strongest selling points and a major reason for its continued growth. The dry desert air means dehydration is a larger concern than precipitation. The morning start can be cool enough to require layers that are shed within the first hour.

## Community & Culture

### Atmosphere
El Tour de Tucson is a festive, inclusive mass-participation cycling celebration rather than a pure competitive race. While the front of the Century field features strong amateur and former professional riders racing for the win, the vast majority of participants are recreational cyclists, charity riders, and first-timers (60% first-timers in 2025). The event is intergenerational and family-friendly, with route options for all abilities from 1-mile fun rides to the full 102-mile Century.

### Charity Focus
Charitable fundraising is the heart and soul of El Tour. Since 1983, Perimeter Bicycling events have raised over $130 million for charitable organizations. In recent years:
- 2024: ~$8 million raised for 85 nonprofit partners
- 2023: ~$6 million raised
- 60+ nonprofit organizations register annually as Official Charity Partners
- Causes span: animals, arts, children, community development, education, environment, health and wellness, human services, diabetes research, cancer research
- Historical beneficiaries include: American Diabetes Association, American Parkinson Disease Association, Leukemia & Lymphoma Society, Juvenile Diabetes Research Foundation, Big Brothers Big Sisters, Girl Scouts, Boy Scouts
- The event is Tucson's second-largest economic driver (behind the Tucson Gem Show)

### International Participation
- Riders from 49 US states and 12+ countries
- Strong draw from across the American West and Southwest
- International participants attracted by Tucson's cycling-friendly climate and infrastructure
- Not heavily international compared to European gran fondos, but increasingly global

### Demographics
- Strong turnout among women and riders over 50
- Wide range from competitive racers to casual participants
- Military/first responder category reflects Tucson's military community (Davis-Monthan AFB)

### Expo & Finish Line Festivities
- Three-day Expo & Fiesta at the Tucson Convention Center is a major social event
- Free and open to the public
- Features cycling vendors, food trucks, live music, Dragoon Brewing beer garden, contests, panels
- Finish line celebrations include awards ceremony, live entertainment, food and drink
- Strong sense of community accomplishment and shared celebration

### Tucson as a Cycling City
- Tucson is widely recognized as one of the best cycling cities in the United States
- Year-round riding weather (though summer heat limits midday riding June-September)
- Extensive bike lane network and cycling infrastructure
- Home to numerous professional and elite amateur cyclists who train in the region
- The Loop (shared-use path system) provides 131+ miles of car-free cycling paths
- Proximity to world-class climbing (Mt. Lemmon: 27 miles, 5,500ft gain; Gates Pass; Tucson Mountains)
- Major destination for winter training camps (professional teams visit annually)

### Media Coverage
- USA TODAY 10Best "Best Road Cycling Event" (2023)
- Sports Illustrated feature (1992)
- Coverage from Gran Fondo Daily News, PezCycling News, GranFondo.com
- Local TV coverage (KGUN9, KOLD) and Tucson Daily Star newspaper
- PeopleForBikes feature on participation and community impact (2025)
- No live broadcast television coverage (unlike Tour Down Under or major European gran fondos)

### Participant Experience
- Finisher medal and results for all participants
- Chip timing for accurate individual results
- Photo services along the route
- Post-ride festivities at the Convention Center
- The experience is widely described as welcoming, well-organized, and community-focused
- Volunteers are a major part of the atmosphere; aid stations staffed by local service organizations create a festive, supportive environment
