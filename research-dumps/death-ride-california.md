# Research Dump: Death Ride — Tour of the California Alps

## Quick Facts
- 103mi / 166km, 14,000+ ft / 4,267m elevation gain, 6 HC climbs across 3 mountain passes (both sides of each)
- Start/Finish: Turtle Rock Park, 2 miles north of Markleeville, Alpine County, California (elev. ~5,500ft / 1,676m)
- Markleeville is the county seat of Alpine County — California's least populous county (population 1,204 per 2020 census). Markleeville itself: population 191.
- Three passes: Monitor Pass (8,314ft / 2,534m), Ebbetts Pass (8,730ft / 2,661m), Pacific Grade Summit (8,021ft / 2,445m)
- Elevation range: ~5,500ft to 8,730ft (1,676m to 2,661m)
- Date: Second Saturday of July. 2026: July 11 (45th edition). 2025: July 12 (44th edition).
- Field size: ~2,000-3,500 participants (historically capped at 3,000; recent years ~2,000 cap). Sells out regularly.
- Founded: 1978 (some sources cite 1980 for the first formal organized edition). Five friends decided to ride all the California Alps passes in a single day.
- Entry fee: Tiered pricing with early-bird discount and price increases (e.g., prices increase on Valentine's Day and again April 1). Exact 2025/2026 dollar amounts not publicly cached — registration processed through BikeReg. Historical estimates place entry at approximately $150-$200.
- Start format: Mass rolling start at 5:00 AM. Not a race — it is an organized, fully-supported challenge ride. No categories, no prizes, no timing chips for competition. Cutoff: 6:30 PM (13.5-hour window).
- Route options: Riders choose how many passes to tackle — 2, 3, 4, 5, or all 6 climbs ("The Whole Shebang"). All-pass finishers earn the "Tombstone Club" pin and the right to purchase the exclusive Finisher's Jersey.
- Course is ~90% closed to vehicle traffic on event day (Caltrans partnership).
- Discipline: Century ride / gran fondo / challenge ride. Not a race.
- Fundraiser: Primary fundraiser of the Alpine County Chamber of Commerce and local nonprofits serving Markleeville, Woodfords, Bear Valley, and Kirkwood.

## Course Profile

### Overview
The course starts and finishes at Turtle Rock Park in Markleeville. Riders complete up to 6 Hors Categorie climbs — both sides of three Sierra Nevada mountain passes: Monitor Pass (Highway 89), Ebbetts Pass (Highway 4), and the Pacific Grade (Highway 4). The original course included Carson Pass instead of Pacific Grade, but was changed due to safety concerns with traffic on Highway 88 and dangerous left turns at Highway 89/88 junction and the Carson Pass summit rest stop.

Average gradient for ascent-only segments (excluding flat and descent): 5.3%.

### The Six Climbs (in order)

**Pass 1 — Monitor Pass West (Climb 1)**
- Distance: 8.2mi / 13.2km
- Elevation gain: 2,729ft / 832m
- Average gradient: 6.1%
- Summit elevation: 8,314ft / 2,534m (Highway 89)
- Character: The opening climb. Wide, open road with sweeping High Sierra views once riders climb out of the canyon at the base. A steady, sustained effort to start the day.

**Pass 2 — Monitor Pass East (Descent then Climb 2)**
- Descent from Monitor summit to Topaz turnaround (full support/SAG at mile 24.6), then climb back up the east side.
- Distance: 9.5mi / 15.3km
- Elevation gain: 3,348ft / 1,021m
- Average gradient: 6.5%
- Gradient breakdown: 70% of the climb at 5-10% average grade, 5% at 10-15%, no sections exceeding 15%.
- Character: The most sustained climbing of the day. The descent to Topaz is a fast, technically moderate joy ride with views over the high desert toward Nevada. The return climb is a long, grinding effort with consistently steep grades.

**Pass 3 — Ebbetts Pass East (Climb 3)**
- Distance: 11.4mi / 18.3km
- Elevation gain: 2,950ft / 899m
- Average gradient: 5% overall
- Two distinct halves: First 5.5mi at 3.1% (gentle, +956ft). Second 5.9mi at 6.4% with frequent double-digit grades (+2,006ft). Maximum gradient: 15.6%.
- Summit elevation: 8,730ft / 2,661m (Highway 4) — the highest point on the course.
- Character: The steepest segments of all five ascents. The approach through the Carson River canyon features stark beauty — meadows, aspens, then scaling the side of a big canyon with grand vistas. The upper half punishes with repeated steep ramps. Awe-inspiring granite crags near the summit.

**Pass 4 — Pacific Grade Summit East (Climb 4)**
- Descent from Ebbetts summit (west side) to the Pacific Grade base, then climb to Pacific Grade Summit.
- Distance: ~2.5mi / 4km
- Elevation gain: ~980ft / 299m
- Average gradient: ~7.5%
- Character: Short but punchy. Includes several very steep ramps interspersed with shallower sections. The gradient is not uniform — steep pitches can hit 12-15%+. A road sign on the west side descent reads "24% grade ahead." Narrow road, not much wider than a single lane, but smooth pavement in good condition.
- Summit elevation: 8,021ft / 2,445m

**Pass 5 — Pacific Grade Summit West (Descent to Lake Alpine turnaround, then Climb 5)**
- Descent to Lake Alpine turnaround (7,303ft / 2,226m; full support/SAG at mile 68.3, open 10 AM - 3 PM, cutoff 2:45 PM). Then climb back up.
- Distance: ~5.2mi / 8.4km
- Elevation gain: ~1,650ft / 503m
- Average gradient: ~6%
- Character: The penultimate pass. The turnaround at Lake Alpine provides a full support station. The climb back up to the Pacific Grade summit is 5.2 miles of steady effort.

**Pass 6 — Ebbetts Pass West (Climb 6, the return)**
- After descending from Pacific Grade summit on the east side, riders descend to the base of Ebbetts Pass and then climb back over Ebbetts summit to return toward Markleeville.
- Distance: 5.2mi / 8.4km
- Elevation gain: 1,650ft / 503m
- Average gradient: 6%
- Character: The sixth and final pass. By this point, riders have covered 80+ miles and climbed 12,000+ feet. Mental fortitude matters as much as legs. The reward: the long, fast descent back toward Markleeville after cresting Ebbetts for the final time.

### Road Surface Quality
- **Highway 89 (Monitor Pass)**: Wide, well-maintained mountain highway. Good pavement quality. Open, sweeping road.
- **Highway 4 (Ebbetts Pass)**: Narrower mountain road with some tight turns. Caltrans regularly repairs pavement ahead of the event — in 2024, crews used over 48 tons of asphalt in four days to repair six large areas of failed pavement and eight potholes along Ebbetts Pass. The east side descent is especially technical with numerous sharp turns and steep drop-offs. Infamous "Cadillac Curve" has the ruins of a car at its base. Despite maintenance, expect rougher pavement sections compared to Highway 89.
- **Highway 4 (Pacific Grade)**: Narrow road, barely wider than a single lane in places, but pavement quality is surprisingly smooth and in good condition.
- Overall: Paved roads throughout. Quality varies from excellent (Highway 89) to adequate-with-patches (Ebbetts Pass sections). No gravel or unpaved segments.

### Key Cutoff Times
- Monitor Pass (eastbound): 10:00 AM
- Monitor Pass (westbound): 12:00 PM (noon)
- Lake Alpine turnaround: Open 10:00 AM - 3:00 PM; cutoff for eastbound riders: 2:45 PM
- Markleeville General Store (mile 100.6): Open 12:00 PM - 6:00 PM
- Overall finish: 6:30 PM

## Logistics

### Location
- Turtle Rock Park, Markleeville, CA (Alpine County). 2 miles north of Markleeville on Highway 89.
- Markleeville elevation: 5,501ft / 1,677m.
- Extremely remote — Alpine County is the least populated county in California.

### Nearest Airports
- **Reno-Tahoe International (RNO)**: ~63mi / 1hr 15min drive. Best option — regional hub with direct flights from major US cities.
- **Lake Tahoe Airport (TVL)**: ~18mi / 30min drive. Very small regional airport with limited commercial service.
- **Sacramento International (SMF)**: ~120mi / 2hr 15min drive. Larger hub with more flight options.
- **San Francisco (SFO) / Oakland (OAK) / San Jose (SJC)**: ~200-230mi / 4-5hr drive. Major international hubs but long drive.

### Lodging Strategy
Markleeville is tiny (population 191) with extremely limited accommodations. This is the single biggest logistical challenge for participants.

**Near the start (book very early — sells out months ahead):**
- Carson River Resort: 2mi south of Markleeville on Hwy 89. Cabins, RV hookups, tent camping. On the Death Ride route.
- Woodfords Inn: 6mi north of Markleeville.
- Grover Hot Springs area: Vacation rentals adjacent to Grover Hot Springs State Park.
- VRBO/Airbnb rentals in Markleeville: Very few units; expensive during race weekend.

**Camping (most practical for many riders):**
- Grover Hot Springs State Park: ~4mi from Markleeville. Popular and fills quickly.
- Indian Creek Reservoir: Another strong camping option.
- General camping in the area is abundant — far more available than hotel rooms.

**Nearby towns with more options:**
- Carson City, NV: ~45-50min drive. Plenty of inexpensive rooms.
- Gardnerville/Minden, NV: ~35-40min drive. Carson Valley corridor.
- South Lake Tahoe, CA: ~45min drive. More lodging but also premium pricing in July.
- Kirkwood, CA: ~30min drive. Ski resort area with some summer lodging.

### Race-Day Transport
- Check-in: Friday 11:00 AM - 7:00 PM at Turtle Rock Park. Limited Saturday morning check-in: 4:00 AM - 4:45 AM.
- Start: 5:00 AM from Turtle Rock Park.
- Parking at Turtle Rock Park and surrounding areas. Arrive very early given the 5 AM start.
- SAG (Support and Gear) vehicles on course throughout the day.
- Course is ~90% closed to traffic. Caltrans issues road closures for both Hwy 89 and Hwy 4 segments.

### Rest Stops / Feed Zones (9 total)
1. **Heenan Lake Quick Stop** (Mile 11.8): Water, quick energy, tech support
2. **Monitor Pass Summit** (Mile 14.8, 34.4): Full support, SAG, bib stamp. Cutoff: 10 AM eastbound, noon westbound.
3. **Topaz Turnaround** (Mile 24.6): Full support, SAG
4. **Wolf Creek** (Lunch stop): Full lunch service with seasoned potatoes, hard-boiled eggs, PB&J, trail mix
5. **Ebbetts Pass Summit**: Full support, bib stamp
6. **Lake Alpine Turnaround** (Mile 68.3): Full support, SAG, water, quick energy, bib stamp. Open 10 AM - 3 PM.
7. **Markleeville General Store** (Mile 100.6): Water. Open noon - 6 PM.
8-9. Additional water/electrolyte stops along the route.

**Nutrition provided**: Water, soda, snack items, seasoned potatoes (early stops), hard-boiled eggs (early stops), PB&J, trail mix, licorice, Hammer Nutrition gels and Heed, Pickle Juice.

**Drop bags**: Available at Monitor Pass rest stop and Topaz rest stop. Mark with bib number. Rest stop captains transport bags to finish line when stops close.

### Support Infrastructure
- EMS on course
- HAM radio operators throughout
- SAG vehicles for mechanical support and rider evacuation
- Medical support at rest stops
- 200+ volunteers

## History

### Origin Story
The Death Ride traces its roots to the mid-1970s Northern California cycling scene. In the early 1970s, Carter Squires of the Diablo Wheelmen organized a tour called "SuperTour." After the 1975 edition, Squires categorized it as an "utter failure because everyone finished." The 1976 tour was designed as a much more demanding course.

The Death Ride itself was born in 1978 when five friends decided to ride all the mountain passes of the California Alps in a single day, supported only by friends and family following in vehicles. The ride was informally organized by Wayne Martin, a former Markleeville resident.

What started as a dare among a handful of friends grew rapidly. By the early 1980s, elite riders from across the United States — and eventually from foreign countries — were signing up. The event became the primary fundraiser for the Alpine County Chamber of Commerce and local nonprofits.

### Course Evolution
- **Original course**: Both sides of Monitor Pass, both sides of Ebbetts Pass, and both sides of Carson Pass (Highway 88). This was sometimes described as "5 passes" (since you go over Monitor and Ebbetts twice, plus Carson once — 5 summits, 6 climbs).
- **Course change (circa 2017-2019)**: Carson Pass was replaced with the Pacific Grade (Highway 4). Primary reason: safety. Too many complaints from motorists and Caltrans about the number of cyclists on Highway 88. The left turn from Highway 89 onto Highway 88 was especially dangerous, and the left turn at the top of Carson Pass coming out of the rest stop was hazardous. The Pacific Grade provides a more contained, safer finish with less mixed traffic.
- **Modern course**: Monitor Pass (both sides) + Ebbetts Pass (both sides) + Pacific Grade (both sides) = 103 miles, 14,000+ ft climbing, 6 HC climbs.

### Key Milestones
- **1978**: First Death Ride. Five friends ride the California Alps passes in a single day.
- **1980s**: Rapid growth. Event gains national recognition in the cycling community.
- **1990s-2000s**: Peak participation years. Field capped at 3,000 riders. Event regularly sells out. Attracts international participants.
- **2018**: 40th anniversary celebration.
- **2020**: Cancelled due to COVID-19 pandemic.
- **2021**: Cancelled less than 24 hours before the start due to the Tamarack Fire. Lightning started the fire on July 4; it erupted on July 16 and advanced toward Markleeville, forcing cancellation of the July 17 ride.
- **2022**: Return after two consecutive cancellations (COVID + fire). 41st edition. Course now uses Pacific Grade instead of Carson Pass.
- **2024**: 43rd edition. Held successfully.
- **2025**: 44th edition. July 12.
- **2026**: 45th edition. July 11. Registration open.

### Cultural Legacy
The Death Ride is widely considered one of America's most legendary and iconic century rides, alongside events like the Triple Bypass (Colorado) and the Assault on Mt. Mitchell (North Carolina). Its combination of extreme climbing, high-altitude Sierra Nevada scenery, remote location, and nearly five decades of history gives it an outsized reputation in the cycling world. The name alone — "Death Ride" — has become a cultural touchstone.

## Climate (Mid-July at 5,500-8,730ft)

### Temperature Ranges
- **Pre-dawn start (5:00 AM, ~5,500ft)**: 40-55F / 4-13C. Can be very cool. Arm warmers and vest recommended.
- **Valley floors (mid-morning to afternoon)**: 85-95F / 29-35C typical. In extreme years (e.g., 2023), ambient temperatures reached 105F / 41C mid-afternoon with pavement temperatures of 140F+ / 60C+.
- **Pass summits (8,000-8,700ft)**: 55-75F / 13-24C during the day. Significant cooling at altitude. Wind chill can lower effective temperature further.
- **Temperature swing**: Riders experience a 40-60F+ temperature differential over the course of the day, from freezing pre-dawn to scorching valley heat to cool summit winds.

### Weather Hazards
- **Afternoon thunderstorms**: Common in the Sierra Nevada in July. Rain showers and thunderstorms can develop rapidly in the hot afternoon. In at least one recent year, approximately 80 riders were pulled off the course near Ebbetts Pass due to a heavy thunderstorm.
- **Hail**: Documented in at least one year, with temperatures dropping into the 30s F at the passes.
- **Lightning**: Real risk at 8,000+ ft with no shelter on exposed switchbacks.
- **Extreme heat**: The valleys between passes can become furnace-like by mid-afternoon. Heat-related illness is a significant risk for later riders.
- **UV exposure**: Greatly increased sunburn risk at high elevation. SPF 50+ and reapplication essential.
- **Wind**: Generally moderate compared to events above treeline, but exposed ridgelines and passes can produce gusty conditions.

### Altitude Effects
- The entire course is above 5,500ft, with extended time above 7,000-8,700ft.
- Riders coming from sea level may experience mild altitude effects: increased heart rate, reduced VO2max, faster dehydration.
- Hydration demands are significantly higher at altitude + heat combination.
- Acclimatization: Arriving 2-3 days early is recommended, especially for sea-level residents.

### Historical Weather Extremes on Race Day
- **Hottest**: 105F+ ambient temperature (2023)
- **Coldest**: 30s F with hail at the passes (year unspecified)
- **Typical**: High 80s to low 90s F in valleys, 60-70s F at summits, with afternoon thunderstorm risk.

## Community & Culture

### The Challenge Ethos
The Death Ride is not a race — it is a challenge. There are no timing chips, no categories, no prizes, no podiums. The only competition is against yourself and the mountain. The event's identity is built around the question: Can you complete all six passes in a single day?

### Completion Rates
The Death Ride has a notoriously high attrition rate. Multiple sources cite that 40-60% of riders do not complete all six passes. Many riders aim for 2, 3, or 5 passes as their personal goal. Completing all 6 is a genuine achievement that requires months of specific training, proper nutrition strategy, and favorable weather.

### The Tombstone Club
Riders who complete all six passes within the 13.5-hour window earn membership in the "Tombstone Club" — the unofficial fraternity of all-pass finishers. They receive:
- The all-pass finisher's pin
- The right to purchase the exclusive Death Ride Finisher's Jersey (a coveted item in cycling circles)
- The right to sign the official ride poster

Multi-year finishers are particularly revered. Some riders have completed 10, 15, or even 20+ consecutive Death Rides.

### Atmosphere
- **Pre-dawn start**: Riders gather at Turtle Rock Park in the dark, headlights illuminating the staging area. There is a communal buzz of nervous energy — the day ahead is daunting.
- **On-course camaraderie**: Despite the individual nature of the challenge, riders encourage each other on the climbs. The shared suffering creates bonds. Rest stops are social hubs.
- **Volunteerism**: 200+ volunteers make the event possible. Rest stop captains, SAG drivers, HAM radio operators, medical staff — all contribute to an event that is fundamentally a community effort.
- **Post-ride**: Celebration at Turtle Rock Park for finishers. Stories are exchanged. The finisher's jersey purchase line is a rite of passage.

### Economic Impact
The Death Ride is the single most important economic event for Alpine County. It is the primary fundraiser for the Alpine County Chamber of Commerce and multiple local nonprofits. The influx of 2,000-3,500 riders plus support crews transforms the tiny community for one weekend each year.

### Media & Recognition
- Covered by cycling media: CyclingTips, VeloNews, Gran Fondo Guide, Adventure Sports Journal, Bike Tahoe, PJAMM Cycling.
- Featured in mainstream California media: Sierra REC Magazine, Record-Courier, Tahoe Daily Tribune, CAViews.
- Rated and tracked by Strambecco, Battistrada, Gran Fondo Guide, and other event aggregators.
- The FulGaz virtual cycling platform offers a Death Ride challenge course.
- PJAMM Cycling provides detailed climb profiles for all six passes individually and for the combined ride.

### Comparison to Other US Challenge Rides
- **Triple Bypass (CO)**: 120mi, 10,000ft, 3 passes. Less climbing, lower difficulty.
- **Assault on Mt. Mitchell (NC)**: 102mi, 10,357ft, single massive climb. Different character (one long ascent vs. repeated passes).
- **Haute Route Rockies**: Multi-day stage race, different format entirely.
- **Death Ride stands apart**: The combination of 6 HC climbs, 14,000+ ft total, high altitude (5,500-8,730ft), remote location, extreme heat, and the 40-60% attrition rate makes it arguably the most demanding single-day organized ride in the United States.
