# Research Dump: Etape Caledonia

## Quick Facts
- 85 miles / 136.8km (flagship route), 1,555m / 5,101ft total climbing
- Route options: 85 miles (full Etape experience), 55 miles / 88.5km (1,166m / 3,826ft climbing), 40 miles / 64.4km (860m / 2,823ft climbing)
- Start/Finish: Pitlochry, Highland Perthshire, Scotland (elevation ~173m / 567ft)
- Second Sunday in May (20th edition: May 10, 2026)
- ~5,000 riders annually (capped field; regularly sells out)
- Founded 2007 — the UK's original closed-road sportive
- Entry fee (2026): 40-mile GBP65 (early bird GBP49), 55-mile GBP79 (early bird GBP65), 85-mile GBP105 (early bird GBP89). Charity entry via Cancer Research UK: GBP30 + GBP500 minimum fundraising
- Wave start system: groups of ~160 riders depart every 2 minutes, first wave at approximately 07:00. Waves allocated by estimated finishing time provided during registration
- Sweep vehicle follows at 13mph average — riders who cannot maintain this pace are requested to board and return to the village
- Fastest 85-mile times: 3:23:29 (Mark Walker, 2021), 3:35:26 (Tom Miller, 2024)
- Fully closed roads for all three routes — a signature feature that distinguishes this from most UK sportives
- Headline charity partner: Cancer Research UK
- Organizer: Motiv Sports UK (previously IMG / Limelight Sports)
- Discipline: sportive (non-competitive, mass participation, chip-timed)

## Course Profile

### Route Overview (85-Mile / 136.8km)
The full Etape Caledonia route is an epic loop through Highland Perthshire, starting and finishing in the charming Victorian resort town of Pitlochry. The road climbs out of Pitlochry from the start, rolling constantly as it weaves through pine forests along the northern shore of Loch Tummel. After Feed Station 1 at Kinloch Rannoch, the route continues along the flatter but no less scenic road hugging the northern shore of Loch Rannoch to the turning point at Bridge of Gaur — the most remote and wild section of the course, on the edge of Rannoch Moor. Riders return along the southern side of Loch Rannoch before veering southward, taking on the legendary Schiehallion climb, then looping through Glen Lyon and Aberfeldy before the cheering crowds greet them at the finish in Pitlochry.

### Key Climbs (85-Mile Route)

1. **Loch Tummel Rollers** — The road out of Pitlochry is uphill from the gun. Constant rolling terrain through pine forest along the northern shore of Loch Tummel. Not one single defined climb, but cumulative elevation gain that wears down the legs early. Passes near Queen's View, one of Scotland's most famous viewpoints looking west up Loch Tummel to Schiehallion.

2. **Schiehallion Climb** — THE signature climb of the Etape Caledonia. Approximately 5km long, average gradient ~6%. The hardest section comes right at the start as the road rears up from the valley floor with a couple of short, steep ramps at approximately 12% gradient for the first 0.75-1km. After this initial shock, it transitions into a steadier big-ring climb for the remaining 2-3km at a more manageable gradient. Described as "more of an enjoyable climb than a lung buster" once past the initial steep sections. The road twists its way up the flanks of Schiehallion (1,083m / 3,553ft), one of Scotland's most iconic Munros, with panoramic views back toward Loch Rannoch. The 40-mile route avoids this climb entirely; the 55-mile and 85-mile routes both include it.

3. **Glen Lyon / Fortingall Climb** — A longer, more gradual ascent through Glen Lyon (Scotland's longest glen) on the 85-mile route. Less dramatic than Schiehallion but adds significant cumulative elevation in the second half of the ride when legs are fatigued.

4. **Aberfeldy Approach** — Rolling terrain on the return leg through Strathtay toward Aberfeldy. Several short, punchy rises that test tired legs in the final quarter of the 85-mile route.

### Road Surface & Conditions
- Entirely paved, well-maintained Scottish A-roads and B-roads. The route uses roads such as the B8019 (Loch Tummel), the B846 (Schiehallion area), and minor roads around Loch Rannoch.
- Road surface quality is generally good, though typical of rural Scottish Highland roads — expect occasional patching, minor rough sections, and the possibility of debris (pine needles, small gravel) near forest sections.
- Full road closure for all routes — no motorized traffic. This is the defining feature of the event and exceptionally rare in UK sportives.
- Route is well-signed with marshals at junctions. Course markings and directional signage positioned throughout.
- No significant technical descents — descents are fast but on wide, well-surfaced roads with good visibility. The descent off Schiehallion is the most notable, with sweeping bends requiring controlled speed.
- Riders should be aware of potential road surface water in shaded forest sections, especially in early morning.

### Feed Stations
- **Feed Station 1 — Kinloch Rannoch** (all three routes): Fruit, flapjacks
- **Feed Station 2 — Forestry Commission Car Park** (all three routes): Fruit, flapjacks, macaroni cheese pies (local butcher), bacon rolls (donation to Pitlochry Scouts), potatoes, hot drinks
- **Feed Station 3 — Glengoulandie Campsite** (55 & 85-mile only): Fruit, flapjacks, cakes (from Glengoulandie Campsite), pork pies (local butcher)
- **Feed Station 4 — House of Menzies** (85-mile only): Fruit, flapjacks, macaroni cheese pies (local butcher), homemade cakes (donation to Aberfeldy Scouts)
- Mechanical support at each feed station (Cycle Hire Scotland)
- Maurten energy gels and solids available at Event Village (Saturday collection) and at Feed Stations 1 & 2
- Water refill points at all feed stations. No plastic cups — bring your own bottles

### Cut-Off / Checkpoint Times (85-Mile Route)
- Tummel Bridge Route Split: riders arriving after 08:30 diverted to 40-mile route
- Feed Station 1 (Kinloch Rannoch): must leave by 09:00 or diverted to 55-mile route
- Feed Station 3: must leave by 11:46 to reach Fortingal loop before closure at 11:56
- Sweep vehicle follows at 13mph average speed — riders unable to maintain this pace are asked to board and return to Pitlochry
- Roads reopen directly behind the sweep chain; any rider continuing past this point rides on open roads at their own risk

## Logistics

### Getting There

- **Edinburgh Airport (EDI)** — 70 miles / 113km south of Pitlochry. Approximately 1 hour 25 minutes by car via the M90 and A9. The most practical choice for international travelers due to extensive international connections (direct flights from major European hubs, plus transatlantic routes). The A9 is a well-maintained dual carriageway for most of the journey north.
- **Glasgow Airport (GLA)** — 90 miles / 145km southwest of Pitlochry. Approximately 1 hour 50 minutes by car. Good international connections but longer drive.
- **Inverness Airport (INV)** — 90 miles / 145km north of Pitlochry. Approximately 1 hour 50 minutes by car down the A9. Useful for those combining the trip with Highlands tourism, but fewer international routes.
- **Dundee Airport (DND)** — 32 miles / 52km from Pitlochry. Small regional airport with limited services (primarily London City connections via Loganair). Not practical for most international travelers.
- **Perth** — 30 miles south of Pitlochry; no commercial airport, but nearest large town with rail connections.

### Train Access
- **Pitlochry railway station** is on the Highland Main Line (Edinburgh/Glasgow to Inverness). Direct services from Edinburgh Waverley (~1 hour 40 minutes) and Glasgow Queen Street (~2 hours). ScotRail operates the service. Bike spaces available but limited — book in advance. The station is walkable to the Event Village.

### Accommodation
- **Pitlochry** is a small Victorian resort town with ~2,800 residents. Accommodation is limited and books out fast for Etape weekend.
- **Budget/Mid-range**: B&Bs and guesthouses from ~GBP80-150/night. Average B&B rate ~GBP140/night during event weekend. Popular options include Claymore Guest House, Atholl Villa, Westlands of Pitlochry, Craigatin House (boutique B&B).
- **Hotels**: Rosemount Hotel offers an Etape-specific package — near start line, early hi-carb breakfast from 05:30, cycle store, car parking. B&B from GBP340 per room for 2 nights over Etape weekend. Fisher's Hotel and Fonab Castle Hotel are other notable options.
- **Self-catering / Airbnb**: Holiday cottages and apartments available in and around Pitlochry, Killiecrankie, Blair Atholl, and Aberfeldy.
- **Strategy**: Book as early as possible — ideally the day entries open. Pitlochry has limited bed capacity relative to the 5,000-rider field. Consider Blair Atholl (6 miles north), Aberfeldy (15 miles southwest), or even Perth (30 miles south) if Pitlochry is full. Many riders camp at local campsites (e.g., Faskally Caravan Park).
- **Tip**: Some accommodation providers offer early breakfast (05:00-05:30) specifically for Etape riders — confirm this when booking.

### Race-Day Transport
- Event Village located at Atholl Road car park in central Pitlochry.
- Parking available in Pitlochry but limited; expect congestion on event morning. Arrive early.
- All routes start and finish in Pitlochry town centre — walkable from most town accommodation.
- ScotRail runs services to Pitlochry station, but Sunday morning trains may not arrive early enough for the first waves.

### Registration / Pack Collection
- Rider packs can be posted (paid option) — arrive up to Friday before the event
- **Saturday pack collection**: Event Village, 10:00-19:00. Each rider must collect their own pack. QR code required.
- **No Sunday morning collection** — you cannot collect your pack on race day
- Pack contains: rider bib (color-coded by distance: gold = 85-mile, dark blue = 55-mile, red = 40-mile), bike number with integrated timing chip, 2 cable ties
- Spot checks on bike condition at start and feed stations; organizers reserve the right to disqualify riders with unsafe bikes

## History

### Origin Story
- **2007**: Etape Caledonia was created by Ollie Knights and the IMG Challenger World team as Britain's first closed-road cycling sportive. The concept — closing public roads entirely to motorized traffic for a mass-participation cycling event — was revolutionary in the UK at the time. The inaugural event attracted approximately 2,500 riders in Highland Perthshire, instantly proving the demand for a traffic-free sportive experience.
- The name "Etape" (French for "stage") reflected the ambition to create a UK event with the prestige and atmosphere of European sportives like L'Etape du Tour.
- The route was designed to showcase the natural beauty of Highland Perthshire — lochs, glens, forests, and mountains — while being achievable for competent amateur cyclists.

### Growth & Ownership Changes
- **2007**: Inaugural edition, ~2,500 riders. Created under IMG ownership.
- **2008-2015**: Rapid growth to a capped field of 5,000. Entries routinely sold out within 72 hours of opening. The event became one of the most sought-after sportive entries in the UK calendar.
- **2009**: The infamous "tack attack." Thousands of carpet tacks were scattered across a 5-10 mile stretch of the route, causing hundreds of punctures, crashes, and injuries. The event was halted for 90 minutes while marshals and police cleared the road. Alexander Grosset, a local church elder and chairman of the Rannoch and Tummel Community Council from Bridge of Gaur, was charged with the sabotage but was subsequently cleared of all charges. The incident highlighted local tensions over road closures in rural communities.
- **2010-2012**: Further tack/screw attacks occurred in subsequent years — screws, tacks, and drawing pins found on the route before or during the event. Increased pre-event road sweeping and security patrols were implemented.
- **2018**: Limelight Sports took over event management under license from IMG.
- **2019**: New organizers confirmed after ownership transition. Perth and Kinross Council approved the event's continuation.
- **2020-2021**: Cancelled/rescheduled due to COVID-19 pandemic. The 2021 edition was moved to September 19 (from the traditional May date).
- **2022-2023**: Return to the traditional May date. Motiv Sports UK (under Y11 Sport & Media) acquired the event portfolio.
- **2024**: 18th edition. Over 4,000 riders participated. Described as having "a vibrant event village and hubub in the main drag of Pitlochry" — felt like "the event of old."
- **2025**: 19th edition. Sunday May 11. Thousands of cyclists from all over the UK. Event Village featured live music, artisan food, and a festival atmosphere.
- **2026**: Landmark 20th edition confirmed for Sunday May 10. Already sold out.

### Notable Moments
- **2009 Tack Attack**: The most notorious incident in the event's history. Highlighted the tension between a large-scale cycling event and rural Highland communities who face road closures and disruption.
- **2017**: Cancellation/rescheduling due to COVID-era disruptions (events around this period were affected by various factors).
- **2021 (September)**: Unusual September date due to COVID rescheduling. Mark Walker set the fastest recorded 85-mile time of 3:23:29.
- **2024 (Wahoo presented)**: Commercial partnership with Wahoo fitness technology. Tom Miller set the fastest 2024 time of 3:35:26 for the 85-mile route.

## Climate (May in the Scottish Highlands)

### Temperature
- Average high: 12-14C (54-57F)
- Average low: 4-5C (38-41F)
- Race-day range typically 6-14C (43-57F)
- Dawn start (06:30-07:00) temperatures potentially 3-6C (37-43F) — arm warmers and a gilet strongly recommended
- In exceptional warm spells, temperatures can reach 18-20C (64-68F), but this is not the norm
- Frost is still possible in exposed Highland valleys in early May

### Precipitation
- May averages 57-86mm of rainfall in Pitlochry (sources vary), spread across 16-18 rain days
- Rain is a genuine and probable factor — riders should prepare for wet conditions
- Scottish Highland rain tends to be light and persistent rather than torrential, but heavier showers are possible
- Mist/low cloud can affect exposed sections, particularly around Schiehallion and the higher moorland roads
- May is the driest month relative to the rest of the year, and also receives the most sunshine (~5.8 hours/day average, ~193 hours monthly)

### Wind
- Prevailing winds in Scotland are from the west and southwest
- Spring (including May) has a higher frequency of northeasterly winds than other seasons
- Wind exposure varies significantly along the route: sheltered in forested sections around Loch Tummel, potentially exposed along Loch Rannoch and on the Schiehallion climb
- The route's loop design means riders will face headwinds and crosswinds on different sections regardless of wind direction
- Extreme gusts are rare at the low elevations of the route (max road elevation ~400m), but Highland valleys can funnel wind

### Weather Strategy for Riders
- The organizers explicitly warn: "There is still the potential for snow in the Highlands in May!"
- Layering is essential: base layer, short-sleeve jersey, arm warmers, gilet or lightweight rain jacket
- Pack options for all conditions: full-finger gloves, shoe covers for cold/wet starts
- Weather can change rapidly in the Highlands — a sunny start can become rain within an hour
- The warmest, most sheltered sections are typically through forested roads; the most exposed sections are along open lochside stretches and the Schiehallion area

## Community & Culture

### Event Atmosphere
- The Event Village in Pitlochry creates a festival atmosphere throughout the weekend. Live music, artisan food vendors, cycling expo, partner activations. The small-town setting concentrates the energy — Pitlochry's main street is effectively taken over by the event.
- Post-ride celebrations include a finisher's medal, food, and music in the Event Village. Bike racking available until 16:00.
- The combination of closed roads and stunning scenery creates an experience that participants frequently describe as "the best sportive in the UK" — the traffic-free riding is a massive differentiator.
- Strong charity ethos: Cancer Research UK is the headline partner, and much of the fundraising energy and community spirit is channeled through charitable participation.

### Rider Demographics
- Predominantly UK-based riders, with the majority traveling from Scotland and northern England.
- Growing international participation — riders from "as far away as Hong Kong, Australia and the United States" attend, drawn by the combination of Scottish scenery and the closed-road format.
- Three distance options make the event accessible to a wide range of abilities: the 40-mile route is marketed as "perfect for newer riders," while the 85-mile route targets "experienced riders ready for a proper challenge."
- Bib colors distinguish route distances (gold = 85-mile, blue = 55-mile, red = 40-mile), creating a visible hierarchy on the road.

### Scottish Cycling Culture
- Etape Caledonia was a catalyst for closed-road cycling events in the UK. Before 2007, no UK sportive offered fully closed roads — a standard feature of Continental European sportives.
- The event helped establish Highland Perthshire as a cycling tourism destination. The local economy benefits significantly from the influx of 5,000 riders plus supporters over the weekend.
- Scotland has a distinct cycling culture shaped by challenging terrain, variable weather, and dramatic scenery. Events like Etape Caledonia, the Bealach na Ba sportive, and the Deeside Gran Fondo have positioned Scotland as a serious cycling destination.
- The event has navigated local tensions — road closures affect rural Highland communities, and the tack attacks of 2009-2012 were a visible manifestation of this friction. The relationship between the event and local communities has improved over time, with volunteer involvement from local groups (Pitlochry Scouts, Aberfeldy Scouts provide food at feed stations).

### Media Coverage
- Regular coverage in UK cycling media: Cycling Weekly, BikeRadar, road.cc, Cyclist magazine
- VisitScotland (Scotland's tourism agency) actively promotes the event as a showcase for Highland Perthshire
- ScotRail promotes special travel options for the event
- The tack attack incidents generated significant UK-wide media coverage (BBC, national newspapers)
- Official social media: @EtapeSeries (X/Twitter), @etapecaledonia (Instagram)

### Comparison to Other UK Sportives
- **Unique selling point**: Fully closed roads — still extremely rare in the UK. Most UK sportives (RideLondon, Dragon Ride, Fred Whitton) operate on open roads or partially closed roads.
- RideLondon (formerly RideLondon-Surrey) is the only other UK event with comparable road closure scale, but it operates on a different model (London-centric, flatter, urban).
- The Etape Caledonia fills a specific niche: a challenging, scenic, Highland sportive with the safety and experience benefits of completely traffic-free roads.
- The 5,000-rider cap maintains a premium, intimate feel compared to mega-events like RideLondon (~25,000) or Cape Town Cycle Tour (~28,000-35,000).

### Charitable Impact
- Cancer Research UK is the headline charity partner. The GBP30 charity entry (with GBP500 minimum fundraising) provides an affordable entry pathway while generating significant charitable income.
- Other charities offer places: Marie Curie, Retina UK, Revive MS Support, SignHealth, Shark Trust, among others.
- The event's charitable identity is integral to its brand and community standing.

## Scenic & Geographic Context

### Highland Perthshire Landscape
- Pitlochry sits at the southern edge of the Scottish Highlands, in the valley of the River Tummel. The town is surrounded by mountains, forests, and lochs — a quintessential Highland setting.
- **Loch Tummel**: A freshwater loch stretching west from Pitlochry. The road along its northern shore is one of the most scenic sections of the route — forest-lined, with views across the water to distant peaks.
- **Queen's View**: A famous viewpoint on the north shore of Loch Tummel, looking west toward Schiehallion and, on a clear day, the mountains of Glencoe 50 miles distant. Named after Queen Victoria's 1866 visit (though some claim it predates her, referencing Queen Isabella of Scotland).
- **Loch Rannoch**: Larger and wilder than Loch Tummel, stretching 15km east-west. The route along both shores provides the most remote, rugged sections of the 85-mile route. The turning point at Bridge of Gaur is on the edge of Rannoch Moor — one of the largest and most desolate expanses of moorland in Europe.
- **Schiehallion** (1,083m / 3,553ft): A Munro (Scottish mountain over 3,000ft) that dominates the skyline throughout much of the route. Its distinctive conical profile makes it one of the most recognizable mountains in Scotland. The cycling climb ascends its lower flanks, not the summit.
- **Glen Lyon**: Claimed to be Scotland's longest and loneliest glen. The 85-mile route passes through its eastern end, adding a sense of remoteness and wild beauty.
- **Aberfeldy**: A small market town on the River Tay, known for the Wade Bridge (1733) and the Birks of Aberfeldy (celebrated by Robert Burns). Riders pass through on the return leg.
- **Dull**: A tiny hamlet on the 40-mile route, famously twinned with Boring, Oregon, USA, and Bland, New South Wales, Australia.

### Wildlife & Natural Environment
- Red squirrels are common in the pine forests along the Loch Tummel road
- Red deer, buzzards, and golden eagles inhabit the wider Highland Perthshire landscape
- The route passes through protected forest areas and ancient Caledonian pine remnants
- Spring wildflowers (bluebells, primrose) are typically in bloom in May

## Scoring Dimension Notes

### Distance (85-mile flagship)
- 136.8km / 85 miles — a substantial one-day sportive distance. Longer than many European gran fondos but not ultra-distance.

### Climbing
- 1,555m / 5,101ft over 136.8km — moderate climbing. The route is rolling rather than alpine. Climbing ratio of ~11.4m/km is comparable to a hilly road sportive, not a mountain stage.

### Descent Technicality
- Low. Descents are on wide, well-surfaced roads with good visibility. No hairpin switchbacks or extreme gradients. The Schiehallion descent is the most notable but is straightforward.

### Road Surface
- Good quality paved roads throughout. Rural Scottish roads with occasional rough patches. No gravel, cobbles, or unpaved sections. Closed roads eliminate traffic hazard.

### Climate Risk
- Moderate to high. May in the Scottish Highlands is genuinely unpredictable. Snow is possible. Rain is probable (~16-18 rain days in May). Temperature range from near-freezing at dawn to mild in sunshine. Wind exposure on open sections. However, the low altitude and forested sections provide some shelter.

### Altitude
- Maximum road elevation ~400m. Start/finish at ~173m. No altitude effects on performance. No mountain pass riding.

### Logistics
- Moderate. Edinburgh Airport is 70 miles away (1.5 hours). Pitlochry is well-connected by rail. Accommodation is limited for the field size and books fast. Small-town infrastructure means limited bike shops, limited dining options compared to city-based events.

### Prestige
- High within the UK sportive scene. The UK's original closed-road sportive — a pioneering event. 20th edition in 2026. Not internationally prestigious in the way that L'Etape du Tour or Maratona dles Dolomites are, but carries significant cachet in British cycling.

### Organization
- High. Professional organization by experienced operators (IMG lineage, now Motiv Sports). Chip timing, wave start system, sweep vehicle, mechanical support at every feed station, well-provisioned feed stations with local food, Event Village with expo and entertainment. The closed-road operation requires extensive coordination with Perth and Kinross Council, Police Scotland, and local communities.

### Scenic Experience
- Very high. Highland Perthshire is genuinely stunning — lochs, pine forests, mountain panoramas, historic glens. Schiehallion as a backdrop is world-class. The route showcases a concentrated sample of Scottish Highland scenery. Queen's View alone is one of Scotland's most celebrated vistas.

### Community & Culture
- Strong local community involvement (Scouts at feed stations, local butchers providing food, volunteer support). Festival atmosphere in Pitlochry. Charitable fundraising central to the event identity. History of local tension (tack attacks) now largely resolved. The event is an economic pillar for the Pitlochry area.

### Field Depth
- Moderate. 5,000 riders is a healthy field for a UK sportive. Sells out annually. No professional riders — this is purely a mass-participation sportive. International participation is growing but still predominantly UK-based.

### Value
- Good. GBP89-105 for 85 miles of fully closed roads, 4 feed stations with local food, chip timing, mechanical support, and a well-organized event. The closed-road aspect alone justifies premium pricing versus open-road sportives.

### Expenses
- Moderate. Entry fee GBP89-105 for the 85-mile route. Accommodation in Pitlochry runs GBP80-340+/night for Etape weekend (premium demand pricing). Travel from Edinburgh ~GBP30-60 fuel or ~GBP20-40 train. Food/drink in Pitlochry is moderately priced for a tourist town. Total weekend cost for a UK-based rider: approximately GBP250-500. For international riders: GBP500-1,000+ including flights.
