# Research Dump: La Stelvio Santini (Gran Fondo Stelvio)

## Quick Facts
- Long route: 130km / 80.8mi, 4,270m / 14,009ft climbing, 3 major climbs (Rogorbello/Sondalo, Mortirolo, Stelvio)
- Route options: Short (64km/1,950-2,280m), Medium (108km/3,050m), Long (130km/4,270m)
- Traditional routes (pre-2024 roadworks): Long 160km/4,450m (via Teglio), Medium 137.9km/3,053m, Short 60km/1,950m
- Start: Bormio (1,225m), Lombardy, Italy. Finish: Summit of Passo dello Stelvio (2,757m)
- First Sunday in June (14th edition: June 7, 2026 — confirmed). Weekend format new for 2026: gravel on Saturday June 6, road on Sunday June 7.
- ~1,800-2,000 riders from ~50 nations. Field capped at approximately 3,000-3,500 bibs.
- Founded 2011 (1st edition). Rebranded from "Granfondo Stelvio Santini" to "La Stelvio Santini" in 2024.
- Entry fee: EUR 90 (early bird), EUR 100 (first tier), EUR 130 (final tier). Jersey included in entry. Bib+jersey+bibshorts option also available (~EUR 120).
- Mass start from Bormio. Rankings based solely on timed climb segments (not overall time) to discourage dangerous descending.
- Requirements: Sports medical certificate for competitive activities (mandatory for all routes, Italian law).
- Organizer: US Bormiese Cycling. Title sponsor: Santini Maglificio Sportivo (Italian cycling apparel brand).

## The Climbs (Long 130km Course — Current Routes, 2024-2026)

### 1. Rogorbello / Sondalo climb
- Part of the approach loop out of Bormio before doubling back. Moderate warm-up climb through Sondalo.
- Included on medium and long routes. Replaced the Teglio climb (inaccessible since 2024 due to Milan-Cortina 2026 Winter Olympics roadworks).

### 2. Passo Mortirolo (from Tovo di Sant'Agata) — HC
- **Distance**: 12.2km
- **Elevation gain**: 1,277m (4,190ft)
- **Average gradient**: 10.5%
- **Maximum gradient**: 18% (some sources cite 22% on steepest ramps)
- **Summit altitude**: 1,852m (6,076ft)
- **Category**: HC (Hors Categorie)
- One of the hardest climbs in professional cycling. Lance Armstrong called it "the hardest climb I have ever done." Mark Cavendish: "It's savage, f**king savage."
- First appeared in the Giro d'Italia in 1990. Marco Pantani memorial near the summit.
- Only featured on the LONG route. Two sustained brutal sections: km 3-6 averaging 12.5%, and the final 4km averaging 13.7%.
- Narrow, steep road through chestnut and fir forests. Limited views until summit.
- For the 2022 10th anniversary edition, a variant start from Sernio was introduced with gradients peaking at 17%.

### 3. Passo dello Stelvio (from Bormio) — HC — THE CLIMB
- **Distance**: 21.5km (from Bormio town center)
- **Elevation gain**: 1,533m (5,030ft)
- **Start altitude**: 1,225m (Bormio)
- **Summit altitude**: 2,757m (9,045ft)
- **Average gradient**: 7.1-7.4%
- **Maximum gradient**: 14%
- **Category**: HC (Hors Categorie)
- **Hairpin turns**: 40 numbered switchbacks on the Bormio side (not to be confused with the 48 on the Prato side)
- **Tunnels**: 7 tunnels/galleries between km 7 and km 10 — poorly lit, can be cold and wet.
- **Profile**: Remarkably steady climb. 86% of the route is at 5-10% grade, only 2% exceeds 10%. The first few kilometers are moderate (5-6%), the middle section passes through tunnels, and the final 4-5km are the hardest at 8%+ with dramatic hairpins and exposed alpine terrain.
- **Summit finish**: All three routes finish at the top of the Stelvio Pass. Riders cross the finish line at 2,757m — one of the highest summit finishes in any amateur cycling event worldwide.
- The Stelvio is the highest paved mountain pass in the Eastern Alps and the second highest in all the Alps (7m below Col de l'Iseran at 2,764m).
- When included in the Giro d'Italia, it is always designated the Cima Coppi (highest point of the race). Featured in: 1953, 1972, 1975, 1980, 1994, 2005, 2012, 2014, 2017, 2020.
- Road surface: Good quality tarmac throughout. Well-maintained Italian state road (SS38).

## The Stelvio Pass — Full Context

### The Prato allo Stelvio / Trafoi Side (NOT used in the Gran Fondo, but essential context)
- **Distance**: 24.3-25km from Prato allo Stelvio
- **Elevation gain**: 1,808-1,849m
- **Average gradient**: 7.5-7.6%
- **Maximum gradient**: 14%
- **48 numbered hairpin turns** — THE iconic image of the Stelvio. The most photographed switchbacks in cycling.
- This is the "classic" side used in most Giro d'Italia stages (1953 Coppi attack).

### The Umbrail Pass / Swiss Side
- Connects to Santa Maria Val Mustair in Switzerland.
- The Umbrail Pass (2,501m) joins the Stelvio road about 3km below the summit on the western side.
- Adds an international dimension — the Dreisprachenspitze (Three Languages Peak) above the pass marks where Italian, German, and Romansh-speaking areas meet.

### Summit Details
- Summit elevation: 2,757m (9,045ft). Some sources round to 2,758m.
- Facilities at summit: Hotels, restaurants, souvenir shops, Carlo Donegani Museum (history of the road and Giro d'Italia), summer skiing area.
- The road is typically open late May through October. Exact opening depends on snow clearance.

## Traditional Route (Pre-2024 Roadworks)
- **Long**: ~160km, 4,450m elevation gain. Bormio descent (46km) to Bianzone, climb to Teglio (6km, 402m gain), descent, 23km steady climb to foot of Mortirolo, Mortirolo (12km), descent to Bormio, then Stelvio summit finish.
- **Medium**: 137.9km, 3,053m. Same as long but without Mortirolo.
- **Short**: 60km, 1,950m. Bormio via Sondalo, then straight to Stelvio.
- The Teglio climb has been inaccessible since 2024 due to Milan-Cortina 2026 Winter Olympics road construction. Routes were shortened and rerouted via Sondalo/Tovo.

## Logistics
- **Nearest airports**:
  - Milan Bergamo (BGY): ~3hr drive. Best for budget airlines (Ryanair hub).
  - Milan Malpensa (MXP): ~3hr drive. Major international hub.
  - Milan Linate (LIN): ~3hr drive. Domestic/short-haul.
  - Bolzano (BZO): ~2hr drive. Limited flights, mostly domestic.
  - Innsbruck (INN): ~3hr drive. Good for central European connections.
  - Brescia (VBS): ~3hr drive.
  - St. Gallen-Altenrhein: ~3hr drive (via Switzerland).
- **Getting to Bormio**: Car or transfer service recommended. No direct rail. Nearest major train station is Tirano (40min drive), connected to Milan and to St. Moritz via the Bernina Express.
- **Lodging in Bormio**: Alpine resort town with extensive hotel infrastructure (also a ski/thermal spa destination). Hotels, B&Bs, apartments available. Book well in advance — the event coincides with early summer tourism season. Bormio also hosts 2026 Winter Olympics alpine skiing events, driving major hotel and infrastructure investment.
- **Bormio thermal baths**: Natural hot springs — excellent for post-ride recovery. Bagni Vecchi and Bagni Nuovi are the main facilities.
- **Race-day logistics**: Closed roads for the event. Start in Bormio town center. Riders finish at the summit — transport/buses provided from summit back to Bormio. Bike transport from summit also arranged.
- **Tour operator packages**: Multiple operators offer packages (EUR 699-999+ through European Cycle Events, Grand Tours Project, Sportive Breaks, etc.). Typically include accommodation, race entry, transfers, mechanical support.
- **Entry includes**: Custom bib with rider name, timing chip, official Santini jersey (must be worn during race), race package, feed zones, summit finish transport.

## Feed Zones
- Multiple feed/aid stations along all routes. Sponsor: Enervit (gels, bars, isotonic drinks). Water stations on climbs.
- No specific public details on exact food offerings, but Italian gran fondos typically feature local specialties at aid stations.

## Climate (Early June)
- **Bormio valley (1,225m)**: Highs 15-20C (59-68F), lows 5-10C (41-50F). Generally pleasant early summer.
- **Stelvio summit (2,757m)**: Dramatically colder. Average 7-11C in June, but can drop to near-freezing. Wind chill significant on exposed upper switchbacks.
- **Temperature differential**: 15-20C difference possible between Bormio and summit. Riders must carry warm layers for summit and descent.
- **Rain/weather risk**: HIGH. Mountain weather at nearly 3,000m is inherently unpredictable. Thunderstorms, rain, sleet, and even snow at the summit are possible in early June. The road typically opens only days or weeks before the event.
- **Historical weather incidents**: In the 2023 edition (11th), the Stelvio Pass was CLOSED during the race due to treacherous weather conditions (snow/ice at summit). Riders were rerouted and did not summit. Almost 2,000 riders started but conditions forced route modification.
- **Mandatory gear**: Warm clothing essential. Gilet/rain jacket strongly recommended. Arm warmers, leg warmers, full-finger gloves for descent from 2,757m.
- **Daylight**: ~15.5 hours in early June. Sunrise ~5:25 AM, sunset ~9:10 PM.
- **Humidity**: Moderate (~57% average in Bormio in June). Higher on climbs due to alpine conditions.

## History & Culture
- **Founded 2011**: Created by cycling club US Bormiese in partnership with Santini Maglificio Sportivo, the renowned Italian cycling apparel manufacturer based in Bergamo. The event was conceived to celebrate the legendary climbs accessible from Bormio.
- **Rapid growth**: From the inaugural 2011 edition, the event grew quickly to become one of Italy's most prestigious gran fondos. By the 9th edition (2021), over 1,600 finishers. By 2023 (11th edition), nearly 2,000 starters. By 2025 (13th edition), 1,800 participants from 50 nations.
- **2022 milestone**: 10th anniversary edition featured a new Mortirolo attack route from Sernio.
- **2024 rebrand**: Renamed from "Granfondo Stelvio Santini" to "La Stelvio Santini" with a new minimalist logo. The elongated "L" represents a break with the past; the double "S" of Stelvio Santini evokes the switchbacks of the iconic climbs.
- **2025 bicentennial**: The 13th edition coincided with the 200th anniversary of the Stelvio Pass road construction (built 1820-1825 by engineer Carlo Donegani under the Austrian Empire).
- **2026 expansion**: For the first time, a weekend-long format with a new gravel event on Saturday (in collaboration with 3T) and the traditional road gran fondo on Sunday.
- **Connection to Giro d'Italia**: The event's identity is inseparable from the Giro. The Stelvio first appeared in the Giro in 1953 (Stage 20, Bolzano-Bormio), where Fausto Coppi attacked Hugo Koblet on the climb, took the maglia rosa, and won his fifth and final Giro in Milan. The Mortirolo debuted in the Giro in 1990. In the 2012 Giro, a single stage combined both the Mortirolo and the Stelvio from Bormio — one of the most brutal days in Grand Tour history.
- **Stelvio Pass road history**: Built 1820-1825 by Carlo Donegani with 2,500 workers for the Austrian Empire to connect Habsburg Lombardy with Austria via Tyrol. Emperor Ferdinand attended the opening in 1825 and later knighted Donegani as "Nobleman of the Stelvio." The road has 88 total hairpin turns (48 on the north/Prato side, 40 on the south/Bormio side).
- **World War I**: The pass was the front line between Italy and Austria-Hungary. Fierce battles were fought at extreme altitude. Trenches, foxholes, and gun positions remain visible. The Dreisprachenspitze hotel was destroyed.
- **Stelvio Bike Day**: Separate annual event (not the Gran Fondo). Last Saturday of August or first Saturday of September. The entire Stelvio road is closed to motorized traffic and opened exclusively to cyclists. Free, non-competitive, ~6,700+ participants (2025). The 24th edition is August 29, 2026.
- **Stelvio National Park**: The route passes through one of Italy's oldest and largest national parks, established in 1935. Home to ibex, chamois, eagles, and marmots.
- **Top Gear connection**: Jeremy Clarkson famously declared the Stelvio "the greatest driving road in the world" on Top Gear. He described the Bormio side as "15 miles of asphalt spaghetti draped on an Alp."
- **Rankings format**: Unique among gran fondos — rankings based solely on timed climbing segments, not overall elapsed time. This deliberate decision by organizers reduces dangerous descending.

## Community & Atmosphere
- **Italian gran fondo culture**: The event is deeply embedded in Italian cycling tradition. More about the experience and personal challenge than competition. Bormio transforms into a cycling festival town.
- **International participation**: 50 nations represented in 2025. Strong contingents from Germany, Switzerland, Austria (neighboring countries), plus growing UK, Benelux, and broader European participation. Tour operator packages make it accessible to international riders.
- **Post-race**: Summit finish creates a unique celebratory atmosphere at 2,757m. Riders celebrate at the highest point in the Eastern Alps before descending (by bus or riding down). Post-race events in Bormio town center.
- **Santini connection**: The title sponsorship by Santini (one of cycling's most respected apparel brands, official supplier to multiple Grand Tours) lends the event significant prestige and guarantees high-quality race merchandise. The mandatory custom jersey is a collector's item.
- **Bormio as destination**: Beyond the race, Bormio offers thermal spas, excellent Italian cuisine, a charming historic center, and access to some of the world's greatest cycling climbs (Stelvio, Mortirolo, Gavia, Bernina). The area also hosts 2026 Winter Olympics alpine skiing.
