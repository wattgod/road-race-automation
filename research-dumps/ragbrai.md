# Research Dump: RAGBRAI (Register's Annual Great Bicycle Ride Across Iowa)

## Quick Facts
- **Distance**: ~391-540 mi (varies annually; historical average 467 mi / 751 km over 7 riding days). 2026 route: 391.4 mi / 630 km (shortest ever). 2024 route: 434 mi (most recent completed).
- **Elevation**: ~10,000-18,700 ft total (varies by route). 2026: 16,027 ft / 4,885 m. 2024: 18,737 ft (hilliest ever). 2025: 10,487 ft (6th lowest).
- **Location**: Across Iowa, USA — Missouri River (west) to Mississippi River (east). Route changes every year; passes through 6 of Iowa's 7 landform regions.
- **Date**: Last full week of July, ending on Saturday. 2026: July 18-25.
- **Field size**: Capped at ~10,000 weeklong riders + 1,500 day riders (registered). Actual daily counts far exceed this — unregistered riders swell numbers to 20,000-50,000+ on popular stages. Largest single-day count: ~50,000-60,000 riders on the Ames-to-Des Moines stage during the 50th anniversary in 2023.
- **Founding year**: 1973 (RAGBRAI I, then called the "Great Six-Day Bicycle Ride Across Iowa")
- **Edition**: RAGBRAI LIII (53rd) in 2026. Note: no ride in 2020 (COVID).
- **Entry fee**: Weeklong pass $230-$400 (sliding scale by registration date: $230 Nov-Dec, $250 Jan-Feb, $275 Mar, $300 Apr, $350 May, $400 Jun onward). Day pass: $45-$50.
- **Start format**: Mass start. Riders dip rear wheel in Missouri River (or Big Sioux River), ride east across the state, dip front wheel in Mississippi River at finish. No timing, no race — it is an organized touring ride, not a competition.
- **Route**: Changes annually. Typically 7 riding days with 8 overnight towns. 3-5 pass-through towns per day plus meeting towns. Overnight towns: selected ~1 year in advance, announced at the Route Announcement Party in January.
- **Discipline**: Multi-stage recreational touring ride (NOT a race)
- **Cumulative participation (1973-2024)**: 474,103+ registered riders over 51 editions, covering 23,247 total route miles.

## Course Profile

### Terrain Overview
Iowa is often assumed flat, but 10,000+ years of glacial erosion carved a network of stream and river valleys across the state, creating rolling terrain with short, steep hills. The closer to the Missouri and Mississippi rivers (western and eastern borders), the more dramatic the terrain. The Loess Hills along the western border feature wind-sculpted ridges, while the Driftless Region in the northeast has ancient limestone bluffs with punchy climbs.

### Road Surface
- Primarily paved state highways and county roads. Iowa maintains ~64% unpaved roads statewide, but RAGBRAI routes are designed to use paved surfaces.
- RAGBRAI has introduced optional gravel route alternatives in recent years (added ~2020s), but the main route remains fully paved asphalt.
- Road quality varies: well-maintained state highways mix with rougher county roads. Expect some chip-seal, patched surfaces, and occasional rough pavement on secondary roads.
- No technical descents — the terrain produces fast but straightforward downhills on wide roads.

### Typical Daily Stage Breakdown
Average daily distance: 67.1 mi (historical average). Stages range from 35-85+ mi. Each day includes 3-5 pass-through towns and 1-2 meeting towns with food/entertainment stops.

### Historical Route Records
- **Longest route**: 540 mi (1985, RAGBRAI XIII)
- **Shortest route**: 391.4 mi (2026, RAGBRAI LIII)

### Riding Pace
Under typical conditions, RAGBRAI riders average about 10 mph including stops. Most riders take 6-10 hours per day, spending significant time at town stops for food, entertainment, and socializing. The route is officially open with support from 6:00 AM to 6:00 PM daily.

## Logistics

### Nearest Airports
- **Des Moines International (DSM)** — Primary hub, centrally located in Iowa. Most convenient for most route years. ~150 mi to typical start towns in western Iowa.
- **Omaha Eppley Airfield (OMA)** — Closest to western Iowa start towns. ~80-120 mi from typical start points.
- **Eastern Iowa / Cedar Rapids (CID)** — Closest to eastern Iowa end towns. ~100 mi from typical finish points.
- **Quad Cities International (MLI)** — Moline, IL. Good for eastern Iowa routes or Dubuque finishes.
- **Chicago O'Hare (ORD) / Midway (MDW)** — Major hubs, 3-4 hour drive to eastern Iowa.

### Getting To/From the Ride
- Transport to start town and from end town is the rider's responsibility.
- Charter services (bicycle clubs, bike shops, private operators) provide bus transport to/from the ride, often departing from Des Moines, Omaha, Chicago, or the Quad Cities.
- Long-term parking available in start and end towns (reservations required).
- Many riders drive to Des Moines, park, and take a charter bus to the start; return buses bring them back to Des Moines from the end town.

### Lodging Strategy
- **Camping (primary)**: The vast majority of riders camp. Each overnight town designates camping areas (parks, school grounds, fairgrounds). Camping is included in registration — no additional fee for basic tent camping in designated areas.
- **Baggage service**: RAGBRAI's official baggage service (included or available for additional fee via charters) hauls gear from overnight town to overnight town. Riders pack their tent, sleeping bag, and gear into a bag; trucks transport it.
- **Charter camping**: Charter services ($300-$615+ per week) provide premium camping setups — dedicated campsites, shower trucks, portable toilets, meal service, bike mechanics, massage therapists, charging stations, and social areas.
- **Hotels/indoor lodging**: Extremely limited. Some host towns have a few motels, but they sell out months in advance. Not a realistic strategy for most riders.
- **Host families**: Some overnight towns arrange host family stays, but availability varies and is usually arranged informally.

### Charter Services (Major Operators)
- **Pork Belly Ventures**: Premium full-service charter. Morning coffee, vendors, catered dinners, live music, hot showers with towels. Can arrange limited hotel stays. ~$800-1,200+ per week.
- **Brancel Bicycle Charters**: Baggage-only option at $345/week; full-service packages higher.
- **North Iowa Touring Club (NITC)**: $300/week including round-trip bus, baggage hauling, team shirt.
- **Anywhere Cycling (ACE)**: Baggage-only per day $65; includes campsite, beverage tent, limited SAG, showers.
- **Padre's Cycle Inn**: Limits camp to ~150 guests; provides large six-person tents.
- **Quad Cities Bicycle Club**: Charter service with airport proximity (MLI).

### Support on Route
- **SAG (Support and Gear) wagons**: Emergency transport only for registered riders with medical/mechanical issues. Not a taxi service.
- **Medical**: Four ambulances and two motorcycle first-responders patrol the route daily. Medical teams (paramedics, doctors) stationed in each campground. Emergency medical treatment available in every overnight town.
- **Bike repair**: Bicycle repair shops along the route and in campgrounds. SAG drops riders with mechanical issues at the next repair station.
- **Iowa State Patrol**: Select intersection control during route service hours (6 AM - 6 PM).
- **Showers**: Available in overnight towns ($5-$10) via community pools, school facilities, or charter shower trucks.

## History

### Origin Story
RAGBRAI was born from a bet and a newspaper column. In 1973, **John Karras**, a Des Moines Register features writer and avid cyclist, challenged **Donald Kaul**, the Register's popular "Over The Coffee" columnist, to ride bicycles across Iowa together. Kaul, who was known for his acerbic humor, agreed — and the two planned to write about the experience in the newspaper.

The public was invited to join. The inaugural ride departed **Sioux City** on August 26, 1973, heading east to **Davenport**, covering the state in six days. An estimated **300 riders** showed up at the start; **114 completed the full route**. One of the most celebrated early participants was **Clarence Pickard**, an 83-year-old man who completed the entire ride on a used ladies' Schwinn, wearing woolen long underwear and a silver pith helmet. His story, covered extensively in the Register, captured the public's imagination and helped cement the ride's popularity.

### Name Evolution
- 1973: "The Great Six-Day Bicycle Ride Across Iowa"
- 1975 (RAGBRAI III): Michael Gartner, then Register editor, directed that "Register" be added to the title. The name RAGBRAI — **R**egister's **A**nnual **G**reat **B**icycle **R**ide **A**cross **I**owa — with Roman numeral suffixes was adopted.

### Growth Milestones
- **1973**: 300 starters, 114 finishers. Six-day ride, Sioux City to Davenport.
- **1975**: Event became officially seven days. RAGBRAI naming convention adopted.
- **1985**: Attendance surpassed 10,000 riders for the first time. Longest route ever at 540 mi. Attendance has remained above 10,000 every year since.
- **1988**: Estimated 23,000 riders on the leg from Boone to Des Moines.
- **1992**: Estimated 17,000 riders into Des Moines.
- **1997 (RAGBRAI XXV)**: Completed the founders' goal of riding through all 99 Iowa counties (Lucas County was the last). Estimated 20,000 riders into Des Moines.
- **2006**: Ownership transferred from the Des Moines Register to Iowa Rides, Inc. (later rebranded).
- **2013**: Estimated 36,000 riders counted on the road from Perry to Des Moines.
- **2020**: Cancelled (first time in history) due to COVID-19.
- **2021**: Returned post-COVID.
- **2023 (RAGBRAI L / 50th anniversary)**: Largest single-day turnout in history — estimated 50,000-60,000 riders on the Ames-to-Des Moines stage. Massive celebration.
- **2024**: Hilliest route ever at 18,737 ft of climbing over 434 mi.
- **2026**: Shortest route ever at 391.4 mi.
- **Lifetime**: 474,103+ registered riders through 2024. 939 Iowa towns visited. 127 overnight host towns. 23,247 cumulative route miles.

### Notable Moments
- Clarence Pickard (1973) riding the entire route at age 83 became the enduring symbol of RAGBRAI's "everyone is welcome" ethos.
- RAGBRAI has inspired over **200 similar multi-day rides** worldwide.
- The ride has been featured in national media (New York Times, Outside Magazine, CNN) and is recognized as the world's oldest, largest, and longest recreational multi-day bicycle touring event.
- The Des Moines Register's ownership and media coverage gave RAGBRAI unique cultural legitimacy from day one — it was not just a ride but a newspaper-sponsored Iowa institution.

## Climate (Late July in Iowa)

### Temperature
- **Average high**: 85-90°F (29-32°C); frequently reaches 90-95°F (32-35°C)
- **Peak days**: Can push 95-100°F (35-38°C)
- **Average low**: 60-67°F (16-19°C); nighttime camping is warm
- **Heat index**: Upper 90s to low 100s°F (37-40°C) when humidity is factored in

### Humidity
- Iowa July humidity averages ~78% relative humidity
- Muggy, sticky conditions are the norm, especially in eastern Iowa
- Dew points frequently in the 65-75°F range (oppressive)

### Precipitation & Storms
- July is Iowa's wettest month (~17 rainy days on average)
- Summer thunderstorms are common and can be severe: lightning, heavy rain, strong winds, occasional hail
- Storms tend to be afternoon/evening phenomena but can occur anytime
- Riders must be prepared for sudden downpours and should have rain gear and a plan for lightning
- Organizers sometimes delay or reroute stages for severe weather

### Wind
- Prevailing southerly winds in summer; headwinds from the south or southwest are common on east-west routes
- Open farmland provides no windbreak — wind exposure is constant
- Crosswinds on exposed ridgelines can be gusty

### Overall Climate Risk Assessment
Late July in Iowa presents **significant heat and humidity risk**. Heat exhaustion and dehydration are real concerns over seven consecutive days of riding. Thunderstorm risk adds an unpredictable element. Riders must manage hydration aggressively (RAGBRAI recommends drinking before feeling thirsty). Sunscreen is essential — long exposed stretches with no shade.

## Community & Culture

### Atmosphere
RAGBRAI is frequently described as **"Woodstock on wheels"** — a week-long rolling party across the state of Iowa. It is as much a social event and cultural festival as it is a bicycle ride. The riding itself is almost secondary to the community experience.

### Town Celebrations
- Every overnight town throws a massive celebration: live music stages, beer gardens, food vendors, local entertainment.
- Meeting towns and pass-through towns also host riders with food, games, activities.
- Towns compete informally to outdo each other. Past examples include Hawaiian/South Pacific themes with tiki torches, grass-skirted volunteers, and 14-piece bands; wading pools; water slides; petting zoos; and elaborate street festivals.
- Overnight towns receive financial support from RAGBRAI; meeting towns receive $10,000; pass-through towns receive $5,000.
- Local schools, churches, civic groups, and service organizations host food stalls and fundraise — RAGBRAI is a major economic event for small-town Iowa.

### Pie Culture
Pie is the defining culinary symbol of RAGBRAI. **"No RAGBRAI stop is complete without pie"** — and "every day on RAGBRAI is Pie Day." Church groups, school organizations, and local families sell homemade pie slices at virtually every stop. Apple, cherry, rhubarb, and coconut cream are Iowa staples.

### Food Culture (Beyond Pie)
- **Mr. Pork Chop**: Iowa pork chops on a stick are a RAGBRAI icon (Iowa is the #1 pork-producing state).
- Breakfast burritos, pancake feeds, pulled pork, sweet corn, and baked goods are standard fare.
- Food trucks from across the Midwest follow the ride.
- Community centers and churches host sit-down breakfasts and lunches.
- Beer is everywhere — local breweries and major sponsors ensure cold beer at most stops.

### Party Culture
- Live music every night in overnight towns (often multiple stages).
- Beer gardens and cocktail bars set up in parks and Main Streets.
- Team parties: many RAGBRAI teams (some with 50-200+ members) have elaborate themed camps, costumes, and nightly gatherings.
- "The party starts at sunrise and doesn't stop until well past midnight."
- The ride has a reputation as much for beer consumption as for cycling.

### Community Spirit
- Iowa hospitality is the backbone of RAGBRAI. Residents open their homes, lawns, and businesses.
- Volunteers number in the thousands across the host towns.
- The ride creates a temporary moving city of 10,000-20,000 people that transforms small Iowa towns for a day.
- Many riders return year after year (some have ridden every RAGBRAI since 1973).
- Team culture is strong — teams range from small friend groups to large organizations with matching jerseys, team buses, and decades of history.

### Media Coverage
- **Route Announcement Party** (January): Live-streamed event with music, interviews, and national cycling media attention.
- Published in the **Des Moines Register** (historically the ride's owner and primary media outlet).
- National coverage in cycling press (Bicycling Magazine, VeloNews, CyclingTips) and general media (NYT, CNN, USA Today).
- RAGBRAI's official website and social channels document each year extensively.
- Numerous documentaries and books about the ride (e.g., "RAGBRAI: Everyone Pronounces It Wrong" documentary).

### Demographics
- Riders come from all 50 US states and 20+ countries, though the majority are from Iowa and surrounding Midwest states.
- Ages range from children in trailers to riders in their 80s and 90s.
- Wide range of cycling ability — from seasoned century riders to casual cyclists on beach cruisers.
- Strong representation of recumbents, tandems, folding bikes, and unconventional rides.

## Scenic Experience

### Western Iowa (Days 1-2)
- **Loess Hills**: Wind-sculpted ridges of fine glacial soil, found only in Iowa and China. Rolling terrain with panoramic views of the Missouri River valley.
- Farmland: corn and soybean fields stretching to the horizon. Classic Midwest pastoral landscape.

### Central Iowa (Days 3-4)
- Flatter terrain with gentle rolling hills. Prairie remnants and agricultural landscape.
- Small-town Iowa at its most quintessential — grain elevators, white church steeples, and Main Street America.
- The High Trestle Trail Bridge near Boone (if on route) is a spectacular 13-story-high art installation spanning the Des Moines River valley.

### Eastern Iowa (Days 5-7)
- **Driftless Region**: Unglaciated landscape with dramatic limestone bluffs, deep valleys, and winding roads. The most visually striking terrain on most RAGBRAI routes.
- **Dyersville**: Home of the Field of Dreams movie site (the 2026 route passes through).
- **Mississippi River bluffs**: Steep, scenic approach to the river with panoramic views. The final descent into river towns like Dubuque is a signature RAGBRAI moment.
- Covered bridges, historic river towns, and bluff-top vistas.

### Overall Scenic Assessment
Iowa's landscape is underrated. The diversity — from Loess Hills to Driftless bluffs — surprises riders expecting flat monotony. However, the scenery is primarily agricultural/pastoral rather than dramatic mountain or coastal landscapes. The scenic experience is enhanced enormously by the cultural element (decorated towns, roadside activities, and community engagement).

## Scoring Dimension Notes

These notes map research findings to the 14 scoring dimensions:

1. **Distance (3-4)**: 391-540 mi over 7 days. Daily average ~55-77 mi. Long cumulative distance but moderate daily stages. Not extreme by ultra-endurance standards, but substantial for a week.
2. **Climbing (2)**: 10,000-18,700 ft total over 7 days. Rolling hills, not mountain passes. Average ~1,500-2,700 ft/day. Very low by mountain event standards.
3. **Descent Technicality (1)**: Wide paved roads, no technical descents. Straight, fast downhills on open farmland roads. Zero technical challenge.
4. **Road Surface (4)**: Fully paved route (main route). Well-maintained state highways mixed with rougher county roads. Some chip-seal. Good but not pristine. Optional gravel alternatives exist.
5. **Climate Risk (4)**: Significant. Late July Iowa heat (85-100°F), extreme humidity (78%+ RH), frequent thunderstorms (wettest month), wind exposure on open farmland. Heat exhaustion is a genuine concern over 7 consecutive days.
6. **Altitude (1)**: Iowa elevation ranges ~600-1,670 ft above sea level. Zero altitude challenge.
7. **Logistics (3)**: Moderate complexity. Requires transport to/from start/end towns. Camping is the default (no hotel strategy feasible). Baggage service required. Charter system well-established but adds cost. No single airport is convenient — most riders drive.
8. **Prestige (4-5)**: The oldest, largest, and longest recreational multi-day bike ride in the world. 50+ years of history. 474,000+ cumulative participants. Cultural institution in American cycling. Inspired 200+ similar events globally.
9. **Organization (4)**: Well-oiled machine after 50+ years. Iowa State Patrol intersection control, medical teams (4 ambulances, 2 motorcycle responders daily), SAG wagons, designated camping, baggage service, extensive charter ecosystem. Route officially supported 6 AM-6 PM. Professional but not corporate-slick.
10. **Scenic Experience (3)**: Underrated but not spectacular. Loess Hills and Driftless Region provide genuine scenic highlights. But most miles are through flat-to-rolling farmland. Pastoral rather than dramatic. Enhanced by cultural/community experience.
11. **Community & Culture (5)**: RAGBRAI's defining strength. "Woodstock on wheels." Small-town Iowa hospitality, pie culture, nightly parties, live music, team culture, 50+ years of tradition. Arguably the strongest community/culture score of any cycling event in the world.
12. **Field Depth (2)**: Not a race — no competitive field. Participants range from elite cyclists to casual riders on beach cruisers. No qualification required. Pure participation event. Very accessible.
13. **Value (5)**: Exceptional value. $230-400 registration for 7 days of organized riding with support. Food is cheap ($5-10 meals from church/school groups). Camping included. Total cost $600-2,000 for a full week, depending on charter level. Difficult to find a better $/day ratio for a multi-day cycling event.
14. **Expenses (2)**: Low absolute cost. Registration $230-400. Budget riders can do the whole week for $600-800. Even with a premium charter, total rarely exceeds $2,000. No international travel for US riders. No expensive hotels (camping default).

**Cultural Impact Bonus (4-5)**: RAGBRAI is an American cycling institution. Founded 1973, it predates most organized cycling events. Inspired 200+ similar rides. Featured in national media. A cultural event for Iowa, not just a bike ride. The Des Moines Register connection gives it unique media/institutional legitimacy.
