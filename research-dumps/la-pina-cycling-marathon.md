# Research Dump: La Pina Cycling Marathon (Gran Fondo Pinarello)

## Event Identity
- **Official name**: Granfondo Pinarello (marketed as "La Pina Cycling Marathon" or simply "La Pina")
- **Alternate names**: Gran Fondo Pinarello, La Pinarello Cycling Marathon, GF Pinarello
- **Discipline**: gran_fondo
- **Country**: Italy
- **Region**: Veneto (Province of Treviso)
- **Status**: Active. Last edition: July 14, 2024 (25th edition). Next edition not yet announced as of Feb 2026.
- **Official registration**: ENDU (https://www.endu.net/en/events/la-pina-cycling/)
- **Instagram**: @granfondopinarello

## Quick Facts
- **Gran Fondo route**: ~153 km / 95 mi, ~2,250 m / 7,380 ft elevation gain, Category 1 climbs (Passo San Boldo, Passo Praderadego)
- **Medio Fondo route**: ~114 km / 71 mi, ~1,100 m / 3,610 ft elevation gain, Category 3 hills through the Prosecco vineyards
- **Start/Finish**: Treviso city center, Veneto, Italy (elevation ~15 m / 49 ft)
- **Typical date**: Mid-July (second or third Sunday). 2024 edition: July 14, 2024.
- **Field size**: 3,500-4,000 riders (3,500 confirmed for the 20th edition in 2016; 4,000 reported for 2014)
- **Founded**: 1998 (1st edition). 20th anniversary celebrated in 2016; 25th edition in 2024.
- **Entry fee**: ~EUR 70 (2024). Earlier years ranged EUR 20-50. Fee typically includes race bib, timing chip, feed zones, mechanical support.
- **Start format**: Mass start from Treviso, approximately 07:30. Both routes depart together; riders choose their route split during the event.
- **Requirements**: Italian sports medical certificate for competitive activities (certificato medico agonistico) mandatory under Italian law.
- **Title sponsor**: Pinarello (the legendary Italian bicycle manufacturer headquartered in Treviso)
- **Organizer**: Local cycling association in partnership with Cicli Pinarello S.p.A.

## Route Options

### Gran Fondo (Long Route) — ~153 km / 2,250 m+
- Departs Treviso heading north through the Veneto plain (~20 km flat)
- Enters the Prosecco Hills (UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2019) via Susegana
- Includes the Muro di Ca' del Poggio, a short but vicious wall (1.1 km, avg 12.7%, max 19%) — a regular Giro d'Italia climb
- Continues into the pre-Alpine foothills toward the Treviso Alps
- **Passo San Boldo** (712 m): the "Road of 100 Days" — 5.9 km from the Tovena side, avg 7.7%, max 16.9%. 18 hairpin bends, 5 inside tunnels carved into cliff face. A WWI engineering marvel.
- **Passo Praderadego** (914 m): 8.3 km from Valmareno, avg 9.4%, max peaks to 21%. A brutally steep Category 1 climb — almost constantly above 10%.
- Returns south along the shoulder of the Monte Grappa massif
- Final rolling terrain through Prosecco vineyards back to Treviso
- The mountain crossings are classified as Category 1 climbs

### Medio Fondo (Medium Route) — ~114 km / 1,100 m
- Same start from Treviso heading north
- Takes riders into the lower hills just north of Treviso
- Rides through the Prosecco vineyard landscape (Conegliano-Valdobbiadene UNESCO zone)
- Features three Category 3 hills
- Does NOT include Passo San Boldo or Passo Praderadego
- Returns to Treviso through gentler terrain
- Suitable for strong recreational riders; still challenging but accessible

## Key Climbs (Gran Fondo Route)

### 1. Muro di Ca' del Poggio
- **Distance**: 1.1 km
- **Elevation gain**: 140 m (102 m to 242 m ASL)
- **Average gradient**: 12.7%
- **Maximum gradient**: 19%
- **Surface**: Excellent asphalt, fairly wide road
- **Character**: Short, explosive wall climb. Alternating ramps of 15-19% with very brief flatter sections.
- **Significance**: The only climb in Italy certified by the Italian Cycling Federation. Symbolic climb of the Prosecco Hills. Featured in the Giro d'Italia 7 times: 2009, 2013, 2014, 2017, 2020, 2022, 2024.
- **Location**: Within the Prosecco Hills of Conegliano and Valdobbiadene (UNESCO World Heritage Site).

### 2. Passo San Boldo (712 m)
- **Distance**: 5.9 km (from Tovena/south side, as used in the GF)
- **Elevation gain**: 451 m
- **Average gradient**: 7.7%
- **Maximum gradient**: 16.9% (steepest 100 m section)
- **Summit altitude**: 712 m
- **Character**: Spectacular engineering — 18 hairpin bends, 5 of which pass through tunnels carved directly into the sheer rock face. The tunnels are narrow, dimly lit, and atmospheric. Gradient never exceeds 12% (designed as a military road), though the steepest natural sections approach 17%.
- **History**: Built by hand in just 100 days in 1916 during WWI by Austrian-Hungarian troops as a military supply road. Known as the "Strada dei 100 Giorni" (Road of 100 Days).
- **Note**: The north side (from Trichiana/Belluno) is much gentler: 10.3 km, avg 3.5%, max 7.3%.

### 3. Passo del Praderadego (914 m)
- **Distance**: 8.3 km (from Valmareno)
- **Elevation gain**: 781 m
- **Average gradient**: 9.4%
- **Maximum gradient**: ~21%
- **Summit altitude**: 914 m
- **Category**: Category 1
- **Character**: The queen stage climb of the Gran Fondo. Almost constantly over 10%, with savage ramps approaching 21%. A serious, grinding effort. Less famous than Passo San Boldo but significantly harder.
- **Alternate approach**: From Valmaor (Belluno side) — 4.3 km, avg 10.3%, max 21%. Even steeper but shorter. Described as "very tough start/middle section with 2 km at 13%, peaks up to 21%."

## Course Profile & Road Surface
- **Terrain breakdown**: ~20 km flat from Treviso, followed by rolling Prosecco hills, then two major mountain passes, with rolling return through pre-Alpine foothills.
- **Road surface**: Entirely paved. Italian provincial and state roads in good-to-excellent condition. The Prosecco region roads are well-maintained tourist routes. Mountain passes are paved but narrow in sections (especially San Boldo tunnels). No gravel or unpaved sections.
- **Descent technicality**: San Boldo descent through tunnels requires caution — tight hairpins inside poorly lit tunnels. Praderadego descent is steep and technical with some tight switchbacks. Otherwise, descents are manageable on good roads.
- **Traffic**: Roads fully closed for the event. Police and marshals at all major intersections.

## Logistics

### Nearest Airports
- **Treviso-Sant'Angelo Airport (TSF)**: 5 km from Treviso city center. Served by Ryanair and other budget carriers. Very convenient but limited international routes.
- **Venice Marco Polo Airport (VCE)**: 27 km / 17 mi from Treviso. ~30 min drive. Major international hub with global connections. The primary airport for most international participants.
- **Venice Treviso to Venice Marco Polo transfer**: ATVO bus operates between the two airports, ~30 min, EUR 3-12.
- **Milan Malpensa (MXP)**: ~300 km / 3 hr drive. Alternate for long-haul international connections.
- **Verona Airport (VRN)**: ~150 km / 1.5 hr drive. Another viable option with some international routes.

### Getting to Treviso
- Venice Marco Polo to Treviso: 30 min by car/taxi, regular ATVO bus service
- Train: Treviso Centrale station is well-connected to Venice (30 min), Padova, Verona, and the broader Italian rail network (Trenitalia/Italo)
- Rental cars widely available at Venice Marco Polo

### Lodging Strategy
- **Treviso city center**: Best option — walking distance to start/finish. Numerous hotels, B&Bs, and apartments. A charming walled city with excellent restaurants and bars. Book early as rooms fill for the Gran Fondo weekend.
- **Prosecco Hills** (Conegliano, Valdobbiadene, Follina): 30-45 min north of Treviso. Scenic wine country setting. Several cycling-specific hotels including Hotel Ca' del Poggio (4-star, cycling services, bike storage, mechanic support). Ideal if combining the event with pre/post riding.
- **Venice**: 30 min by train. Abundant accommodation but more expensive and less convenient for early morning start.
- **Cycling hotel services**: Several hotels in the region offer bike storage, laundry, mechanic support, and VIP Gran Fondo entry packages.
- **Budget range**: EUR 60-150/night for hotels in Treviso; EUR 100-250/night for cycling-specific Prosecco Hills hotels with packages.

### Race-Day Transport
- Start/finish in Treviso city center (very walkable from central hotels)
- No shuttle needed if staying in Treviso
- Parking available in Treviso for those driving
- Expo/registration typically held the day before (Saturday) in Treviso

### Feed Zones & Support
- Multiple feed stations along both routes (exact locations vary year-to-year)
- Italian gran fondos typically provide: water, energy drinks, fruit, energy bars, small sandwiches at feed zones
- Mechanical support and broom wagon (sweep vehicle) provided
- Medical support along the route

## History

### Origin Story
The Gran Fondo Pinarello was founded in 1998 as a celebration of cycling culture in Treviso, the hometown of the legendary bicycle manufacturer Pinarello. Giovanni "Nani" Pinarello founded Cicli Pinarello in 1952 after his professional racing career ended — famously finishing last at the 1951 Giro d'Italia (earning the Maglia Nera, the last year that black jersey was ever awarded). He invested his 100,000 lira severance into opening a bicycle workshop in Treviso.

The Gran Fondo grew naturally from Pinarello's deep roots in the community. Treviso is a cycling city through and through — the brand has equipped Tour de France winners (Miguel Indurain, Chris Froome, Bradley Wiggins, Egan Bernal, Tadej Pogacar) and Olympic champions. The Gran Fondo became a way for amateur riders to experience the roads that Pinarello riders train on, passing through the same landscapes that inspired some of cycling's greatest frames.

### Growth & Milestones
- **1998**: 1st edition. Local event centered on the Treviso cycling community.
- **Early 2000s**: Grew steadily as Pinarello's brand prestige rose with professional victories.
- **2014**: ~4,000 participants. Established as one of Italy's major gran fondos.
- **2016**: 20th anniversary edition. 3,500 participants. Tour de France champion Miguel Indurain served as guest of honor, welcoming and encouraging riders at the start. Igor Zanetti (Cannondale-Gobbi-FSA) won in 4:37. Odette Bertolin (SOMEC-MG.K VIS-LGL) was fastest woman in 4:57.
- **2023**: Route offered at 153 km / 2,250 m and 113 km / 1,100 m.
- **2024**: 25th edition (technically 26th year, accounting for potential COVID cancellation). Held July 14, 2024. Routes: 153 km and 114 km.
- The event has evolved its routes over the years while maintaining the core identity of starting in Treviso and tackling the Treviso Alps.

### Notable Characteristics
- One of the "7 most famous Gran Fondos in Italy" according to Italy Bike Hotels
- Unique among Italian gran fondos for its direct brand association — Pinarello is not just a sponsor but the DNA of the event
- The affectionate nickname "La Pina" reflects the intimate, community-driven nature of the event despite its size

## Climate & Weather

### Typical Race-Day Conditions (Mid-July, Treviso/Veneto)
- **Temperature**: 18-30 C (65-86 F). Average high ~30 C (86 F), average low ~19 C (65 F). The start at 07:30 will be around 20-22 C, climbing to 28-30 C by midday.
- **At altitude** (Passo Praderadego, 914 m): Expect 5-8 C cooler than Treviso valley. Summit temperatures around 20-24 C in July. Arm warmers/gilet recommended for descents.
- **Humidity**: Relatively high (67-70% average in July). The Po Valley/Veneto plain can feel muggy. Drier conditions in the mountains.
- **Precipitation**: July averages moderate rainfall in Treviso. Afternoon thunderstorms are common — convective storms build over the mountains and can produce short, intense downpours. Risk is highest in the afternoon (13:00-17:00), which could affect slower riders on the Gran Fondo route.
- **Sunshine**: July averages 12.6 hours of sunshine per day in Treviso — the sunniest month. Mornings are typically clear and stable.
- **Wind**: Generally light in the Treviso plain. Mountain passes can have stronger winds, especially at ridgelines.
- **Climate risk assessment**: Moderate. Heat is the primary risk (dehydration, sunburn on exposed mountain roads). Afternoon thunderstorms are unpredictable but common in the pre-Alpine region. Early starters finishing before 14:00 face lower precipitation risk.

### What to Wear
- Standard summer kit for the start and flat sections
- Arm warmers and lightweight gilet essential for mountain descents (San Boldo tunnels are notably cool and damp)
- Rain jacket recommended as insurance against afternoon storms
- Full sun protection: sunscreen, sunglasses, cap under helmet

## Community & Culture

### Atmosphere
- La Pina is celebrated as much as a festival as a cycling event. "The whole town is taken over by cyclists from across Italy and around the world for this celebration of the famous Pinarello brand and Italian cycling."
- Race weekend transforms Treviso into a cycling village — the expo, finish-line celebrations, and post-ride festivities animate the historic center
- Strong association with Italian cycling heritage — riders are conscious of riding through Pinarello's homeland
- Prosecco is a defining cultural element — the route passes through the Prosecco production zone, and post-ride celebrations invariably involve local sparkling wine
- Food culture is central: Treviso is famous for radicchio trevigiano, tiramisù (which claims Treviso as its birthplace), and cicchetti (Venetian tapas)

### International Participation
- Draws riders from across Europe and increasingly worldwide
- Several international tour operators offer dedicated La Pina packages (Cycle Classic Tours, Granfondo Cycling Tours, CyclingHero, Maglia Nera Tours)
- English-language information available through multiple third-party sites, though the event itself is primarily Italian-language
- The Pinarello brand provides global name recognition that attracts international enthusiasts

### Cultural Significance
- Treviso is the birthplace of Pinarello (1952), Benetton (fashion), and sits in the heart of the Prosecco DOC zone
- The route traverses a UNESCO World Heritage landscape (Prosecco Hills of Conegliano and Valdobbiadene, inscribed 2019)
- WWI history is tangible throughout — Passo San Boldo (built 1916), Monte Grappa (major WWI battlefield), and the Piave River ("Fiume Sacro alla Patria" — River Sacred to the Fatherland)
- The event embodies the Italian gran fondo tradition: competitive timing for those who want it, but fundamentally a participatory celebration of cycling

### Media Coverage
- Covered by Italian cycling media (quicicloturismo.it, granfondo.it, tuttobiciweb)
- International coverage via Gran Fondo Guide, Gran Fondo Cycling Magazine, various tour operator blogs
- Active Instagram presence (@granfondopinarello)
- The Pinarello brand connection generates incidental coverage whenever the company is discussed in cycling media

## Scoring Dimension Notes

### Distance (Gran Fondo: 153 km)
- Long route at 153 km is a serious day in the saddle. Not ultra-distance but substantial, especially given the climbing.
- Medio Fondo at 114 km is a credible medium-distance option.

### Climbing (2,250 m for Gran Fondo)
- Two major passes (San Boldo and Praderadego) plus the Muro di Ca' del Poggio and rolling hills
- Praderadego at avg 9.4% with 21% ramps is genuinely hard
- Total climbing of ~2,250 m is moderate-to-challenging for a gran fondo (less than alpine monsters like Maratona or Stelvio, but more than flat gran fondos)

### Descent Technicality
- San Boldo tunnels require genuine skill and nerve — tight hairpins inside dark, narrow tunnels
- Praderadego descent is steep and technical
- Otherwise standard Italian road descents on good surfaces

### Road Surface
- Excellent throughout. Italian provincial roads in well-maintained condition.
- Tunnel sections of San Boldo can be damp. Otherwise dry, smooth asphalt.

### Climate Risk
- Mid-July heat is the primary concern (30 C+)
- Afternoon thunderstorm risk is real in the pre-Alpine zone
- Humidity in the Veneto plain adds to heat stress
- Altitude is moderate (max 914 m) so cold/altitude-related risks are minimal

### Altitude
- Maximum elevation: 914 m (Passo Praderadego). Low by alpine standards.
- Start elevation: ~15 m (Treviso). The full altitude range is experienced.
- No high-altitude physiological concerns.

### Logistics
- Exceptional. Treviso is 27 km from Venice Marco Polo Airport (VCE), one of Italy's major international hubs. Also has its own airport (TSF).
- Excellent rail connections. Start/finish in a walkable city center.
- Abundant accommodation at all price points. No remote mountain logistics.

### Prestige
- Strongly associated with the Pinarello brand — one of cycling's most prestigious manufacturers
- One of the "7 most famous Gran Fondos in Italy"
- 25+ year history. International recognition via the Pinarello name.
- Not UCI-sanctioned (reduces formal prestige vs. UCI Gran Fondo World Series events)
- Not at the absolute pinnacle like Maratona dles Dolomites or L'Eroica, but solidly in the top tier of Italian gran fondos

### Organization
- Well-run Italian gran fondo with 25+ years of operational experience
- Closed roads, multiple feed zones, mechanical support, medical coverage
- Registration via ENDU (Italy's standard event platform)
- Mass start format with route choice during the ride

### Scenic Experience
- UNESCO World Heritage Prosecco Hills — stunning vineyard terraces
- Passo San Boldo tunnels are a unique, thrilling spectacle
- Pre-Alpine mountain scenery with views of the Dolomite foothills
- Historic Treviso city center at start/finish
- WWI heritage landscape (Monte Grappa massif, Piave River)
- Less dramatic than high Dolomite passes but exceptionally varied and charming

### Community & Culture
- Festival atmosphere in Treviso. Post-ride Prosecco and local food.
- Deep cycling heritage — riding in the homeland of Pinarello
- International mix of riders, predominantly Italian
- "La Pina" nickname reflects the affectionate relationship between the event and its community

### Field Depth
- 3,500-4,000 riders is a strong field for a regional Italian gran fondo
- Attracts competitive Italian amateurs alongside recreational riders
- Not as deep as Maratona (9,000) or GF Strade Bianche (6,000+) but respectable
- International participants add diversity

### Value
- EUR 70 entry fee is reasonable by Italian/European gran fondo standards
- Includes timing, feed zones, mechanical support, finish line celebration
- Good value compared to more expensive events (Maratona EUR 180+, GFNY EUR 200+)
- Treviso accommodation is moderately priced compared to major Italian tourist cities

### Expenses
- Entry: ~EUR 70
- Flights to Venice: EUR 50-300 depending on origin and booking timing
- Accommodation: EUR 60-150/night for 2-3 nights
- Food/drink in Treviso: EUR 30-60/day (excellent and reasonably priced)
- Total estimated weekend cost: EUR 300-700 for European riders, EUR 600-1,200 for intercontinental riders (excluding bike transport)

## Data Gaps & Verification Notes
- Exact elevation gain for the 2024 Gran Fondo route is reported as ~2,250 m based on 2023 data. Some older sources cite 3,300 m for longer historical routes that may have included different climbs. The current (2023-2024) configuration appears to be 153 km / ~2,250 m.
- Field cap (maximum participants) not confirmed. Observed range is 3,500-4,000 based on 2014 and 2016 data.
- The 2023 edition may have been cancelled or modified (dalzero.it lists it as "Annullata" / cancelled), but 2024 proceeded normally. This needs verification.
- 2025 and 2026 edition dates have not been announced as of research date (Feb 2026). The event has been held yearly since 1998, typically in mid-July.
- The official event website domain is unclear — the event is registered through ENDU and promoted through granfondopinarello Instagram, but no dedicated .it domain was confirmed in search results.
- Entry fee of EUR 70 is from 2024 data via GranFondoGuide. Historical fees were lower (EUR 20-50).
- Monte Grappa is referenced as part of the return route landscape but does NOT appear to be climbed in the current GF route — the route passes along its "shoulder" (foothills), not over the summit (1,775 m). The famous Monte Grappa climb itself (19.1 km, 1,542 m gain, avg 8.1%) is a separate legendary ride in the area.
