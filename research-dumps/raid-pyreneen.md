# Research Dump: Raid Pyreneen (Le Raid Pyreneen)

## Quick Facts
- **Full name**: Le Raid Pyreneen (Raid Pyreneen)
- **Distance**: 720 km (447 mi) Randonneur formula; ~800 km (497 mi) Touring formula
- **Elevation gain**: ~11,000 m (36,089 ft) Randonneur; ~18,000 m (59,055 ft) Touring (28 cols)
- **Number of cols**: 18 (Randonneur) / 28 (Touring)
- **Location**: Hendaye (Atlantic coast, Basque Country) to Cerbere (Mediterranean coast, near Spanish border), traversing the entire French Pyrenees. Can be ridden in either direction; 90% start from Hendaye (west to east).
- **Country**: France (crosses 5 departments: Pyrenees-Atlantiques, Hautes-Pyrenees, Haute-Garonne, Ariege, Pyrenees-Orientales)
- **Date window**: June 1 to September 30 each year (permanent tour, NOT a single-day event)
- **Format**: Multi-day timed challenge (randonnee). Two formulas:
  - **Randonneur**: 720 km, 18 cols, 11,000 m climbing, must be completed within **100 hours** (~4.5 riding days)
  - **Touring (Cyclotouriste)**: ~800 km, 28 cols, 18,000 m climbing, must be completed within **10 days**
  - 85% of participants choose the Randonneur formula
- **Field size**: ~300 finishers per year (record year exceeded 600). NOT a mass-start event -- riders register individually and ride at their own chosen dates within the June-September window, either self-supported or with a tour operator.
- **Founding year**: 1950. Created by the Cyclo Club Bearnais (CCB), based in Pau, France. Idea first conceived by Maurice Bugard in 1912; officially launched in 1950 when five CCB pioneers (including Louis Pucheus and Lily Betbeder and Paul Mathis) completed the first crossing.
- **Entry fee**: Extremely low (the official CCB registration). FFCT members: EUR 9 (or EUR 17 with medal). Non-FFCT members: EUR 11 (or EUR 19 with medal). This covers the carnet (route card), certificate, and optional finisher's medal.
- **Organized tour cost**: Tour operators charge GBP 1,300-1,500+ / EUR 730-1,500+ for fully supported 6-night packages including hotels, meals, luggage transfer, feed stops, and mechanical support.
- **Start format**: Individual departure. Riders start at their own chosen time and date. No mass start, no peloton, no wave starts. The carnet is stamped at control points (typically cafes, shops, or hotels) along the route.
- **Route options**: Two official formulas (Randonneur and Touring). Some tour operators add Col de Marie-Blanque (optional, not on official Randonneur route). No "short" course option -- the full traverse is the only route.
- **Discipline**: Gran fondo / randonnee (timed multi-day challenge)
- **Milestone**: Bib #10,000 was issued to William (British citizen) who completed the Raid in 2021. Total finishers since 1950 exceed 10,000.
- **Demographics**: 16 nationalities represented. 90% of participants are non-French, with the UK providing the largest contingent by far.

## Course Profile

### Overview
The Raid Pyreneen is a coast-to-coast traverse of the entire Pyrenean mountain range, from the Atlantic Ocean to the Mediterranean Sea. The route crosses the full width of the French Pyrenees, progressing through five distinct geographical zones: the lush green Basque foothills, the high Pyrenean massif (Hautes-Pyrenees), the central Pyrenean valleys (Haute-Garonne and Ariege), and the sun-baked eastern Pyrenees descending to the Mediterranean. The terrain is overwhelmingly mountainous, with only the first and last stages offering significant flat or rolling sections.

The 18 cols on the Randonneur route range from tiny 169 m bumps near the coasts to the mighty Col du Tourmalet at 2,115 m. The route's climbing is heavily front-loaded: Days 2 and 3 contain the bulk of the major alpine passes, while Day 1 features moderate Basque hills and Day 5 is largely descending to the Mediterranean.

### Road Surface
All roads are paved (100% tarmac). The route follows French departmental roads (D-roads) and national roads. Surface quality is generally excellent on the major cols (Tourmalet, Aubisque, Aspin, Peyresourde) as these are heavily used tourist and Tour de France routes. Some minor cols in the central section have rougher surfaces. No gravel, no off-road sections.

### The 18 Cols (West to East, Randonneur Route)

#### Day 1: Hendaye to Arudy / Oloron-Sainte-Marie region (~180 km, ~2,300 m climbing)
Basque Country foothills. Rolling terrain with four modest climbs. The approach is through the picturesque Basque interior.

**1. Col de Saint-Ignace**
- Summit altitude: 169 m
- Distance: ~2.5 km
- Average gradient: ~5%
- Character: Gentle warm-up climb through Basque countryside near the start. More of a road ramp than a proper col. The famous Petit Train de la Rhune rack railway starts from the summit.

**2. Col de Pinodieta (Pinodiet)**
- Summit altitude: 176 m
- Distance: ~2 km
- Average gradient: ~4%
- Character: Another small Basque Country hill. Barely noticeable.

**3. Col d'Ibardin / Col d'Ipharlatze**
- Summit altitude: 328 m
- Distance: ~4 km
- Average gradient: ~5%
- Character: First taste of climbing through Basque hill country. Views over the Basque coast.

**4. Col d'Osquich**
- Summit altitude: 500 m (some sources 392 m)
- Distance: ~8 km
- Elevation gain: ~350 m
- Average gradient: ~4.5%
- Character: The last Basque col before the route turns toward the high mountains. Still modest but a step up from the coastal bumps.

#### Day 2: Aubisque-Tourmalet day (~110-130 km, ~3,000-3,500 m climbing)
The hardest day. Three legendary Tour de France climbs back-to-back.

**5. Col d'Aubisque**
- Summit altitude: 1,709 m
- Distance (from Laruns): 16.6 km
- Elevation gain: 1,190 m
- Average gradient: 7.2%
- Maximum gradient: 10%
- Character: HC (Hors Categorie) in the Tour de France. A genuine beast. The first 10 km from Eaux-Bonnes are relentlessly steep (7-8%), with a brief false-flat section before the final exposed ramps to the summit. The road passes through a narrow gorge and then opens out to alpine meadows. Spectacular views from the summit over the Cirque du Litor. First featured in the Tour de France in 1910 as part of the legendary "Circle of Death" stage.

**6. Col du Soulor**
- Summit altitude: 1,474 m
- Distance (from Argeles-Gazost): 19.5 km
- Elevation gain: 1,019 m
- Average gradient: 5.2%
- Maximum gradient: 10%+
- Character: Often climbed as an approach to or descent from the Aubisque. From the Aubisque summit, riders descend the eastern side to the Col du Soulor (the Aubisque and Soulor are linked by a spectacular corniche road). Some route variants ascend from Argeles-Gazost via Arrens-Marsous, where the gradient steepens dramatically after Arrens. The Soulor is sometimes described as the Aubisque's "little brother." Views of the Pic du Midi and surrounding peaks.

**7. Col du Tourmalet**
- Summit altitude: 2,115 m
- Distance (west, from Luz-Saint-Sauveur): 19.0 km
- Elevation gain: 1,404 m
- Average gradient: 7.4%
- Maximum gradient: 10.2% (near summit)
- Distance (east, from Sainte-Marie-de-Campan): 17.2 km
- Elevation gain: 1,268 m
- Average gradient: 7.4%
- Maximum gradient: 12%
- Character: The highest paved pass in the French Pyrenees. HC in every classification. The undisputed king of the Pyrenees. Featured in the Tour de France more than any other climb (80+ times since 1910). The western ascent from Luz-Saint-Sauveur is the classic approach on the Raid route. Passes through Bareges and the ski station of La Mongie before the final exposed switchbacks. Giant statue of Octave Lapize (who reportedly shouted "You are assassins!" at Tour organizers during the first ascent in 1910) at the summit. The Tourmalet is typically the climax of Day 2 and the most emotionally significant moment of the entire Raid.

#### Day 3: Aspin-Peyresourde and the Central Pyrenees (~120-140 km, ~2,500-3,000 m climbing)

**8. Col d'Aspin**
- Summit altitude: 1,489 m
- Distance (west, from Sainte-Marie-de-Campan): 12.6 km
- Elevation gain: 642 m
- Average gradient: 5.0%
- Distance (east, from Arreau): 12.0 km
- Elevation gain: 779 m
- Average gradient: 6.5%
- Maximum gradient: 9.5%
- Character: Category 1. A beautiful, relatively steady climb through forests and pastures. Less savage than its neighbors but consistently scenic. Views of herds of cattle and the distinctive Pyrenean barns. Often descended from the west on the Raid route after the Tourmalet, then climbed from the east (Arreau) if the stages split differently.

**9. Col de Peyresourde**
- Summit altitude: 1,569 m
- Distance (from Bagneres-de-Luchon, east): 13.8 km
- Elevation gain: 949 m
- Average gradient: 6.9%
- Maximum gradient: 11.7%
- Distance (from Avajan, west): 8.3 km
- Elevation gain: 559 m
- Average gradient: 6.7%
- Character: Category 1. Featured in the Tour de France 69 times (fourth most-visited TdF climb). First included in 1910. A proper mountain pass with two distinct personalities -- the Luchon side is long and steady, the Avajan side shorter but steeper. Part of the legendary "Circle of Death" route from 1910.

**10. Col des Ares**
- Summit altitude: 797 m
- Distance: ~6 km
- Average gradient: ~4%
- Character: A transitional col between the Haute-Garonne and Ariege valleys. Not particularly steep or long but provides a link between the Peyresourde descent and the approach to Portet d'Aspet.

**11. Col de Buret**
- Summit altitude: 599-602 m
- Distance: ~5 km
- Average gradient: ~3.5%
- Character: Minor col, often barely noticed. Links the Ares descent to the Aspet valley.

**12. Col de Portet d'Aspet**
- Summit altitude: 1,069 m
- Distance (from Aspet): 14.3 km
- Elevation gain: 594 m
- Average gradient: 4.2% (overall); the final 4.4 km from the D618/D44 junction averages 9.6% with sections exceeding 11%
- Maximum gradient: 11%+
- Character: Category 2. A deceptive climb -- gentle for the first 10 km then savage in the final 4 km. Memorialized in cycling for the tragic death of Fabio Casartelli in the 1995 Tour de France, who crashed on the descent. A memorial plaque marks the spot. Forested climb with limited views until near the summit.

#### Day 4: The Central-Eastern Traverse (~130-150 km, ~2,000-2,500 m climbing)

**13. Col de Port**
- Summit altitude: 1,249 m
- Distance: ~12 km (from Tarascon-sur-Ariege)
- Average gradient: ~5%
- Character: Category 2. One of the "easier" major cols on the route. Well-paved road through a quiet, less-touristed part of the Ariege. Scenic but not dramatic compared to the Day 2/3 giants.

**14. Col de Pailheres (Port de Pailheres)**
- Summit altitude: 2,001 m
- Distance (from Usson-les-Bains, east): 16.6 km
- Elevation gain: 1,218 m
- Average gradient: 7.3%
- Distance (from Ax-les-Thermes, west): 18.6 km
- Maximum gradient: 10.2%
- Character: HC. One of only two road passes to top 2,000 m in the French Pyrenees (the other being the Tourmalet). A relative latecomer to the Tour de France (first appearance 2003). Spectacular, wild, and exposed at the summit. On some route variants, the Col du Puymorens is used instead.

**15. Col du Puymorens**
- Summit altitude: 1,915 m
- Distance (from Ax-les-Thermes): ~27 km
- Elevation gain: ~1,200 m
- Average gradient: 4.4%
- Maximum gradient: 7.7%
- Character: Category 1. A long, steady grind rather than a steep wall. Connects the Ariege to the Cerdagne plateau. The road is wide and well-maintained. In winter, a tunnel bypasses the summit (bicycles not allowed in tunnel). The approach passes through Ax-les-Thermes, a thermal spa town popular with cyclists.

#### Day 5: Eastern Pyrenees to the Mediterranean (~90-120 km, ~700-1,500 m climbing)

**16. Col de Jau**
- Summit altitude: 1,513 m (some sources ~1,500 m)
- Distance: ~10.6 km (from Mosset)
- Elevation gain: ~645 m
- Average gradient: 6.1%
- Character: Category 2. A scenic climb through the eastern Pyrenean forests. Less famous than the western giants but offers a different landscape -- drier, more Mediterranean character. The Jau marks the transition from mountain terrain to the Roussillon coastal zone.

**17. Col de Saint-Pierre**
- Summit altitude: 231 m
- Character: A tiny coastal hill near the Mediterranean. More of a road undulation than a proper col. Barely perceptible after days of alpine climbing.

**18. Col de la Ternere (Col de Banyuls)**
- Summit altitude: 233 m
- Distance: ~3 km
- Character: The final col before Cerbere. A small rise overlooking the Mediterranean. The emotional payoff is enormous -- riders crest this bump and see the sea, marking the end of the traverse.

### Optional Col (Not Counted in 18)

**Col de Marie-Blanque**
- Summit altitude: 1,035 m
- Distance (from Escot): 9.2 km
- Elevation gain: 702 m
- Average gradient: 7.6%
- Maximum gradient: 12%+
- Character: Category 1. A short, sharp, brutal climb. Bradley Wiggins reportedly rated it among his least favorite climbs. Not on the official Randonneur route but many tour operators include it as an optional addition on Day 1 or early Day 2.

Riders typically depart at dawn on Day 1 and must arrive in Cerbere before the equivalent time on Day 5 (i.e., 4 full days + up to 4 hours). The final day is often treated as a team time trial by tour groups, with a fast pace to beat the deadline.

## Logistics

### Nearest Airports

**Start (Hendaye, Atlantic end):**
- **Biarritz-Pays Basque Airport (BIQ)**: 30 km / 22 min drive. The closest and most convenient. Ryanair, easyJet, Air France, Volotea serve it with connections from UK, Paris, and European cities. Direct flights from London Stansted, Bristol, Manchester.
- **San Sebastian Airport (EAS)**: 25 km across the Spanish border. Limited commercial service.
- **Toulouse-Blagnac Airport (TLS)**: 328 km / ~3.5 hr drive. Major international hub with far more flight options. Many tour operators offer transfers from Toulouse.
- **Bordeaux-Merignac Airport (BOD)**: ~250 km / ~2.5 hr drive. Another option with extensive connections.

**Finish (Cerbere, Mediterranean end):**
- **Perpignan-Rivesaltes Airport (PGF)**: 47.5 km / 42 min drive. Small airport with Ryanair connections.
- **Girona-Costa Brava Airport (GRO)**: ~80 km south across the Spanish border. Budget airline hub.
- **Barcelona El Prat (BCN)**: ~200 km / 2 hr drive. Major international hub.
- **Montpellier (MPL)**: ~170 km / 1.5 hr drive.

### Lodging Strategy
- **Self-supported riders**: Must pre-book all accommodation. The route passes through small Pyrenean villages with limited hotel capacity. During peak season (July-August), accommodation fills fast. Key overnight towns: Oloron-Sainte-Marie or Arudy (night 1), Luz-Saint-Sauveur or Sainte-Marie-de-Campan (night 2), Bagneres-de-Luchon (night 3), Tarascon-sur-Ariege or Ax-les-Thermes (night 4), Prades (night 4/5).
- **Tour operators**: Handle all accommodation, typically 2-3 star hotels. Prices range from EUR 730 to GBP 1,500+ for 5-6 nights including half-board (breakfast + dinner).
- **Camping**: Some self-supported riders camp, which is permitted at designated campsites along the route. CCB provides camping-compatible route guidance.
- **Key towns with good hotel infrastructure**: Oloron-Sainte-Marie, Luz-Saint-Sauveur, Bagneres-de-Luchon (famous spa town with many hotels), Ax-les-Thermes (spa town), Prades.

### Race-Day Transport & Support
- This is NOT a closed-road event. All riding is on open public roads with normal traffic.
- No official feed zones, timing mats, or race infrastructure. The carnet stamp system uses existing businesses (cafes, shops, tourism offices) as control points.
- Tour operators provide feed stops every ~35 km, luggage transfer between stages, mechanical support, and follow vehicles.
- Self-supported riders must carry their own nutrition or plan cafe/shop stops. Many towns on the route have bakeries and small grocery stores.
- Bike shops are available in Oloron-Sainte-Marie, Pau, Lourdes, Bagneres-de-Luchon, and Ax-les-Thermes. Mid-mountain options are limited.

### Getting Between Start and Finish
- Hendaye to Cerbere by car: 383 miles / 5 hr 25 min drive
- By train (SNCF): ~9 hours with connections
- Tour operators handle transfers. Common pattern: fly into Biarritz or Toulouse, transfer to Hendaye; ride to Cerbere; transfer to Perpignan or Toulouse for flights home.

## History

### Origin Story
The concept of a bicycle traverse of the Pyrenees was first imagined in 1912 by Maurice Bugard, a young cyclist from the Cyclo Club Bearnais (CCB) in Pau, Pyrenees-Atlantiques. However, the two World Wars intervened, and the idea lay dormant for nearly four decades.

In June 1950, five members of the CCB -- including Louis Pucheus, Lily Betbeder, and Paul Mathis -- finally completed the first crossing, riding from Cerbere to Hendaye (east to west). The Raid was officially presented to the regional press at the end of 1951, in the presence of its creator Maurice Bugard, then 73 years old. By the end of 1952, the Raid was deemed a success and Louis Pucheus, a member of the Raid Pyreneen committee, documented the history of the event with passion and precision.

### Growth Milestones
- **1950**: First crossing by 5 CCB pioneers (Cerbere to Hendaye)
- **1951**: Official launch and press presentation with founder Maurice Bugard
- **1952**: First full year of organized entries; 67 official entrants, 56 riders started, 38 finished
- **1950s-1970s**: Slow growth, primarily French participants
- **1980s-1990s**: International awareness grows, especially in the UK. Tour operators begin offering supported Raid packages.
- **2000s**: The Raid becomes one of Europe's most recognized multi-day cycling challenges. Annual finishers consistently above 300.
- **2020**: The Raid Pyreneen celebrates its 70th anniversary
- **2021**: Bib #10,000 issued to William, a British citizen -- marking 10,000 cumulative finishers in 71 years
- **Record year**: More than 600 finishers (exact year not confirmed, likely mid-2010s)
- **Current era**: ~300 finishers per year from 16+ nationalities. 90% are international (non-French), with British riders dominating.

### Notable Moments
- The Raid follows many of the same roads used in the Tour de France's most storied Pyrenean stages, creating a direct link to professional cycling history.
- The 1910 Tour de France's "Circle of Death" stage -- the first-ever mountain stage in professional cycling -- covered cols that are now central to the Raid route (Peyresourde, Aspin, Tourmalet, Aubisque).
- Fabio Casartelli's fatal crash on the descent of the Col de Portet d'Aspet during the 1995 Tour de France is commemorated by a memorial on the Raid route.
- The Raid has inspired similar coast-to-coast challenges elsewhere, including the Raid Alpine (Lake Geneva to Nice) and various Pyrenean variants (e.g., the Trans Pyrenees race, which is an unsupported bikepacking race).

## Climate

### Event Window Weather (June 1 - September 30)

**June (early season)**:
- Generally the best conditions. Stable weather, pleasant temperatures, most snow cleared from the highest passes.
- Valley temperatures: 20-28C (68-82F). Summit temperatures: 8-15C (46-59F).
- Precipitation risk: Moderate. Afternoon thunderstorms possible but less frequent than July/August.
- Daylight: Very long days (sunrise ~6:15am, sunset ~9:45pm), maximizing riding hours.

**July (peak season)**:
- Warmest month. Valleys can be hot (25-33C / 77-91F). Summits remain cool (10-18C / 50-64F).
- Higher thunderstorm risk, especially late afternoon. Thunderstorms develop when southerly winds bring moist air from Spain.
- The Tourmalet summit at 2,115 m can experience temperatures near freezing in sudden weather changes, even in July.

**August (peak season)**:
- Similar to July but with increasing thunderstorm frequency late in the month.
- Temperature ranges: valleys 24-32C (75-90F), summits 8-16C (46-61F).
- Tourist season at its peak -- more traffic on the roads, busier accommodation.

**September (late season)**:
- Cooler temperatures. Valleys 18-26C (64-79F). Summits 5-12C (41-54F).
- Shorter days. Weather more variable -- clear spells but also the first autumn fronts.
- Some years, early snow can dust the highest passes (Tourmalet, Pailheres) by late September.
- The 100-hour window closes September 30, so late-September riders have no margin for weather delays.

### Key Climate Risks
- **Altitude exposure**: The Tourmalet (2,115 m) and Pailheres (2,001 m) are fully exposed above the tree line. Wind, cold, and sudden weather changes are serious risks. Riders must carry warm layers for descents from these summits.
- **Thunderstorms**: Pyrenean thunderstorms are intense -- heavy rain, hail, lightning, dramatic temperature drops. They typically build in the afternoon. An early start each day helps avoid the worst.
- **Heat**: The Basque foothills (Day 1) and the final Mediterranean descent (Day 5) can be extremely hot in July/August. The road from Prades to Cerbere bakes in Mediterranean sun.
- **Rain shadow effect**: The French (north) side of the Pyrenees receives more rain than the Spanish (south) side. The central and eastern sections tend to be drier than the western Basque sections.
- **Average rainfall**: June averages 43 mm with 19 dry days; July averages 73 mm with 20 dry days (mountain station data).

### What to Pack
- Arm warmers, leg warmers, and a windproof gilet are essential for summit descents, even in high summer.
- Full waterproof jacket recommended. Rain can last for hours in the mountains.
- Sun protection critical, especially at altitude and on the Mediterranean final day.

## Community & Culture

### Atmosphere
The Raid Pyreneen occupies a unique position in European cycling culture. It is NOT a race, NOT a sportive with a start gun and timing chips -- it is a personal challenge with a century of tradition. The atmosphere is one of individual accomplishment against the mountains, wrapped in the camaraderie of small groups tackling the same route. There are no crowds, no barriers, no PA systems. The "race village" is whatever cafe is serving as the next control point stamp on your carnet.

The format -- riding at your own pace, on your own dates, getting your carnet stamped at village cafes -- gives it a deeply personal, almost pilgrimage-like quality. Completing the Raid feels like joining a long lineage of riders stretching back to 1950.

### International Participation
- 16+ nationalities represented annually
- 90% of participants are non-French, making this one of the most internationally-skewed cycling events in France
- British riders account for the largest foreign contingent by far (the Raid is extremely well-known in British cycling culture)
- Tour operators based in the UK (Marmot Tours, Bike-Alive, Sportive Breaks, Pyrenees Cycling, Stuart Hall Cycling, Col Experience Tours) dominate the commercial landscape
- Marmot Tours alone supports approximately 200 riders per year through the Raid

### Media Coverage
- Limited traditional media coverage (no TV, no live timing, no professional field)
- Extensive coverage in British cycling media and blogs (Broleur, CTC/Cycling UK, various club magazines)
- Active Strava community -- CCB maintains an official Strava Club and Strava Raid group
- RideWithGPS has the official Randonneur route mapped
- Wikipedia entry exists in English
- Word-of-mouth and cycling club culture are the primary awareness drivers

### Cultural Context
- The Raid is intertwined with Tour de France mythology. Riders climb the same cols that legends like Coppi, Anquetil, Merckx, Hinault, and Indurain conquered in the Tour. The Tourmalet, Aubisque, Aspin, and Peyresourde are sacred ground in cycling history.
- The Basque sections (Day 1) pass through a region with its own distinct language, architecture, and culinary traditions. Basque cycling culture is vibrant (the Basque Country has produced numerous professional riders and sponsors).
- Bagneres-de-Luchon (overnight stop on Day 3) has been a thermal spa town since Roman times and is known as the "Queen of the Pyrenees." It has hosted more Tour de France stage finishes than almost any other town.
- Local cuisine is excellent throughout: Basque pintxos and wines on Day 1, mountain stews and duck confit in the central stages, Catalan-influenced dishes on the final day.
- The certificate and medal from the Cyclo Club Bearnais carry genuine prestige in cycling circles -- they are not participation trophies but recognition of completing one of the great cycling challenges.

### Comparison to Similar Events
- **Raid Alpine (Lake Geneva to Nice)**: The alpine equivalent, slightly longer (~720 km, 16,000 m). Less established historically (created 1990s vs 1950 for the Pyreneen).
- **Haute Route Alps/Pyrenees**: Multi-stage timed events with mass starts, rankings, and higher entry fees. More "race" than "randonnee."
- **LEtape du Tour**: Single-stage sportive on a Tour de France route. Mass participation. Much shorter commitment.
- **Trans Pyrenees Race**: Unsupported bikepacking race across the Pyrenees. Different ethos (racing vs personal challenge).

## Scoring Dimension Notes

For scoring against the 14-dimension system:

- **distance**: 720 km over 4.5 days. Extremely long total, but daily stages are 90-180 km. Multi-day format means daily distances are manageable for fit cyclists. Score context: one of the longest organized cycling challenges in Europe.
- **climbing**: 11,000 m total across 18 cols including 3 HC climbs (Tourmalet 2,115m, Aubisque 1,709m, Pailheres 2,001m). Two consecutive days of 3,000+ m climbing. This is among the most climbing-intensive multi-day events on the calendar.
- **descent_technicality**: Descents are on well-paved roads but some are extremely long and technical (Tourmalet descent is 17-19 km, Aubisque descent is steep and narrow). The Portet d'Aspet descent is notorious (Casartelli fatality). Open roads with traffic. Not technical in an off-road sense but dangerous at speed.
- **road_surface**: 100% paved tarmac. Generally excellent quality on major cols (these are major tourist roads). Some rougher sections on minor cols and valley roads.
- **climate_risk**: High. Mountain passes above 2,000 m, 4.5 days of exposure, thunderstorm risk in summer, dramatic temperature swings (Mediterranean heat to alpine cold within hours). Multiple days means multiple weather systems possible.
- **altitude**: Maximum 2,115 m (Tourmalet). Second highest point 2,001 m (Pailheres). Most riding between 500-1,700 m. Not extreme altitude but high enough for weather exposure.
- **logistics**: Complex. Multi-day event requiring accommodation pre-booking, bike transport, airport transfers at both ends (different cities/countries). Self-supported riders face significant planning burden. Tour operators simplify this greatly.
- **prestige**: Very high. One of the oldest and most recognized multi-day cycling challenges in Europe. 75+ years of history. Directly connected to Tour de France mythology. The CCB medal carries real weight in cycling circles.
- **organization**: Minimal official infrastructure (it's a randonnee, not a race). The CCB provides the carnet and control point system, but there are no feed zones, marshals, or road closures. Tour operators provide the actual support infrastructure.
- **scenic_experience**: Exceptional. The route traverses the entire Pyrenean range through five distinct landscape zones: Basque green hills, high alpine massifs, central Pyrenean valleys, eastern Mediterranean garrigue, and the coast. Multiple world-famous cycling vistas (Tourmalet summit, Aubisque corniche, etc.).
- **community_culture**: Strong international community centered on British cycling culture. The shared challenge creates bonds, but it lacks the concentrated mass-start festival atmosphere of a traditional sportive. The "pilgrimage" quality and historical lineage add depth.
- **field_depth**: ~300 finishers/year, spread across 4 months. No competitive element, no rankings, no age groups. Participants are overwhelmingly well-prepared amateurs (the event self-selects for fitness). Not a deep competitive field because it's not a competition.
- **value**: The official CCB registration (EUR 11-19) is extraordinary value. But the realistic cost including accommodation, food, and transport is EUR 730-1,500+ with a tour operator, or potentially less self-supported. For 720 km and 11,000 m of climbing across 5 days of iconic terrain, the value proposition is strong.
- **expenses**: Total trip cost: EUR 1,500-2,500+ including flights, accommodation (5-6 nights), meals, and tour operator fees. Self-supported could be done for less (EUR 500-800 with budget accommodation and self-catering). The official entry fee is negligible.
