# Research Dump: Marmotte Granfondo Pyrenees

## Quick Facts
- **Granfondo**: 177km / 110mi, 5,700m / 18,700ft climbing, 5 major passes
- **Defi du Tourmalet**: 122km / 76mi, 4,200m / 13,780ft climbing, 3 passes
- **Rando**: 68km / 42mi, 1,500m / 4,920ft climbing (leisure option)
- Start/Finish: Argeles-Gazost (start) / Hautacam summit finish (2024 route onward)
- Hautes-Pyrenees department, Occitanie region, France
- Late August (typically 4th Sunday); 2024 edition was scheduled Aug 25 but cancelled <2 weeks before event
- Field size: ~500 riders at inaugural 2016 edition; grew to estimated 1,500-2,500 in subsequent years (much smaller than the Alps sibling's 7,500 cap)
- Founded: 2016 (inaugural edition August 29, 2016)
- Entry fee: EUR 90-100 tiered (Granfondo), EUR 80-90 tiered (Defi du Tourmalet). First registrants get lowest price.
- Start format: Mass start at 7:30am (Granfondo), wave starts for shorter routes; timing chip on handlebars
- Organized by Sport Communication, part of Grand Trophee Cycling Classics France circuit
- Title sponsor: LOOK (bicycle manufacturer)
- Part of the Marmotte Granfondo Series (Alps since 1982, Pyrenees since 2016, Valais/Switzerland)
- **Status note**: 2024 edition cancelled with <2 weeks notice; 2026 edition also listed as "CANCELLED" on official site. Event future uncertain as of Feb 2026.

## Course Profile

### Granfondo Route (177km / 5,700m D+)
The Granfondo is a relentless mountain marathon through the heart of the Hautes-Pyrenees. There is almost no flat terrain on the entire course. The route tackles five categorized climbs, including a brutal double ascent of the Col du Tourmalet from both sides, making this arguably tougher than the original Marmotte Alps.

The route begins in Argeles-Gazost (elevation ~460m) and immediately heads south toward Luz-Saint-Sauveur before ascending the Tourmalet from the west. After descending the east side toward Sainte-Marie-de-Campan, riders climb the Hourquette d'Ancizan, descend to the Aure valley, then tackle the Col d'Aspin from Arreau. A long descent back to Sainte-Marie-de-Campan sets up the second Tourmalet ascent from the east (the harder side). After descending the west side back through Luz-Saint-Sauveur toward Argeles-Gazost, the route finishes with the HC summit finish at Hautacam.

### The 5 Passes (Granfondo)

1. **Col du Tourmalet — West Side** (2,115m)
   - From Luz-Saint-Sauveur: 18.0km, 1,404m gain, 7.4% avg, 13% max
   - HC classification. Highest paved road in the Pyrenees.
   - The most-used climb in Tour de France history (87+ appearances).
   - Starts gently through Bareges, ramps up significantly in the final 4km.
   - Includes pitches that are almost flat alternating with >13% sections.
   - Riders hit this fresh at the start of the day — the "easy" Tourmalet.

2. **Hourquette d'Ancizan** (1,564m)
   - From the east (via Tourmalet descent): ~22km overall approach, 4% avg with spikes to 12% max
   - Category 1. The "recovery" col — relatively easier but deceptive.
   - First 7km shared with Col d'Aspin route from Campan through Payolle.
   - Final 10km on narrow forest roads, feels wild and remote.
   - Sharp steep sections in the first kilometer (up to 15% from Ancizan side approach).

3. **Col d'Aspin** (1,489m)
   - From Arreau (east side): 12.0km, 783m gain, 6.5% avg, 9.5% max
   - Category 1. Beautiful open meadow views near the summit.
   - Starts gently at 4% for the first 2km, progressively steepens.
   - Mid-climb sections reach 8-9%. Consistent effort climb.

4. **Col du Tourmalet — East Side** (2,115m)
   - From Sainte-Marie-de-Campan: 17.2km, 1,268m gain, 7.4% avg, 12% max
   - HC classification. Generally considered the harder side.
   - Passes through La Mongie ski station before the exposed final kilometers.
   - The "cruel" second ascent — riders have already climbed 100+ km and 4,000+ meters.
   - Final 3km average 10% with pitches at 11%. Mental and physical crux of the event.
   - Cyclist magazine: "spent 30 minutes considering quitting" on this ascent.

5. **Hautacam / Col de Tramassel** (1,635m)
   - From Argeles-Gazost: 13.7km, 1,170m gain, 7.8% avg, 12.8% max (500m segment)
   - HC classification. Summit finish.
   - Irregular gradient with a brutal mid-climb section: ~11% for 2km around halfway.
   - After 4 prior climbs and 160+ km, this is "one hell of a climb to the finish."
   - Six Tour de France summit finishes: Leblanc (1994), Riis (1996), Otxoa (2000), Cobo (2008), Nibali (2014), Vingegaard (2022).

### Defi du Tourmalet Route (122km / 4,200m D+)
Shorter route that still includes the double Tourmalet (both sides) and the Hautacam summit finish, but skips the Hourquette d'Ancizan and Col d'Aspin. Three feed stations. Same start time, same brutal finish.

### Rando Route (68km / 1,500m D+)
Leisure format starting at 8:30am. Designed for cyclists who want to discover the Pyrenees without the extreme challenge. One feed station plus pasta party at finish. No competitive pressure.

### Road Surface
All routes are on paved public roads. The Pyrenean roads are generally well-maintained, with recently resurfaced tarmac on the Tourmalet and Hautacam due to frequent Tour de France use. Some secondary roads (Hourquette d'Ancizan forest section) are narrower with older pavement. Expect occasional rough patches, cattle grid crossings, and loose gravel on road edges in alpine sections. Overall surface quality: good to very good.

### Descent Technicality
Descents are steep and fast, with tight hairpin sequences on the Tourmalet (both sides) and technical corners on the Col d'Aspin descent. The Tourmalet east-side descent features narrow tunnels and exposed sections. Descending Hautacam is not required (summit finish). Overall technicality is moderate-to-high; riders need confidence on steep, winding descents at speed.

## Logistics

### Nearest Airports
1. **Tarbes-Lourdes-Pyrenees (TLP/LDE)**: 28km from Argeles-Gazost, ~30 min drive. Small regional airport with limited seasonal flights (mainly from UK, Belgium). Ryanair and seasonal charters. Best option if flights align.
2. **Pau Pyrenees Airport (PUF)**: 78km from Argeles-Gazost, ~1hr drive. Better flight selection, connections from Paris CDG/Orly.
3. **Toulouse-Blagnac (TLS)**: 195km from Argeles-Gazost, ~2hr drive. Major international hub with extensive European connections. Best for international arrivals. Car rental widely available.

### Lodging Strategy
- **Argeles-Gazost** (start town): Small Pyrenean spa town. Hotels, gites, campgrounds. Limited capacity — book 3-6 months ahead. Le HOUNTA campsite offers bike-specific amenities (secure garage, repair kit, wash station).
- **Lourdes** (15 min north): Major pilgrimage city with extensive hotel infrastructure — 200+ hotels. Best availability and price range. Regular shuttle service to event.
- **Luz-Saint-Sauveur** (25 min south): Charming mountain village near Tourmalet base. Smaller selection but atmospheric.
- **Tour operator packages**: SportActive, Le Domestique Tours, and others offer full-service packages including accommodation, bike rental, and guided training rides. Expect EUR 800-2,500 for multi-day packages.
- Local tourism office: Vallees de Gavarnie (valleesdegavarnie.com) coordinates event lodging.

### Race-Day Transport
- Start and finish are geographically close (Argeles-Gazost → Hautacam). The 2024 route redesign solved the logistics problem of earlier editions that required a 20km ride back post-finish.
- Hautacam's base is near Argeles-Gazost, so post-race return is straightforward.
- Roads are closed to traffic on race day.
- Bike transport and mechanical support on course.

### Feed Zones
- **Granfondo**: 4 feed stations along the 177km route
  - Tourmalet summit (km ~36): First refueling after the big opening climb
  - Post-Hourquette d'Ancizan / Aspin area (~km 80-90): Mid-race fueling
  - Second Tourmalet summit (~km 130): Critical refueling before final push
  - Beaucens area (~km 155-160): Pre-Hautacam staging
- **Defi du Tourmalet**: 3 feed stations (at Tourmalet km 36, Tourmalet km 70, Beaucens km 105)
- **Rando**: 1 feed station plus pasta party at finish
- Finish area: Pasta party, medal ceremony

## History

### Origin Story
La Marmotte (Alps) was founded in 1982 by Yann Contat, starting from Bourg d'Oisans with a route over Col de la Croix de Fer, Col du Telegraphe, and Col du Galibier before the iconic finish at Alpe d'Huez. It became one of Europe's most prestigious cyclosportives — "La Doyenne" (the old lady) — growing from a modest local event to 7,500+ international riders.

In 2015, the Marmotte series expanded to Italy (Marmotte Granfondo Dolomites). In 2016, the organizers at Sport Communication launched the Marmotte Granfondo Pyrenees as the third edition of the series, offering a Pyrenean counterpart to the Alpine original.

### Inaugural 2016 Edition
- August 29, 2016: 500 riders tackled 163km with 5,600m of climbing
- Route: Six legendary cols including a double ascent of the Tourmalet, plus Hourquette d'Ancizan, La Mongie, and a summit finish at Luz-Ardiden (1,666m)
- Only half the field completed the course — a testament to the extreme difficulty
- Cyclist magazine described it as "arguably tougher than its Alpine sibling"

### Route Evolution
- **2016-2022**: Start varied between Luz-Saint-Sauveur and Argeles-Gazost; finish at Luz-Ardiden
- **2023**: Route from Argeles-Gazost, 163km/5,600m D+, finish at Luz-Ardiden
- **2024 redesign**: Extended to 177km/5,700m with Hautacam summit finish. Start and finish brought closer together to solve the 20km post-race ride-back problem. Route described as "highly improved" by Cycloworld.
- **2024**: Cancelled less than 2 weeks before event. Full refunds issued. Reasons not officially disclosed.
- **2025**: Route listed as Luz-Saint-Sauveur start / Luz-Ardiden finish, 150km/5,400m (a shorter variant)
- **2026**: Listed as "CANCELLED" on official website

### The Marmotte Series
1. **Marmotte Granfondo Alpes** (est. 1982): Bourg d'Oisans → Alpe d'Huez. 174km/5,180m. 7,500 riders. Late June. The flagship.
2. **Marmotte Granfondo Pyrenees** (est. 2016): Argeles-Gazost → Hautacam. 177km/5,700m. ~1,500-2,500 riders. Late August. The "monster" sibling.
3. **Marmotte Granfondo Valais** (Switzerland): Part of Tour des Stations. Added to the series.

## Climate (Late August, Hautes-Pyrenees)

### Temperature
- Valley floor (Argeles-Gazost, ~460m): 20-28C daytime, 14-18C early morning
- Tourmalet summit (2,115m): 8-18C, can drop below 10C in rain/cloud. Expect 8-15C temperature differential from valley.
- Hautacam (1,635m): 12-22C. Mid-altitude, slightly warmer than Tourmalet summit.
- Dawn start (7:30am) can be in single digits at altitude.

### Precipitation
- Atlantic weather systems dominate the western Pyrenees — the lush green meadows are testament to frequent rain.
- Afternoon thunderstorms are common in August. Risk estimated at 30-45%.
- Conditions can change rapidly: "scorching sunshine at the bottom and rain at the top" is typical.
- Fog and mist frequent at Tourmalet summit.

### Gear Implications
- Mandatory: windproof jacket, arm warmers for descents
- Recommended: gilet, full-finger gloves for Tourmalet descent, rain jacket
- Sun protection essential for exposed alpine sections
- Climate risk rating is moderate-to-high due to Atlantic weather influence and altitude exposure

## Community & Culture

### Atmosphere
- The Pyrenees edition has a more intimate feel than the massive Alps event. Smaller field creates a less congested, more personal experience.
- Strong Tour de France heritage permeates every climb — riders follow the exact roads of cycling's greatest legends.
- The double Tourmalet is the defining narrative: a punishing, character-building challenge that separates the Pyrenees edition from other sportives.
- The 50% DNF rate at the inaugural edition highlights the event's reputation for extreme difficulty.

### International Participation
- Draws participants from across Europe: France, UK, Belgium, Netherlands, Spain (close proximity to Spanish border).
- Spanish riders from Basque Country and Catalonia contribute given proximity.
- Smaller international footprint than the Alps edition, which draws from 40+ countries.
- English-language website and registration system.

### Local Culture
- Hautes-Pyrenees is deeply connected to cycling culture. The Tour de France has used these roads since 1910 (Tourmalet's first appearance).
- Argeles-Gazost sits at the crossroads of 8 famous Tour de France cols.
- Lourdes (15 min away) is one of the world's most-visited pilgrimage sites — significant tourism infrastructure.
- Pyrenean mountain culture, pastoral traditions, thermal spa heritage.
- The "marmotte" (alpine marmot) is ubiquitous in the Pyrenees — the event's namesake animal.

### Media Coverage
- Coverage from GranFondo Guide, Cycloworld, Cyclist magazine, Sportive.com, Alpine Cols.
- Cyclist magazine provided in-depth ride report calling it a "second helping" of Marmotte difficulty.
- GranFondo.com described the Pyrenees addition as a "monster" joining the series.
- Less media saturation than the Alps edition, which receives extensive coverage from Rouleur, Cycling Weekly, GCN, etc.

## Scoring Dimensions Notes

### Distance (177km Granfondo)
Long-distance mountain sportive. 177km with essentially no flat terrain. Comparable to the Alps Marmotte (174km). Riding time: 7-12+ hours for most participants.

### Climbing (5,700m Granfondo)
Among the highest climbing totals of any single-day cycling event in the world. Five HC/Cat 1 climbs. The double Tourmalet adds ~2,670m of the total on its own. More climbing than the Alps Marmotte (5,180m).

### Descent Technicality
Steep, winding descents on narrow Pyrenean roads. Tourmalet descent from 2,115m is technical with hairpins and tunnels. Col d'Aspin descent is fast. Summit finish at Hautacam means no final descent required.

### Road Surface
Good to very good paved roads. Tour de France route maintenance keeps main climbs in strong condition. Some narrower secondary roads on Hourquette d'Ancizan.

### Climate Risk
Moderate-to-high. Atlantic weather systems bring unpredictable conditions. Afternoon thunderstorms common. 8-15C temperature swings between valley and summit. Fog at Tourmalet summit is frequent.

### Altitude
Maximum elevation 2,115m (Tourmalet). Significant time above 1,500m. No extreme altitude effects but cold and wind exposure at summit.

### Logistics
Remote Pyrenean location. Nearest major airport 2 hours away (Toulouse). Limited regional flight options (Tarbes-Lourdes). Accommodation scarce in start town but Lourdes has extensive capacity. Post-2024 route redesign improved start/finish logistics. Event cancellation history (2024, 2026) is a logistics concern.

### Prestige
Part of the Marmotte brand — one of cycling's most respected sportive families. The Tourmalet is arguably the most iconic climb in cycling. However, the Pyrenees edition is much younger (2016) and less established than the Alps original (1982). Cancellations have damaged prestige.

### Organization
Organized by Sport Communication, experienced operator of the Marmotte series. However, the 2024 cancellation with <2 weeks notice is a significant organizational concern. Timing chip system, feed stations, mechanical support on course. Roads closed to traffic.

### Scenic Experience
Spectacular Pyrenean mountain scenery. Tour de France cols with legendary hairpin sequences. Pastoral valleys, mountain meadows, alpine peaks. The Tourmalet panorama is world-class. Hautacam provides sweeping views of the Argeles valley.

### Community & Culture
Intimate field size creates strong community feel. Deep Tour de France heritage. Pyrenean mountain culture. Less established community than the 40-year-old Alps event.

### Field Depth
Smaller field (estimated 1,500-2,500) means less depth than major events. Attracts serious mountain cyclists. Less competitive depth than Alps edition or Italian gran fondos.

### Value
EUR 90-100 entry fee is very reasonable for a multi-pass mountain sportive with full support. Feed stations, timing, finisher medal, pasta party included. Comparable events charge EUR 100-180+.

### Expenses
Travel to remote Pyrenees location adds cost. Flights to Toulouse + car rental, or limited flights to Tarbes-Lourdes. Accommodation ranges from EUR 40/night (camping) to EUR 150+/night (hotels). Tour packages EUR 800-2,500. Total budget: EUR 400-800 for self-organized, EUR 1,200-3,000 with tour operator.
