# Research Dump: Velo Birmingham (UK Sportive)

**STATUS: DISCONTINUED** — The event has not been held since 2019. The 2020 edition was cancelled due to COVID-19 with no refunds issued. The organiser (ASE, formerly CSM Active) confirmed it was "almost certain" there would be no future editions. The velobirmingham.com domain has since been repurposed as a chamois cream brand (VeloSkin). As of February 2026, the event is definitively defunct.

## Quick Facts
- **Distance**: 100 miles / 161km (flagship); 45 miles / 72km (added for 2020 edition, never run)
- **Elevation**: ~1,300m / 4,290ft (official); ~2,175m / 7,140ft (Strava/GPS-recorded — significant discrepancy)
- **Start/Finish**: Birmingham City Centre (Centenary Square from 2020; original start near Baskerville House/Broad Street)
- **Date**: September 24, 2017 (inaugural); May 12, 2019 (second edition); June 21, 2020 (cancelled)
- **Field size**: 15,000 capacity (2017, sold out in 3 days); 17,000 (2019); 18,000 registered (2020, never held)
- **Finishers**: ~11,453 (2017 edition, per MYLAPS Sporthive results)
- **Founded**: 2017 by CSM Active (later renamed ASE — Active Sport Entertainment Limited), directed by Richard Relton
- **Entry fee**: General Entry GBP 75 + GBP 3.75 booking fee; Fast Track GBP 115 + GBP 3.75 booking fee (2019 prices). Charity entry via Marie Curie: GBP 40 registration + GBP 400 minimum fundraising
- **Start format**: Wave start from 7:00 AM, staggered over ~2 hours. Fast Track ticket holders guaranteed first three waves. Approx 17 waves total.
- **Route options**: Single 100-mile route (2017, 2019). A 45-mile Birmingham-to-Coventry option was announced for 2020 but never run.
- **Discipline**: Closed-road sportive (mass participation, non-competitive but chip-timed)
- **Course closure**: Specific cutoff times at each feed station; overall course closure by late afternoon

## Course Profile

### Overall Character
A rolling 100-mile loop through the West Midlands countryside on fully closed public roads. The terrain is predominantly gentle — this is not a mountainous sportive. The West Midlands is largely low-lying, with the route threading through pastoral farmland, small market towns, and brief urban passages. The climbing is distributed across multiple short, punchy ascents rather than sustained alpine-style efforts.

### 2017 Route (Inaugural)
Started in Birmingham city centre, heading west through Worcestershire, Herefordshire, Staffordshire, Sandwell, and Dudley before looping back to Birmingham. Six classified climbs — two Cat 3, one Cat 4, one Cat 5, plus two unclassified. Four of the six climbs came in the first half. The route was modified before the event due to fierce local opposition in Worcestershire (shortened by 20 miles in that county, Herefordshire section removed entirely, replaced with additional Staffordshire mileage).

### 2019 Route (Revised)
Completely redesigned. Instead of heading west, the route went east through Solihull, Warwickshire, and into Coventry at the halfway point. Notably flatter than the 2017 edition with smaller rolling hills. Highlights included:
- Passage through the closed streets of Coventry, including cobbled roads around the historic Coventry Cathedral
- Packwood House (National Trust estate)
- Georgian streets of Edgbaston
- Bartley Green Reservoir
- Villages of Balsall Common, Berkswell, Cheswick Green, Hurley, Fillongley

### Key Climbs (2017 Route)
1. **St Kenelm's Pass (Clent Hills)** — Cat 3. 2.0km, 105m gain, 5.2% average (5.8% per VeloViewer), max ~10%. Located at mile ~85, this was the sting in the tail — a tough final climb on tired legs. The broader approach from Hampton Lovett is a 14-mile gradual drag gaining 216m/710ft before the sharp kick at St Kenelm's.
2. **Romsley Hill** — Cat 4. Approximately 1.5km, moderate gradients. Near Halesowen, climbed in the final 15 miles.
3. **Clent Climb** — Cat 5. Short, sharp ascent in the Clent Hills area.
4. **Unnamed rolling climbs** — Several 300-700ft undulations with 1-5% average gradients scattered through Worcestershire and Staffordshire.

### Road Surface
Fully paved UK A-roads and B-roads. Surface quality varies — generally good on main roads, occasional patching and minor potholes on rural lanes. All roads were closed to motor traffic, removing the usual UK hazard of narrow roads shared with cars. No off-road or gravel sections.

### Technical Features
- Closed roads enabled high-speed cornering with confidence (no oncoming traffic)
- Cobbled sections through Coventry (2019 route) — brief, aesthetic rather than punishing
- Urban sections through Birmingham at start/finish required navigation of street furniture and roundabouts
- No significant technical descents — the terrain is too gentle for anything demanding

## Logistics

### Airports
- **Birmingham Airport (BHX)**: 8 miles / 13km east of Birmingham city centre. 20-minute train ride on the free AirRail Link monorail to Birmingham International station, then onward to New Street. The most convenient option.
- **East Midlands Airport (EMA)**: 40 miles / 64km northeast. Budget airline hub (Ryanair). ~1 hour by car.
- **London Heathrow (LHR)**: 110 miles / 177km south. 1h20 by train (Chiltern Railways or Avanti West Coast via London). International gateway.
- **Manchester Airport (MAN)**: 85 miles / 137km north. 1h30 by train.

### Accommodation
- **City Centre**: Abundant options. Birmingham has a large hotel market due to its conference/NEC trade. Budget (Travelodge, ibis) from GBP 50-70/night. Mid-range (Holiday Inn, Jurys Inn) GBP 80-130. Premium (Hyatt Regency, The Grand Hotel) GBP 150-250. Centenary Square start line puts most city centre hotels within walking distance.
- **NEC/Airport area**: Hilton Birmingham Metropole, Crowne Plaza, Moxy, Genting Hotel. Useful if flying in the day before.
- **Strategy**: Book city centre to minimize race-morning logistics. Birmingham has excellent public transport (tram, train, bus) if staying slightly further out. No rural accommodation challenge — this is a major UK city.

### Race-Day Transport
- Wave starts from 7:00 AM meant early wake-up. City centre start was walkable from most hotels.
- Bike assembly at the start village (typically open from 5:00 AM).
- Closed roads meant no vehicle access to course — no drive-to-feed-zone option.
- Post-race finish festival in the city centre with food, drink, and entertainment.

### Feed Zones (2017 Route)
Three official feed stations ("Pit Stops"):
1. **Pit Stop 1**: ~30 miles (Fillongley) — water, energy bars, bananas
2. **Pit Stop 2**: ~65 miles (Kingswood) — full food stop, water, energy products
3. **Pit Stop 3**: ~85 miles (Rubery) — final top-up before the closing climbs

Feed stations were criticized in 2017 for being understocked and poorly located, causing queuing on the course. Improved for 2019 with four pit stops and better provisioning. Local residents also informally handed out drinks, sweets, and biscuits along the route.

## History

### Origin (2015-2017)
Conceived by CSM Active (later ASE) as the UK's answer to European-style closed-road sportives. Birmingham lacked a major cycling event despite being the UK's second-largest city. The event was officially launched at the NEC Cycle Show in September 2015, partnering with Birmingham City Council and the Birmingham BikeFest (a free community cycling festival). The vision: bring 15,000 cyclists onto 100 miles of closed roads through the West Midlands — unprecedented for the region.

### Inaugural Edition (September 24, 2017)
- Sold out 15,000 places in three days
- 11,453 finishers recorded
- Weather was ideal: cool, dry, sunny
- Atmosphere praised unanimously — villages through Worcestershire, Staffordshire, and Herefordshire lined with cheering residents, cowbells, vuvuzelas, flags
- **Sabotage incidents**: Local opponents of the road closures scattered nails and tacks on the route (notably in Bobbington and Swindon villages), causing punctures and a delayed start. Police investigated.
- **Organizational issues**: Disjointed start waves with poor communication; feed stations understocked and poorly positioned (long queues); 40-minute wait at finish arena entrance; some marshalling gaps
- Despite problems, the riding experience on empty closed roads was widely praised. Multiple participant reviews called it "the best fun I've ever had on a bike."

### Local Opposition and Route Controversy
The event generated significant backlash from residents and businesses along the route:
- Road closures (up to 8 hours for some villages) prevented residents from driving, businesses from operating
- Councillors in Worcestershire publicly opposed the event
- Route was altered before the 2017 event, removing 20 miles from Worcestershire and skipping Herefordshire
- The opposition was fierce enough that there was **no 2018 edition** — the event was effectively barred from returning on its original timeline

### Name Change and 2019 Return
- Rebranded as "Velo Birmingham & Midlands" to reflect the broader regional route
- Completely new route heading east (Coventry direction) rather than west (Worcestershire) — partly to avoid communities that had opposed the 2017 event
- 17,000 capacity, sold out
- May 12, 2019 (moved from September to May)
- Flatter profile, passage through Coventry with cobbled streets around the Cathedral
- Better organized than 2017: improved feed stations, smoother starts
- Strong community support along the new route

### Velo South Debacle (2018)
While Velo Birmingham was on hiatus, CSM Active launched "Velo South" — a 100-mile closed-road sportive in the south of England for September 2018. It was cancelled at the last minute due to Storm Bronagh. Full refunds were issued (weather cancellation was covered by insurance). This event also faced fierce local opposition before its cancellation.

### 2020 Cancellation and Collapse
- 2020 edition announced for June 21, with 18,000 registered riders and a new 45-mile shorter route option
- Cancelled in March 2020 due to COVID-19
- **No refunds issued** — organisers stated refunds were "simply not going to be possible" as event cancellation insurance did not cover pandemics. With minimum entry fee of GBP 79, the estimated GBP 1.4 million in entry fees was retained.
- ASE disclosed both previous editions had lost money, and the 2020 event would have only broken even in the best case
- Company financials revealed an operating loss of GBP 1.1 million in 2018 on sales of GBP 2.4 million, with net liabilities of GBP 2.4 million by year end
- A Change.org petition demanding refunds gathered hundreds of signatures
- ASE confirmed it was "almost certain" there would be no future Velo Birmingham & Midlands events
- The event has not returned. The velobirmingham.com domain is now used by an unrelated chamois cream brand.

### Legacy
Velo Birmingham demonstrated both the appetite for and the logistical challenges of closed-road sportives in the UK. It briefly proved that 15,000+ riders could enjoy traffic-free roads through the English Midlands, but local opposition, organizational instability, and financial mismanagement ended the experiment after just two editions actually held.

## Climate

### 2017 Edition (Late September)
- Average high: 18.2C / 65F; low: 9.5C / 49F
- Precipitation: 55mm over 9 rain days in September
- Actual race day: Cool, dry, and sunny — ideal conditions
- 4.5 hours average sunshine per day in September
- Wind: moderate, typically 14-15 mph average

### 2019 Edition (Mid-May)
- Average high: 16.4C / 62F; low: 6.9C / 45F
- Precipitation: 50mm over 9 rain days in May
- Day length: 15 hours 50 minutes — very long days
- Wind: 14.8 mph average
- Conditions generally favorable but layers recommended; mornings at 7:00 AM start can be chilly (7-9C / 45-48F)

### General Assessment
Birmingham has a temperate maritime climate — mild but unpredictable. Rain is always possible regardless of month. May and September both offer reasonable conditions for cycling, with May having the edge on daylight hours and September being slightly warmer. True extremes (heatwaves, severe storms) are rare. Wind is a consistent factor on exposed sections.

## Community & Culture

### Atmosphere
The event's greatest strength was the community response along the route. Residents of towns and villages lined the streets, creating a festival atmosphere:
- Cowbells, vuvuzelas, bugles, flags, banners
- Local pubs and churches set up informal cheer stations
- Children with hand-made signs
- The passage through Coventry (2019) was particularly atmospheric, with crowds on the closed city streets
- Described by multiple participants as comparable to watching a stage of the Tour de France pass through

### Charity Dimension
Marie Curie was a major charity partner. Charity runners/riders were a significant portion of the field, giving the event a strong fundraising dimension. Marie Curie offered dedicated support packages with training guides, branded cycling tops, and fundraising packs.

### International Participation
Primarily a domestic UK event. International participation was minimal — this was not positioned as a destination sportive. The vast majority of riders were from the West Midlands, wider Midlands region, and other parts of England. Some participants from Wales, Scotland, and Ireland.

### Media Coverage
- Regional media coverage (BBC Midlands, Express & Star, Birmingham Mail, Coventry Telegraph) was extensive
- National cycling press (road.cc, Cycling Weekly, Cyclist magazine) covered the event
- Significant negative coverage around the 2020 refund controversy and local opposition/sabotage incidents
- No international media presence
- No professional racing component — purely mass participation

### Inclusivity
The event was explicitly positioned as all-abilities. Wave-start format and generous cutoff times allowed slower riders to participate. The 2020 addition of a 45-mile option (never run) was designed to lower the barrier further. Birmingham BikeFest ran concurrently as a free family cycling festival.

## Scoring Dimension Notes

- **Distance**: 100mi/161km flagship puts it in the classic gran fondo range. Score: moderate-high.
- **Climbing**: 1,300-2,175m depending on measurement. Gentle by sportive standards. No sustained climbs. Score: low-moderate.
- **Descent technicality**: Essentially zero. Rolling terrain, no mountain descents. Score: very low.
- **Road surface**: Fully paved UK roads. Reasonable quality but not pristine. Score: moderate.
- **Climate risk**: Maritime UK — always a rain risk. No extreme heat/cold. Score: moderate.
- **Altitude**: Maximum elevation ~300m / 1,000ft. Effectively sea-level riding. Score: very low.
- **Logistics**: Major UK city, excellent transport links, abundant accommodation. Score: high.
- **Prestige**: Brief run (2 editions held), no professional element, no UCI connection, significant controversy. Score: low.
- **Organization**: Mixed — good concept, poor execution in 2017, improved in 2019, catastrophic 2020 refund handling. Score: low.
- **Scenic experience**: Pleasant English countryside but not spectacular. Industrial/suburban sections through Birmingham, Sandwell, Dudley. Score: moderate.
- **Community/culture**: The roadside atmosphere was genuinely exceptional. Charity fundraising dimension adds value. Score: moderate-high.
- **Field depth**: Mass participation, no competitive element, no elite field. Score: low.
- **Value**: GBP 75-115 for a 100-mile closed-road sportive is reasonable by UK standards, but the no-refund scandal destroyed value perception. Score: low-moderate (historically).
- **Expenses**: Birmingham is affordable by UK standards. No travel premium. Score: moderate-high.

**NOTE**: As a discontinued event, scoring is historical only. This event cannot be listed as active.
