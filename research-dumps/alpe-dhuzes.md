# Research Dump: Alpe d'HuZes

## Quick Facts
- **Format**: Charity hillclimb -- participants cycle, run, or walk up Alpe d'Huez up to 6 times in a single day
- **Single ascent**: 13.8km / 8.6mi, 1,120m / 3,675ft elevation gain, 21 legendary hairpin bends
- **Maximum day total**: 6 ascents = ~84km / 52mi, ~6,700m / 21,980ft elevation gain
- **Start**: Bourg d'Oisans (717m / 2,352ft), Isere, Auvergne-Rhone-Alpes, France
- **Finish**: Alpe d'Huez resort (1,850m / 6,070ft)
- **Date**: First Thursday of June (2026: Thursday 4 June, race day 04:30-20:00). Event week runs Mon 1 Jun - Fri 5 Jun 2026. A second smaller start group of 500 departs at 12:00.
- **Warm-up day**: Wednesday 3 June (Alpe d'HuZus -- volunteers and coaches, 09:30-16:00)
- **Field size**: 5,000 participants (capped), plus 500 extra starting spots. Sells out. 2026 sold out within days of registration opening.
- **Founded**: 2006 (first edition: 6 June 2006, with 66 participants -- the "6/6/06 with 66" founding)
- **Entry fee**: EUR 160 registration (2026, from 1 Jan 2026) + mandatory minimum EUR 2,500 fundraising per participant = effective minimum EUR 2,660 total commitment
- **Start format**: Wave starts from 04:30 AM; second wave at 12:00 PM. Cutoff: 20:00 (8 PM). Collective final ascent at end of day.
- **Charity**: 100% of donations go to KWF Kankerbestrijding (Dutch Cancer Society) via the dedicated Alpe d'HuZes/KWF Fund
- **Participation modes**: Cycling (vast majority), running, walking. Mode chosen at registration, cannot be changed.
- **Motto**: "Opgeven is geen optie" (Quitting is not an option)
- **Name etymology**: Portmanteau of "Alpe d'Huez" + Dutch "zes" (six) = "Alpe d'HuZes"
- **Organizer**: Stichting Alpe d'HuZes (foundation)
- **Total raised (all editions through 2025)**: Over EUR 208 million (official site figure as of 2025). Some sources cite EUR 245M+ including 2024-2025 editions.
- **2024 raised**: EUR 18,761,368
- **2025 raised**: EUR 19,066,018

## Course Profile

### Single Ascent: Bourg d'Oisans to Alpe d'Huez
- **Distance**: 13.8km / 8.6mi
- **Elevation gain**: 1,120m / 3,675ft (some sources cite 1,143m depending on exact start/finish point)
- **Start altitude**: 717m / 2,352ft (Bourg d'Oisans)
- **Summit altitude**: 1,850m / 6,070ft (Alpe d'Huez resort; some sources cite 1,841m to 1,860m depending on exact finish)
- **Average gradient**: 8.1%
- **Maximum gradient**: 13% (some sources cite up to 14% in the steepest sections near the base)
- **Steepest section**: First 6 hairpins up to La Garde, averaging ~10-11%
- **Road surface**: 100% asphalt, excellent condition (Tour de France-grade tarmac, regularly maintained)
- **Hairpins**: 21 legendary switchbacks (lacets), numbered in descending order from 21 (bottom) to 1 (top near village)
- **Hairpin names**: Each named after a Tour de France stage winner at Alpe d'Huez (27 winners share 21 signs)
- **PJAMM difficulty**: Hors Categorie (HC)
- **Pro record**: Marco Pantani 37:35 (Tour de France, 1997)

### Gradient Profile (approximate)
- **Km 0-1**: Flat departure from Bourg d'Oisans, then immediately ramps to 10-12%
- **Km 1-4** (Hairpins 21-16): Steepest sustained section, 10-11% average. Dense forest, hairpins tightly stacked.
- **Km 4-6** (Hairpins 16-13): Gradients ease slightly to 7-9% through La Garde village. Brief psychological respite.
- **Km 6-8** (Hairpins 13-9): Returns to 8-9% average. Through Huez village. Views begin opening up.
- **Km 8-10** (Hairpins 9-7): 8-9% average. Approaching the famous Hairpin 7 (Dutch Corner). Exposed alpine meadow.
- **Km 10-12** (Hairpins 7-3): 7-8% average. Above treeline views. Iconic panorama of Oisans valley below.
- **Km 12-13.8** (Hairpins 3-1): Gradient eases to 5-7% approaching the resort. Final kilometer is the gentlest.

### Hairpin 7: Dutch Corner
- The most famous bend in professional cycling during Tour de France stages
- Thousands of orange-clad Dutch fans create an impenetrable wall of noise during the Tour
- Gradient before this bend hits 10-11%
- Named after Michael Rasmussen (and informally "Dutch Corner")
- During Alpe d'HuZes, this hairpin becomes the emotional epicenter for 5,000 Dutch participants

### Six-Ascent Challenge (Full Day)
- **Total distance**: ~84km / 52mi (6 x 14km)
- **Total elevation**: ~6,700m / 21,980ft (6 x ~1,120m)
- **Total descent**: ~6,700m / 21,980ft (riders descend back to Bourg d'Oisans between each ascent)
- **Time window**: 04:30-20:00 = 15.5 hours to complete all six ascents
- **Average time per ascent for recreational cyclist**: 90-120 minutes climbing, ~30 min descent + rest = ~2-2.5 hours per cycle
- **Realistic completion**: Completing all 6 requires sustained pace and efficient transitions
- **Partial participation**: Riders can complete fewer than 6 ascents. Many complete 1-3 ascents.

## Logistics

### Getting There
- **Nearest major city**: Grenoble (63km / 39mi west, ~1hr drive)
- **Nearest airports**:
  - Grenoble-Isere (GNB): 110km, ~1.5hr drive (limited flights, mostly seasonal charters)
  - Lyon-Saint Exupery (LYS): 160km, ~2hr drive (major international hub, best option from Netherlands)
  - Geneva (GVA): 220km, ~2.5hr drive (major international hub, viable alternative)
- **Driving from Netherlands**: ~1,100km / 12-13hr drive (Amsterdam to Bourg d'Oisans). Many Dutch participants drive in convoy or take organized coach transport. Route typically via Belgium, Luxembourg, eastern France (A6/A7).
- **Organized transport**: Many Dutch corporate teams and cycling clubs arrange chartered buses. The event week structure (Mon-Fri) accommodates multi-day travel.
- **Bourg d'Oisans to Alpe d'Huez**: 11mi / 18km by road, ~25min by car. The climb itself IS the route.

### Accommodation
- **Bourg d'Oisans**: Primary base camp at the foot of the climb. Small town (~3,500 residents) that transforms into a Dutch village during event week.
  - Multiple campsites: Camping La Cascade, Camping Le Colporteur, A la Rencontre du Soleil (4-star), Camping La Piscine, Camping Le Chateau de Rochetaillee
  - Hotels, gites, and rental apartments available but limited
  - Book extremely early -- accommodation sells out months in advance for the Alpe d'HuZes week
- **Alpe d'Huez resort**: Ski resort with significant hotel/apartment capacity (summer is lower season)
  - More expensive than valley options
  - Advantage: already at the top, avoid first morning descent
- **Surrounding villages**: Huez, Oz-en-Oisans, Vaujany -- smaller options within 15-30min drive

### Event Week Program
- **Monday-Tuesday**: Arrival, registration, bike checks, acclimatization rides
- **Tuesday evening**: BBQ offered by French municipalities (proceeds to Alpe d'HuZes)
- **Evening programs**: Music, speakers, conversations about cancer -- emotional, communal atmosphere
- **Wednesday**: Alpe d'HuZus day (volunteers, coaches, staff ride)
- **Thursday**: RACE DAY (04:30-20:00) -- the main event
- **Friday**: Departure, cleanup

### Registration
- Opens mid-October for the following year's edition (2026: opened 18 October 2025)
- Sells out rapidly -- 5,000 spots + 500 extra gone within days
- Registration is personal and non-transferable
- Each email address limited to one registration
- Mode of participation (cycling/running/walking) locked at registration
- Official app provides event week logistics, schedule, and live tracking

## History

### Origin Story
- **January 2005**: Peter Kapitein, a Dutch cycling enthusiast and businessman, is diagnosed with non-Hodgkin lymphoma. He undergoes treatment and survives.
- **2005**: Kapitein's cycling friend Bas Mulder dies from the same cancer, despite receiving the same treatment. At Mulder's funeral, Kapitein promises to find out why he survived when his friend did not. This question becomes the seed of Alpe d'HuZes.
- **Concept**: Kapitein, together with co-founders Coen van Veenendaal and Jan Gerrit Schuurman, creates an audacious charity challenge -- climb Alpe d'Huez (a mountain sacred to Dutch cycling culture) six times in one day to raise money for cancer research.
- **Why Alpe d'Huez**: The mountain is known as "the Dutch Mountain" in cycling lore. Eight Dutch cyclists have won Tour de France stages at Alpe d'Huez (more than any other nation), making it a uniquely resonant symbol for the Dutch. Hairpin 7 is already known as "Dutch Corner" during the Tour.

### The 2013-2014 Scandal
- **August 2013**: Shortly after the record EUR 29M edition, it emerges that the foundation was sitting on EUR 37M in unspent donations that KWF had been unable to allocate to qualified research projects.
- **Coen van Veenendaal**: Co-founder accused of receiving nearly EUR 300,000 in management fees through Inspire2Live (a related cancer research advocacy organization) between 2010-2012.
- **Inspire2Live spending**: Of EUR 5M available, only EUR 200,000 went to actual scientific research. EUR 1.4M went to management fees, travel, and conferences. The rest was unspent.
- **Media firestorm**: NOS (Dutch public broadcaster), RTL Nieuws, Nieuwsuur, and major Dutch newspapers covered the scandal extensively. Van Veenendaal went from national hero to pariah virtually overnight.
- **Impact**: 2014 donations dropped from EUR 29M to EUR 13.5M -- a 53% decline.
- **Aftermath**: KWF and Alpe d'HuZes strengthened financial oversight. Van Veenendaal departed. The foundation rebuilt trust over subsequent years. By 2025, annual fundraising had recovered to EUR 19M.

### Notable Moments
- **2006**: Peter Kapitein and 65 others climb Alpe d'Huez six times on 6/6/06. A movement is born.
- **2012**: Kapitein receives honorary doctorate from Free University of Amsterdam for "exceptional merits for investigating and improving the possibilities of living with cancer."
- **2013**: EUR 29M raised -- the single largest one-day charity fundraising event in Dutch history.
- **2020**: First cancellation in history due to COVID-19. Participants organize virtual climbs.
- **2024-2025**: Fundraising surges back to near-peak levels (EUR 18.8M and EUR 19.1M), demonstrating enduring cultural power.

## Climate (June at Alpe d'Huez)

### Temperature
- **Alpe d'Huez resort (1,850m)**: Average highs 18C / 64F, average lows 7C / 45F in June
- **Bourg d'Oisans (720m)**: Average highs 24-26C / 75-79F, average lows 11-13C / 52-55F in June
- **Pre-dawn start (04:30)**: Temperatures at the base can be 8-12C / 46-54F. At the summit, potentially 3-6C / 37-43F.
- **Midday summit**: 15-20C / 59-68F on a good day; can be significantly cooler with wind/rain
- **Temperature differential**: 8-12C difference between valley and summit

### Precipitation
- **June rainfall**: ~142mm average for the month at Alpe d'Huez
- **Rain probability**: ~49% chance of rain on any given day; ~16 rainy days in June
- **Rain character**: Alpine thunderstorms are common in afternoon/evening. Morning tends to be clearer.
- **Waterproof gear**: Essential. Descending in rain at 50+ km/h in 10C temperatures = hypothermia risk.

### Wind and Conditions
- **Humidity**: High average (87% relative humidity)
- **Sunshine**: ~257 hours of sun in June (approximately 8.5 hours/day)
- **Wind**: Variable; exposed upper sections of the climb can be windy, especially above Huez village
- **Altitude considerations**: At 1,850m, UV exposure is significantly higher than sea level. Sunscreen essential.
- **Best-case scenario**: Clear, warm (20C+), light wind -- common enough in early June
- **Worst-case scenario**: Cold rain, reduced visibility, thunderstorms -- happens regularly

### Race-Day Weather Strategy
- **Layer system**: Arm warmers and a rain vest for the 04:30 start; strip layers as day warms
- **Descent preparation**: Full-finger gloves, wind vest, rain jacket for each descent (even on clear days, early morning descents are frigid)
- **Sun protection**: At altitude, sunburn risk is severe even on overcast days
- **Hydration**: High altitude + sustained climbing = aggressive hydration strategy needed

## Community & Culture

### A Dutch National Phenomenon
- Alpe d'HuZes is arguably the Netherlands' most emotionally resonant annual sporting event outside of professional football and the Tour de France
- The event has become embedded in Dutch popular culture -- "opgeven is geen optie" is a widely recognized phrase across the Netherlands
- Corporate teams dominate participation: companies field teams of 10-50+ employees, with months of shared training, fundraising galas, and team building
- The event transforms Bourg d'Oisans and Alpe d'Huez into a temporary Dutch colony for a week -- Dutch flags, orange jerseys, Dutch food, Dutch music, and Dutch language dominate the town
- Estimated 15,000-20,000+ Dutch people travel to the area during event week (participants, support crews, families, spectators)

### Emotional Core
- **Cancer is the purpose**: Every participant either has cancer, has survived cancer, has lost someone to cancer, or rides for someone fighting cancer. Names of loved ones are written on jerseys, printed on bibs, displayed on memorial boards.
- **The collective final climb**: At the end of the day, all remaining participants climb together one last time. Exhausted riders who have already completed their six ascents join those on their first or second. The emotional weight of this final ascent is the event's signature moment.
- **Cancer survivors and patients participate**: Some participants are actively undergoing treatment. The sight of cancer patients climbing Alpe d'Huez alongside their supporters is the event's most powerful image.
- **"Giving up is not an option"**: The motto applies equally to the climb and to the fight against cancer. This dual meaning gives every pedal stroke symbolic weight.

### The Dutch-Alpe d'Huez Connection
- Alpe d'Huez has been a Tour de France climb since 1952 (Fausto Coppi won the first stage)
- 8 Dutch riders have won stages at Alpe d'Huez -- more than any other nationality
- The mountain earned the nickname "the Dutch Mountain" due to Dutch dominance and fan culture
- Dutch Corner (Hairpin 7) during the Tour de France is the most famous spectator party in professional cycling -- orange smoke flares, beer, dance music, and deafening noise
- Alpe d'HuZes extends this cultural bond from spectating to participating -- Dutch fans don't just watch the mountain, they climb it themselves

### Media Coverage
- **Omroep Gelderland**: Provides live television coverage and livestreaming (9:00-17:00) on race day
- **NOS**: National public broadcaster covers results and human interest stories
- **RTL Nieuws**: Commercial broadcaster covers the event
- **Dutch print/digital media**: De Volkskrant, AD, Telegraaf, and regional papers provide extensive coverage
- **Social media**: The event generates massive Dutch social media activity; the official app enables live tracking of participants
- **International recognition**: Covered by Sofii (international fundraising case study database), featured in Cycling Plus, recognized as one of the most successful charity cycling events globally

### Fundraising Model
- **100% donation pledge**: All donated money goes directly to cancer research via KWF. Organizational costs are covered separately by registration fees and sponsorships. This is the event's most powerful trust proposition.
- **Minimum EUR 2,500 per participant**: Each participant must raise at least EUR 2,500 in donations from their personal network
- **Personal fundraising pages**: Every participant gets an online page to collect donations, share their story, and track progress
- **Corporate sponsorship**: Major Dutch corporations sponsor teams and the event itself, covering operational costs
- **A-CaRe research program**: Alpe d'HuZes funded the A-CaRe (Alpe d'HuZes Cancer Rehabilitation) research program -- four randomized controlled exercise trials studying cancer rehabilitation through physical activity

### Field Composition
- **Not a race**: There are no prizes, no rankings, no competitive timing. This is a challenge event with a charity purpose.
- **Demographics**: Overwhelmingly Dutch (95%+). All ages, all fitness levels. Corporate teams, cycling clubs, families, friend groups.
- **Cancer connection**: A significant percentage of participants are cancer survivors or active patients. Many teams are organized around a specific person's cancer journey.
- **Fitness range**: From serious amateur cyclists who complete all 6 ascents in under 10 hours, to first-time riders who complete 1-2 ascents and walk portions.
- **Support crews**: Each participant or team typically brings support crew (family, friends) who wait in Bourg d'Oisans, provide nutrition, mechanical support, and emotional encouragement at designated points.

### The Event Village
- Bourg d'Oisans is taken over by the Alpe d'HuZes community for the full week
- Evening programs feature music, guest speakers, and communal conversations about cancer
- Tuesday BBQ hosted by French municipalities with proceeds to the foundation
- The atmosphere blends somber reflection (cancer stories, memorials) with fierce celebration (completing the climbs, camaraderie, Dutch party culture)
