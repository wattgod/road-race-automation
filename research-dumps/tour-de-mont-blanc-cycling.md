# Research Dump: Tour du Mont-Blanc Cyclo

## Quick Facts
- Single-day ultra cyclosportive circumnavigating the Mont Blanc massif through France, Switzerland, and Italy
- Solo route: ~330-338km / ~205-210mi, ~8,300-8,500m / ~27,230-27,887ft climbing (9 major climbs, 5 Hors Categorie)
- Duo relay option: Leg 1 ~155km / 4,300m+, Leg 2 ~175km / 4,000m+ (changeover at Col du Grand Saint-Bernard)
- Start/finish: Les Saisies (1,648m), commune of Hauteluce, Savoie, Auvergne-Rhone-Alpes, France
- 2026 date: Saturday, July 11, 2026. Start time: 5:00am. Finish line closes at 23:59
- 2025 date: Saturday, July 12, 2025
- Field size: ~400-700 starters per edition. Early editions had ~150-400 starters. ~693 starters recorded in one recent edition
- Attrition rate: ~33-50% DNF rate. Up to 50% of starters do not finish. Across the first 7 editions, only ~1,300 total finishers
- Fewer than 10 women have ever completed the event (some sources cite only 4)
- Founded: 2010 (inaugural edition). Official organized series from 2012 onward. 2024 = approximately 11th edition
- Entry fee (2024 pricing): Solo EUR 150 (first 75 bibs), EUR 160 (next 175 bibs), EUR 170 (final bibs). Duo EUR 225-240 per team
- Tour operator packages (e.g., Alpcycles): 3-night package from Les Saisies with Geneva airport transfer, hotel, course support included
- Start format: mass start at 5:00am. Not officially a race — classified as a "randonnee" (timed sportive). All participants are timed but there is no official classification. All finishers receive a "Gold" certificate
- Finishing time range: ~12 hours (fastest) to ~19 hours (time limit). Finish line closes at 23:59
- Medical certificate: not required but strongly recommended. Own insurance mandatory (FFC licence or supplementary). Additional insurance available at registration
- Helmet mandatory. Front and rear lights mandatory for entire ride (pre-dawn start, possible night riding)
- Organizer: Sportcommunication (contact: info@sportcommunication.com). Based in Savoie region
- Tagline: "The world's toughest one day bike event" (as described by cycling press)
- Described as "roughly the equivalent of riding La Marmotte twice" in terms of distance and climbing

## What's Included in Entry Fee
- Frame identification and timing plate (front handlebar mount)
- Route description sticker
- Chip timing on back of frame plate
- Access to all 7 refreshment/feed stations along the route
- Bag drop at 1 and/or 2 refreshment points (rider selects which stations)
- Emergency contact number in road book
- Medical/safety support along route
- Finisher certificate ("Gold" for all who complete within time limit)

## Route Options

### Solo — 330-338km / 8,300-8,500m+
- Full circumnavigation of Mont Blanc massif in one day
- Departs Les Saisies at 5:00am, clockwise through France, Switzerland, Italy, back to France
- 9 categorized climbs, 5 of which are Hors Categorie
- 3 international border crossings (France-Switzerland at Col des Montets/Chamonix valley, Switzerland-Italy at Col du Grand Saint-Bernard, Italy-France at Col du Petit Saint-Bernard)

### Heroic Duo — 330km total relay
- Two-person relay team covering the full route
- Leg 1: ~155km with ~4,300m climbing (Les Saisies to Col du Grand Saint-Bernard summit)
- Leg 2: ~175km with ~4,000m climbing (Col du Grand Saint-Bernard to Les Saisies)
- Changeover at the Swiss-Italian border at the summit of the Grand Saint-Bernard (2,469m)
- Start: 5:00am for Leg 1 rider

## Course Profile — Solo Route (clockwise)

The route departs Les Saisies at 5:00am with a descent to Megeve, then proceeds clockwise around the Mont Blanc massif through nine major climbs across three countries. The terrain is characterized by sustained high-altitude Alpine climbing with long valley approaches between passes. All road surfaces are paved.

### Climb 1: Col de Vaudagne (~630m summit)
- Category: 2nd category
- A warm-up climb on the road toward Chamonix after descending from Les Saisies to Megeve
- Moderate gradient, relatively short approach

### Climb 2: Col des Montets (1,461m)
- Category: 1st category
- Distance: ~11.7km
- Average gradient: ~3.7-3.8%
- Maximum gradient: ~13.8%
- Elevation gain: ~469m (from ~1,000m to 1,461m)
- Gradual climb through the Chamonix valley toward Switzerland. Descent crosses the border into Switzerland at high speed (~70 kph)

### Climb 3: Col de la Forclaz (1,527m)
- Category: 2nd category
- Distance: ~13.7km (from Martigny) or ~7km (from Le Chatelard, south side)
- Average gradient: ~7.4% (from Martigny), ~6.1% (from south)
- Maximum gradient: ~10%
- Elevation gain: ~1,052m (from Martigny), ~437m (from south)
- In the TMB Cyclo the approach is from the south (Trient valley side), not from Martigny. Precedes an amazing descent to Martigny at speeds well in excess of 85 kph
- Feed station at the foot of this climb (2nd feed station of the day)

### Climb 4: Champex / Col de Champex (1,498m)
- Category: Hors Categorie (HC) — 1st HC climb of the day
- Distance: ~10.6-12km (depending on approach)
- Average gradient: ~8% (from Les Valettes/Orsieres approach used in event)
- Maximum gradient: ~12-12.5%
- Elevation gain: ~621-850m (from ~650m valley to ~1,500m)
- Encountered at approximately km 110. This is where the route "begins to unleash its fury." Many long sections of 12% gradient. The first truly devastating climb of the day
- From Orsieres: 10.7km, 621m gain, 5.8% average, 11.2% max steepest 100m

### Climb 5: Col du Grand Saint-Bernard (2,469m)
- Category: Hors Categorie (HC)
- Distance: ~30.6-41.1km (from Sembrancher/Orsieres, varying measurement points)
- Average gradient: ~4.8-5.7%
- Maximum gradient: ~10-11.1%
- Elevation gain: ~1,800-1,968m
- Summit altitude: 2,469m — the highest point on the route
- The legendary pass connecting Switzerland and Italy. Last 6km are the most demanding after exiting the tunnel gallery section
- Feed station at summit. Duo relay changeover point (Leg 1 / Leg 2 transition)
- Border crossing into Italy at the summit. Long, fast descent into Aosta Valley follows

### Climb 6: Aosta Valley approach
- Gradual valley-floor climb from Aosta up the valley toward La Thuile and Col du Petit Saint-Bernard
- Not a categorized climb per se, but steady elevation gain over a long distance

### Climb 7: Col du Petit Saint-Bernard (2,188m)
- Category: Hors Categorie (HC)
- Distance: ~29.8km (from Bourg-Saint-Maurice, French side) or ~23km (from La Thuile, Italian side used in the event)
- Average gradient: ~4.6% (from Bourg-Saint-Maurice), ~5.1% (from La Thuile)
- Maximum gradient: moderate — gradients are never extreme
- Elevation gain: ~1,378m (from Bourg-Saint-Maurice), ~1,179m (from La Thuile)
- Summit altitude: 2,188m
- 28km long with 19 hairpins (French side). Accessible by Alpine standards: 30km with 1,400m climbing yet gradients average less than 5%. Even gradient and flat hairpins mean the ascent never feels too strenuous despite the altitude
- In the TMB Cyclo, riders approach from the Italian side (La Thuile, 23km at 5.1%). Passes through the ski resort of La Thuile with a further 11.4km to summit
- Ancient road linking Lyon and Rome for over 1,000 years
- Crosses from Italy back into France at the summit

### Climb 8: Cormet de Roselend (1,967m)
- Category: Hors Categorie (HC)
- Distance: ~19.3km (from Bourg-Saint-Maurice)
- Average gradient: ~6.0-6.1%
- Maximum gradient: ~9.4-13.8% (steepest 100m averages 9.4%)
- Elevation gain: ~1,155m
- Summit altitude: 1,967-1,969m
- At around 7km the road ramps up to an average 8% for 3km through 10 tight hairpins. This climb constitutes often a critical moment of the day
- TIME CUTOFF: Must reach summit of Cormet de Roselend before 22:30 (10:30pm). This becomes the ultimate objective for many participants

### Climb 9: Col des Saisies (1,648m) — Final climb / finish
- Category: Hors Categorie (HC) — 5th HC climb
- Distance: ~15.6-16.1km (from Beaufort)
- Average gradient: ~5.7-5.9%
- Maximum gradient: ~13%
- Elevation gain: ~917-918m
- Divided into two sections by a 0.6km descent: first section rises at ~6.5% for 8km; second section is ~6.6km averaging over 7%
- Mountain-top finish at the alpine resort of Les Saisies. The final climb after 300+ km of riding
- Has been featured in Tour de France stages

## Time Limits / Cutoff Points
- 3 time limit checkpoints along the route for safety
- Bourg-Saint-Maurice: cutoff at 20:00 (8:00pm) — approximately km 250-260
- Summit of Cormet de Roselend: cutoff at 22:30 (10:30pm) — approximately km 280
- Finish line at Les Saisies: closes at 23:59 (11:59pm) — approximately 19 hours after start
- Riders who do not pass checkpoints before cutoff times are not permitted to continue

## Feed Zones / Refreshment Stations
- 7 refreshment stations along the route (included in entry fee)
- Bag drop service available at 1-2 selected stations (riders choose which ones during registration)
- Feed stations offer water, refreshments, and food
- Tour operator VIP options (e.g., Alpcycles) offer additional rolling feed stations with homemade real food (sweet and savoury)
- Key feed station locations include: foot of Col de la Forclaz (2nd station), summit of Col du Grand Saint-Bernard, and several others spaced across the route

## Logistics

### Nearest Airports
- **Geneva International Airport (GVA)**: ~110-115km from Les Saisies. Transfer time ~2h10-2h15. The primary/allocated airport for the event
- **Lyon-Saint Exupery Airport (LYS)**: ~165km from Les Saisies. Transfer time ~2h45
- **Chambery Airport (CMF)**: ~80km from Les Saisies (seasonal, limited flights)

### Ground Transport
- Closest train station: Albertville, with daily bus connections to Les Saisies
- Private transfers from Geneva airport available through tour operators (~2h drive)
- Car: A40 motorway from Geneva, then D925/D218 to Les Saisies. Parking available in resort
- Alpcycles and other official tour operators provide airport transfer packages (typically Geneva pickup/dropoff)

### Lodging Strategy
- Les Saisies is a small alpine ski resort (elevation 1,650m) with varied accommodation: hotels, chalets, apartments, vacation rentals
- Hauteluce village (commune containing Les Saisies) offers traditional Alpine lodging
- Book early — accommodation is very limited for a resort of this size during event weekend
- Tour operator packages (Alpcycles): 3 nights in 3-star family-run hotel in heart of Les Saisies, right by finish line
- Arrive minimum Friday (day before) for pre-race briefing (Saturday afternoon) and equipment check
- Budget options: vacation rentals through Airbnb/booking platforms in Les Saisies or Hauteluce
- Alternative bases: Megeve (~30min drive), Albertville (~45min drive), Beaufort (~30min drive)
- Le Hameau du Beaufortain: quiet woodland setting, collection of seven traditional chalets in Hauteluce
- Summer season in Les Saisies is active from end of June — shops and services are open

### Pre-Race Requirements
- Registration packet pickup (includes frame plate, timing chip, roadbook, route sticker)
- Pre-race briefing: Saturday afternoon (mandatory attendance recommended). Race Director and announcer provide safety instructions
- No medical certificate required, but full medical examination strongly recommended
- Insurance mandatory: FFC competition licence or supplementary coverage. Additional insurance available at registration
- Bike must have front and rear lights (mandatory for entire ride due to pre-dawn start and possible late-night finish)
- Helmet mandatory for entire ride
- Visibility vest essential for low-light/night sections
- Emergency number in roadbook must be filled out before starting

## History

### Origins and Early Editions
- **2010 — First Edition**: 150 starters in perfect weather conditions. 105 finishers. "A legend was born." Exact founding details and organizer backstory unclear, but the event achieved immediate cult status
- **2011 — Brutal Conditions**: 300 entrants at Saturday briefing. Only 250 lined up at Sunday start. Horrific conditions: 50mm rain, sleet, snow, strong winds, real temperatures as low as 2 degrees Celsius, wind chill on descents as low as minus 30 degrees. Only 92 finishers within time limit — a ~63% DNF rate. This edition cemented the event's fearsome reputation
- **2012 — Growth Year**: Near-perfect conditions. Almost 400 riders started, including ~30 two-man relay teams. Battistrada records this as the first "officially organized" edition
- **Subsequent years**: Steady growth. Event earned the tag "the world's toughest one day bike event" from cycling press. Hailed by cycling magazines worldwide. Event has been described as "bigger, longer, harder and tougher than its famous siblings such as the Marmotte, the Mallorca 312 and the 3 Ballons"
- **7th edition milestone**: Only ~1,300 cumulative finishers across all 7 editions. Attrition rate of more than one-third of starters
- **2024**: Approximately 11th edition. Saturday July 13, 2024
- **One edition recorded**: 470 participants with finishing times published. Another edition: 693 starters recorded

### Notable Statistics
- Cumulative finisher count after 7 editions: ~1,300
- Average DNF rate across editions: ~33-50%
- Fewer than 10 women have ever completed the event (some accounts say only 4)
- No official classification — all finishers receive "Gold" certificate regardless of time

## Climate & Weather

### Typical July Conditions (Race Day)
- Valley floors (500-800m): 20-30 degrees C daytime, ~10 degrees C at night
- Alpine passes (1,500-2,500m): 5-15 degrees C, significantly cooler with wind chill
- Temperature can swing 20+ degrees C in a single day between valley floors and high passes
- July is the warmest month in the Alps, but afternoon thunderstorms are common
- Precipitation risk: moderate to high. Afternoon convective thunderstorms are frequent in July
- Fresh snow is possible on passes above 2,400m even in July/August — not uncommon
- Wind chill on descents can drop effective temperature dramatically (2011 edition: wind chill minus 30 degrees C)

### Specific Altitude Exposure
- Riders spend extended time above 2,000m: Col du Grand Saint-Bernard (2,469m), Col du Petit Saint-Bernard (2,188m)
- Pre-dawn start (5:00am) means first 1-2 hours in darkness and cool temperatures
- Late-night finish possible (up to 23:59) — temperatures drop significantly after sunset (~21:00-21:30 in July)
- Must carry warm layers, rain gear, and lights for the full ride

### Climate Risk Assessment
- High variability: conditions range from perfect sunshine to life-threatening cold/wet in the same ride
- The 2011 edition is the cautionary tale: sleet, snow, 50mm rain, winds, near-zero temperatures
- Mountain weather can change rapidly — riders must be self-sufficient with clothing and nutrition
- 3 countries crossed means 3 different weather microclimates in a single day

## Community & Culture

### Atmosphere
- The event is explicitly "not a race" — organizers emphasize this at the pre-race briefing. It is a "randonnee" — the French term for an endurance challenge ride
- The common goal is to finish. There is no podium, no prize money, no official ranking
- Deep camaraderie among participants given the extreme shared challenge
- Survival ethos: the 33-50% DNF rate means finishing is the achievement itself
- Pre-race briefing on Saturday afternoon is a communal gathering with safety instructions from the Race Director

### International Participation
- Riders from multiple European countries and beyond, though predominantly French-speaking nations
- The three-country format (France, Switzerland, Italy) gives it inherent international character
- Tour operators (Alpcycles, Hammer Sport, Imp Adventures, Classic Cycling Tours) attract English-speaking international riders

### Media Coverage
- Featured in Cyclist magazine ("Beyond Limits" review), Road Cycling UK, Velofanatics, Shutt Velo Rapide
- Described as "the mother granfondo of all granfondos" and "an ultra cycling granfondo — bigger, longer, harder and tougher than its famous siblings"
- Cycling press consistently uses the tagline "The world's toughest one day bike event"
- Multiple detailed race reports and training guides published by participants and cycling media

### Comparison to Other Ultra Events
- Described as equivalent to riding La Marmotte twice (distance and climbing)
- Compared favorably in difficulty to Mallorca 312, Trois Ballons, Marmotte, Maratona dles Dolomites — but universally rated as harder
- The closest comparables in endurance cycling: Transcontinental Race (multi-day, unsupported), but TMB Cyclo compresses similar suffering into a single day
- The UTMB (Ultra-Trail du Mont-Blanc) running race follows a similar circumnavigation on foot/trail

### Training and Preparation
- Requires months of dedicated preparation — one participant started training 9 months in advance
- Participating in one or more other granfondos (Marmotte, Etape du Tour) is recommended preparation
- Must be comfortable riding 200+ km with 5,000+ m climbing as a baseline before attempting the TMB
- Mental resilience equally important as physical fitness given the 12-19 hour duration
- Night riding skills necessary (pre-dawn start, possible post-sunset finish)

## Road Surface & Terrain Notes
- Entire route is on paved roads — no gravel or off-road sections
- Road surface quality is generally very good across all three countries (France, Switzerland, Italy)
- Swiss roads particularly well-maintained; Italian Aosta Valley roads in good condition; French Alpine roads standard quality
- Some roads are main traffic corridors (e.g., approach to Grand Saint-Bernard has traffic, trucks, and tunnel sections)
- The last 6km of Grand Saint-Bernard are on open mountain road after exiting the tunnel gallery
- Descents are on the same roads as climbs — some are technical with tight hairpins (Cormet de Roselend has 10 tight hairpins)
- Descents can be extremely fast: speeds exceeding 85 kph reported on Col de la Forclaz descent to Martigny

## Scenic Experience
- The route circles the entire Mont Blanc massif (4,808m) — the highest peak in the Alps and Western Europe
- Views of glaciers, snow-capped peaks, and Alpine meadows throughout
- Chamonix valley: dramatic views of the Aiguilles, Mer de Glace, and Mont Blanc itself
- Swiss Valais: vineyard-lined approaches to Martigny, pastoral Alpine valleys
- Col du Grand Saint-Bernard: historic hospice at summit, panoramic mountain views, centuries-old pilgrimage route
- Aosta Valley (Italy): dramatic glacial valleys, Italian Alpine architecture
- Lac de Roselend: stunning mountain reservoir visible near summit of Cormet de Roselend
- Col des Saisies finish: views across Beaufortain Alps toward Mont Blanc
- Three distinct national characters (French, Swiss, Italian) visible in architecture, signage, and landscape
- Pre-dawn and possible post-sunset riding adds unique atmosphere (riding through the night in high mountains)

## Scoring Dimension Notes

### Distance (5)
330-338km in a single day. One of the longest single-day cyclosportives in the world. Equivalent to riding La Marmotte twice.

### Climbing (5)
8,300-8,500m of elevation gain. 9 categorized climbs including 5 Hors Categorie. Two passes above 2,000m (Grand Saint-Bernard 2,469m, Petit Saint-Bernard 2,188m). Extreme sustained climbing load.

### Descent Technicality (3-4)
Descents are on paved Alpine roads. Some are very fast (85+ kph on Col de la Forclaz descent). Cormet de Roselend has 10 tight hairpins. Not technical gravel, but high-speed Alpine descending on fatigued legs after 200+ km is genuinely dangerous.

### Road Surface (4)
Entirely paved roads across three countries. Swiss roads excellent quality. French and Italian Alpine roads generally well-maintained. Some main traffic corridors with trucks and tunnel sections on approach to Grand Saint-Bernard.

### Climate Risk (5)
Extreme variability. July Alpine weather can range from 30 degrees C in valleys to near-freezing with snow on passes above 2,400m. The 2011 edition saw 50mm rain, sleet, snow, wind chill to minus 30. Pre-dawn start and possible post-sunset finish add temperature extremes. Afternoon thunderstorms common.

### Altitude (5)
Summit of Col du Grand Saint-Bernard at 2,469m. Col du Petit Saint-Bernard at 2,188m. Extended time above 2,000m on two separate climbs. Cormet de Roselend at 1,967m. Physiological altitude effects are a real factor at these elevations after 250+ km of riding.

### Logistics (3-4)
Geneva airport 2h15 away is convenient. Les Saisies is a small ski resort with limited accommodation — book early. Three-country route requires awareness of border crossings (within Schengen, so no passport checks in normal circumstances). Self-sufficiency required between feed stations. Night riding equipment mandatory.

### Prestige (4-5)
Known worldwide as "the world's toughest one day bike event." Immediate cult status from first edition. Featured in major cycling media (Cyclist, Road Cycling UK). Fewer than 1,300 finishers after first 7 editions. Extremely exclusive completion rate. However, smaller field size and younger history compared to L'Etape or Marmotte.

### Organization (4)
7 feed stations, medical support, chip timing, bag drop service, emergency contact system, pre-race briefing, 3 time limit checkpoints for safety. Sportcommunication is experienced organizer. Tour operator support available. Lights and helmet mandatory. No official classification (randonnee format).

### Scenic Experience (5)
Circumnavigation of Mont Blanc (4,808m), highest peak in Alps. Three countries with distinct Alpine character. Chamonix valley views. Historic Col du Grand Saint-Bernard. Lac de Roselend. Italian Aosta Valley. Glaciers, snow-capped peaks, Alpine meadows. Pre-dawn and possible night riding through mountains adds unique atmosphere.

### Community & Culture (3-4)
Strong but small community. Randonnee spirit — not a race, but a shared survival challenge. Deep camaraderie. International participants but modest field size. Pre-race briefing is communal. Very few women finishers (under 10 total). Growing media profile but still niche compared to Marmotte or L'Etape.

### Field Depth (2-3)
Small field: 400-700 starters. No official classification means no competitive depth in traditional sense. Ultra-endurance self-selected field — all participants are serious, but numbers are limited. No elite/pro wave.

### Value (3-4)
EUR 150-170 solo entry includes 7 feed stations, timing, bag drop, support. Reasonable for the distance and complexity. However, with travel to Alps, accommodation in resort, and equipment requirements (lights, layers, rain gear), total cost adds up significantly. Tour operator packages (3-night hotel + support) offer convenience at premium pricing.

### Expenses (4-5)
Entry: EUR 150-170. Accommodation in Les Saisies: EUR 80-200/night depending on type. Flights to Geneva: varies by origin. Airport transfer: EUR 50-100. Minimum 2-3 nights accommodation. Tour operator all-inclusive packages likely EUR 600-1,000+. Total budget: EUR 500-1,500+ depending on origin and accommodation choices. Not cheap, but reasonable compared to multi-day stage races.
