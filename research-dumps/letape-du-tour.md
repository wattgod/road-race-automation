# Research Dump: L'Etape du Tour de France

## Quick Facts
- 170km / 105.6mi, 5,400m / 17,717ft climbing (2026 edition), 4 major cols + Col de Sarenne finish
- Course changes every year (follows a Tour de France mountain stage); 2026 is one of the toughest editions ever
- Start: Bourg d'Oisans (720m) -> Finish: Alpe d'Huez (1,860m) via Col de Sarenne (2026 edition)
- Saturday 19 July 2026 (34th edition). Typically held on a Tour de France rest day in mid-July
- 16,000 entries sold. International field from 60+ countries. Open registration, first-come-first-served (sells out quickly)
- Founded 1993 (1,700 riders first edition, Tarbes to Pau over Col du Tourmalet)
- EUR 159 base entry fee (2025 pricing); EUR 231 with event jersey and medal. 2026 pricing expected similar or slightly higher
- Wave start in multiple groups seeded by estimated finish time (faster riders in earlier waves)
- Mandatory: approved helmet, rear light (for tunnels), rain jacket/wind jacket. Medical certificate required (FFC licence or doctor's certificate issued within 1 year). No e-bikes, no TT/aero bars, no recumbents
- Title sponsor: Tour de France / ASO (Amaury Sport Organisation). Partners include Shimano, Maurten, Santini
- Organiser: Amaury Sport Organisation (ASO), same company that runs the Tour de France

## Course Profile (2026 Edition: Stage 20 of 2026 Tour de France)

**Note: The course changes every year because L'Etape replicates a different Tour de France stage each edition. The 2026 route is a classic "Marmotte-style" Alpine itinerary with 4 major climbs plus a final ascent via Col de Sarenne to Alpe d'Huez. Recent editions have varied between 130-170km and 4,000-5,400m climbing.**

### The Climbs (2026 Edition)

1. **Col de la Croix de Fer** (2,067m summit) -- From Allemont/Rochetaillée side. 27.6km, ~1,522m gain, 5.2-5.75% avg, 11% max. HC. The first major block. Three distinct sections: lower slopes from Allemont (gentle approach), steep defile at Maupas (~11% for 1km), passage past Lac de Grand'Maison dam (1,773m), junction with Col du Glandon, then final push to summit. Wide roads. Has featured in TdF 19+ times since 1947. Iron cross monument at summit.

2. **Col du Telegraphe** (1,566m summit) -- From Saint-Michel-de-Maurienne. 11.9km, 856m gain, 7.1% avg, 9.8% max. Cat 1. Steady, consistent gradient through wooded switchbacks. Named for TV/radio towers visible from distance. Wide ski-road quality. Acts as "gateway" to the Galibier. Short descent to Valloire (1,403m) before Galibier begins.

3. **Col du Galibier** (2,642m summit) -- From Valloire. 17.5km, 1,214m gain, 7% avg (some sources say 6.9%), 9.7% max. HC. Highest point on course and one of the most famous climbs in cycling history. Henri Desgrange memorial stele near summit. First used in Tour de France in 1911, featured 63+ times. Altitude effects significant above 2,200m. Sections spike to 10-11%. Very cold at summit even in July. 2,642m is the third-highest paved pass on the Route des Grandes Alpes. Panoramic views of Meije, Ecrins, and Mont Blanc on clear days.

4. **Col de Sarenne** (1,999m summit) -- From Bourg d'Oisans side via Col du Lautaret descent. Multiple profile measurements exist depending on where measurement starts:
   - From the valley floor: ~12.7-12.8km, 7.3% avg
   - From Alpe d'Huez station: 2.9km, 255m gain, 8.7% avg, 16% max. Cat 2
   - The Etape route approaches Sarenne from the south/east after descending from Galibier via Col du Lautaret
   Summit at 1,999m. Narrow road, no guardrails in places. Used controversially in 2013 Tour de France (Tony Martin criticized the narrow, dangerous road). Rarely used -- makes this edition truly special. After summiting Sarenne, riders descend briefly then arrive at Alpe d'Huez finish.

5. **Alpe d'Huez (finish)** -- Rather than the traditional 21 hairpins, riders arrive via the "back door" through Col de Sarenne. Final short ramp to finish at ~1,860m. Riders then descend the famous 21 bends as a cooldown after finishing -- a rare treat.

- **Total climbing**: 5,400m across ~170km
- **Road surface**: Excellent throughout. French national and departmental roads (D-roads) in very good condition. Exception: Col de Sarenne section is narrower with rougher patches.
- **Traffic/road closure**: Fully closed to all motorized traffic. Police-enforced road closures. One of the key selling points of the event.
- **Feed zones**: Multiple refreshment stations on course, typically positioned before/after major climbs. Serving energy gels, bars, fruit, water, Coca-Cola. Pasta party at the finish included in entry fee. Official nutrition partner is Maurten.

### Recent Edition Routes (for context on variability)
- **2025** (33rd ed): Albertville -> La Plagne. 131km, 4,500m. 5 climbs incl. Col des Saisies, Col du Pre, Cormet de Roselend, La Plagne summit finish (2,052m at 7.2% avg over 19.1km)
- **2024** (32nd ed): Nice -> Col de la Couillole. 138km, 4,600m. Col de Braus, Col de Turini, Col de la Colmiane, Col de la Couillole
- **2023**: Annemasse -> Morzine. 135km. Col de la Ramaz, Col de Joux Plane
- **2022**: Briancon -> Alpe d'Huez. 167km, 4,700m. Col du Galibier, Col de la Croix de Fer, Alpe d'Huez (21 bends)
- **2019**: Albertville -> Val Thorens. 135km, 4,563m (2020-2021 cancelled due to COVID-19)

## Logistics

### Nearest Airports with Drive Times
- **Grenoble-Isere Airport (GNB)**: 1hr 10min to Alpe d'Huez / Bourg d'Oisans. Closest airport but limited flight options (mostly seasonal/budget airlines)
- **Lyon Saint-Exupery (LYS)**: 2hr 20min. Major international hub. Best for long-haul connections. TGV station in terminal
- **Geneva (GVA)**: 2hr 40min. Major international hub. Popular for UK/Northern European riders. Cross-border (Switzerland/France)
- **Chambery (CMF)**: 1hr 40min. Small regional airport
- **Turin Caselle (TRN)**: 2hr 55min. Alternative for Italian-side approach
- **Grenoble train station**: TGV from Paris (3hrs), then bus/shuttle to Bourg d'Oisans (~1hr)

### Hotel/Lodging Strategy
- **Bourg d'Oisans**: Start village. Hotels, gites, Airbnbs. Base elevation (720m). Book 6-12 months in advance. Budget: EUR 80-150/night for hotels, EUR 50-100 for Airbnb. Limited supply -- sells out fast
- **Alpe d'Huez**: Finish village and event expo/village location. Ski resort with plentiful accommodation but premium pricing during Etape week. EUR 120-250/night. Riders staying here can descend to Bourg d'Oisans start on race morning (cold descent, bring layers)
- **Tour operator packages**: Official tour operators (Sports Tours International, Sportive Breaks, Love Velo, Cycle Classic Tours, 44|5 Cycling Tours) offer packages from EUR 299 (entry only) to EUR 895-4,399+ (3-5 night packages with hotel, transfers, guided rides, guaranteed entry). Geneva airport transfers commonly offered
- **Book early**: Accommodation in the Oisans valley sells out months in advance. Nearby towns (Vaujany, Oz-en-Oisans, Villard-Reculas) are alternatives

### Race-Day Transport
- Event shuttles between start and finish areas
- Baggage transport service from start to finish (optional, included or add-on depending on package)
- Parking at Bourg d'Oisans with shuttle connections
- After finishing, riders descend Alpe d'Huez via 21 bends to return to Bourg d'Oisans

### Start Area Logistics
- Race village/expo opens 2 days before the event for bib pickup (typically Friday-Saturday)
- Bib pickup is mandatory in person, with ID and medical certificate
- Arrive at start area 60-90 minutes before your wave start time
- Start areas are secure; access via rear entrance with bib verification
- Wave starts begin early morning (~6:30-7:00am for first wave, subsequent waves at intervals)
- "Road Captains" (experienced volunteer cyclists) ride within the field to provide guidance and pacing

### Course Closure / Cutoff Times
- Broom wagon (voiture-balai) follows the last riders. Riders overtaken by the time-scheduled car are eliminated
- Roads reopen progressively behind the broom wagon to allow local traffic
- Typical overall time limit: approximately 12-13 hours from first start. Exact cutoff times are published in the roadbook closer to the event
- Intermediate cutoff points at key climbs; riders failing to pass checkpoints by specified times are pulled from the course

## History

### Founding & Origin Story
- **1993**: Conceived by Amaury Sport Organisation (ASO), the same organization behind the Tour de France. Vision: allow amateur cyclists to ride a complete Tour de France mountain stage on fully closed roads
- **Inaugural edition**: July 11, 1993. 1,700 participants. Tarbes -> Pau, 208km, including the legendary Col du Tourmalet
- French cycling newspaper L'Equipe partnered from the start, cementing its credibility and Tour de France heritage

### Evolution
- **1994**: Participation nearly doubled. Alpe d'Huez to Val Thorens
- **1996-97**: International participation established (riders from across Europe, North America, Australia). 5,000+ participants by 1997
- **2000**: Carpentras -> Mont Ventoux became a legendary edition (scorching heat on the "Giant of Provence"). Coincided with Armstrong-era Tour
- **2003**: 10th anniversary featured Alpe d'Huez, record 8,500 participants
- **2004**: Included same time trial course as that year's Tour (Bourg d'Oisans -> Alpe d'Huez)
- **2006**: Dual-event format introduced to meet demand -- two Etapes on different weekends replicating different stages
- **2011-2012**: Two-day format (two stages). 2011 saw heavy rain, only 2,094 finishers on stage two
- **2013**: Two stages; Stage 2 featured controversial Col de Sarenne descent
- **2020-2021**: Cancelled due to COVID-19 pandemic
- **2022**: Return after COVID. 167km, Briancon -> Alpe d'Huez. 16,000 entries. Austrian Stefan Kirchmair won (5:17:27). 9,000 finishers
- **2025**: New addition -- L'Etape du Tour will also take place alongside Tour de France Femmes
- **Current**: 16,000 entries annually, 30+ L'Etape Series events worldwide in 25 countries

### Notable Editions & Moments
- **2000 Mont Ventoux**: Extreme heat, iconic Tour de France stage
- **2003 Alpe d'Huez**: Record participation at the time
- **2006**: Introduction of dual-event format
- **2011**: Catastrophic weather; only 2,094 finishers out of thousands of starters
- **2013 Col de Sarenne**: Tony Martin publicly criticized the dangerous descent; Christophe Riblon left the road on a corner during the Tour stage but recovered to win
- **2022**: Post-COVID return, huge demand

### Celebrity & Pro Participation
- **Greg LeMond** (2-time Tour winner): Rode the Etape in 2007
- **Victor Lafay**: Won 2018 edition; went on to win Stage 8 of the 2021 Giro d'Italia
- **Jonas Abrahamsen**: Won 2017 edition (now a professional with Uno-X)
- **Lilian Jegou**: Won 2011 Stage 2; former professional (Bretagne-Schuller, Francaise des Jeux)
- **Damon Hill** (F1 World Champion): Competed in 2023 and 2024 editions
- **Gladys Verhulst-Wild**: Professional rider (FDJ-Suez) won 2024 women's race in 5:36:24
- Multiple ex-professionals compete annually

### Controversies & Significant Changes
- COVID cancellations (2020-2021) disrupted momentum
- Occasional course modifications due to weather (shortened stages, reroutes)
- Col de Sarenne safety concerns (2013 TdF edition)
- Shift from dual-event format back to single-event format
- Growth of L'Etape Series worldwide (30+ events in 25 countries) extending the brand beyond France

## Climate (July -- French Alps)

### Typical Weather
- **Valley floor (Bourg d'Oisans, 720m)**: 20-30C (68-86F) during the day. Early morning start can be 10-15C (50-59F)
- **Mid-altitude (Telegraphe, 1,500m)**: 15-22C (59-72F)
- **Summit (Galibier, 2,642m)**: Can be 5-12C (41-54F) even in July. Wind chill drops perceived temperature further. Snow patches possible near summit
- **Alpe d'Huez (1,860m)**: Highs ~20C (68F), lows ~10C (48F) in July
- General summary: Mildly cool at altitude, humid

### Rain/Weather Risk
- ~35% chance of rain on any given July day at Alpe d'Huez altitude
- Afternoon thunderstorms are a significant risk in the Alps (convective storms build over mountain ridges)
- Morning starts often clear, with cloud buildup through the day
- Historic precedent: 2011 edition saw devastating rain; 1996 TdF Galibier stage cancelled due to weather

### Specific Weather Challenges
- **Temperature range on race day**: Riders can experience 25-30C heat in valleys and near-freezing conditions at Galibier summit -- a 20C+ swing
- **Descents**: Long, fast descents from Croix de Fer, Galibier, and Col du Lautaret can cause severe wind chill. Mandatory jacket enforcement
- **Sun exposure**: Extended climbing at high altitude with intense UV. Sunscreen essential
- **Climate change impact**: Recent research (Nature, 2026) indicates increasing heat stress during July cycling events in France. Valleys becoming hotter while summits remain cold, exacerbating the temperature differential

## Community & Culture

### Atmosphere
- Often described as "the world's greatest sportive" and "the most famous mass participation bike ride in the world" (Epic Road Rides)
- The closest an amateur cyclist can get to a full Tour de France experience: same roads, closed to traffic, timing, feed zones, broom wagon, finish line arch
- Emotional, carnival-like atmosphere at start and finish. Massive peloton departure is spectacular
- Tour de France heritage permeates everything: yellow jersey podium ceremony, official Tour branding, TdF-style timing and classifications
- "Road Captains" -- experienced volunteer cyclists who ride within the field providing encouragement and pacing assistance

### Post-Race Events
- Pasta party at the finish line (included in entry fee)
- Event village/expo running 2-3 days around race day with equipment exhibitors, brand activations, Shimano bike prep, Maurten nutrition
- Podium ceremony with Tour de France jerseys for category winners
- L'Etape Championship crowning (top 3 from L'Etape Series events worldwide compete for overall championship at the French Etape)
- Activities for accompanying guests at Alpe d'Huez (hiking, mountain kart, alpine coaster, MTB park, swimming pool, museum)

### International Participation
- 60+ nationalities represented annually
- Strong UK, German, Belgian, Dutch, Australian, American contingents
- L'Etape Series events in 25 countries (Australia, Brazil, Canada, Colombia, Greece, Indonesia, Malaysia, Mexico, Slovenia, USA, etc.) feed riders toward the flagship French event
- 50,000+ riders across the global L'Etape Series annually; 16,000 at the flagship French event

### Media Coverage
- ASO official media (L'Equipe, letapedutourdefrance.com)
- Cycling Weekly, Sportive.com, CyclingNews, BikeRadar, Rouleur coverage
- CNN feature (July 2025): "Cycling like a pro for a day: How amateurs measure themselves up to pros"
- GranFondoGuide Top 10 European Gran Fondos (consistently ranked)
- Specialist cycling travel media (Epic Road Rides, More Than 21 Bends, Mountain Massif)
- Social media: significant Instagram/Strava presence, official L'Etape accounts

### Unique Traditions
- Course replicates a Tour de France stage that the professionals will ride just days later
- Held on a Tour rest day so that riders are literally on the same roads as the Tour
- The broom wagon (voiture-balai) tradition mirrors the real Tour de France
- Finishers receive same-style finish line experience as Tour riders (arch, timing, photos)
- L'Etape Championship program: top performers from worldwide Series events earn free entry and first-wave start
