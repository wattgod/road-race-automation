# Research Dump: Mallorca 312

## Quick Facts
- **Flagship route**: 312 km / 194 mi, 5,050 m / 16,568 ft cumulative elevation gain
- **Route options**:
  - Mallorca 312: 312 km, ~5,050 m climbing, 14-hour time limit (finish by 20:40)
  - Mallorca 225: 225 km, ~3,973 m climbing, finish by 17:00
  - Mallorca 167: 167 km, ~2,475 m climbing, finish by 17:00
- **Registration covers all three routes** — riders choose on race day
- **Location**: Start/finish at Platja de Muro (Playa de Muro), northeast coast of Mallorca, Balearic Islands, Spain
- **Date**: Last Saturday in April each year. 2026 edition: Saturday, April 25, 2026 (XVI edition). 2025 edition: April 26, 2025 (XV edition).
- **Founded**: 2010 (first edition June 2010, 199 participants). Conceived in 2008 by Xisco Lliteras.
- **Field size**: 8,500 participants (2024-2026 capacity). Sold out in 6 minutes for 2024; sells out "almost immediately" for 2025 and 2026.
- **Entry fee**: EUR 159.99 (2026, tax included). Includes race jersey, feed station food/drink, digital diploma, pasta party entry, physiotherapy/massage service.
- **Start format**: Mass start from Platja de Muro. Pens rolling 06:30-07:00. All three distances depart together.
- **Discipline**: Gran fondo / sportive (closed-road)
- **Title sponsor**: OK Mobility. Gold sponsor: Pinarello (3-year deal from 2026).
- **Organizer**: Founded and directed by Xisco Lliteras
- **Countries represented**: 79-80 nations
- **Nationality breakdown** (approximate): Spain 27-38%, United Kingdom 18-30%, Germany 14-16%, with Belgium, Ireland, Norway, Denmark, Netherlands, Switzerland, Poland well-represented
- **Women's participation**: Over 1,000 women in 2023 edition (first time crossing that threshold)
- **Overall completion rate**: ~92% (2016), ~78% (2017, reportedly bad weather year)

## Course Profile

### Route Overview
The 312 km route traces a complete circuit of Mallorca, starting at Platja de Muro on the northeast coast. The route heads north to Port de Pollenca, then turns west into the Serra de Tramuntana mountain range for approximately 120 km of sustained climbing through the island's most dramatic terrain. After the mountains, the route descends to the southern coast, crosses the flat central plain (Pla de Mallorca), and returns north to the start. The critical strategic fact: nearly all major climbing occurs in the first 150-160 km, with the final 150 km largely flat or gently rolling.

### Detailed Route Sequence (312 km)

**Km 0-25: Platja de Muro to Pollenca** — Flat coastal roads. Warm-up section through Alcudia Bay. Groups form.

**Km 25-55: Coll de Femenia and descent to Lluc** — First major climb. Category 2. After the summit, fast descent into the monastery town of Lluc.

**Km 55-70: Lluc to Puig Major (Monnaber Tunnel)** — Steady climbing past the Embassment de Cuber and Gorg Blau reservoirs. Summit at the Monnaber Tunnel (864 m), the highest point on the route. Riders need front/rear lights for the tunnel. First feed station at ~55.2 km (top of Monnaber).

**Km 70-85: Descent to Soller** — Fast technical descent from Puig Major down into the town of Soller.

**Km 85-100: Coll de Soller** — Category 2 climb from Soller side (north approach). 29 hairpin bends. Second feed station at Coll d'en Claret (~93.1 km). **Km 97 is the first time checkpoint (must arrive by 11:15).**

**Km 100-120: Descent and traverse to Andratx** — Winding descent from Coll de Soller into Bunyola, then rolling terrain to Andratx via Valldemossa and Banyalbufar on the spectacular MA-10 coast road.

**Km 120-160: Andratx loop — Es Capdella, Galilea, Puigpunyent, Es Grau** — The southern mountain loop. Multiple short, sharp climbs. Feed station at Es Capdella (~135.3 km). This is the last significant climbing block.

**Km 160-190: Descent to Lloseta and central plain** — Transition from mountains to flat. Feed station at Lloseta (~192.7 km). Terrain becomes rolling farmland.

**Km 190-240: The flatlands — Lloseta to Ariany** — The mental grind. Flat, exposed, potentially windy. Legs are heavy. Feed station at Ariany (~241 km). **Km 220 is the second time checkpoint (must arrive by 16:30).**

**Km 240-285: Ariany to Arta** — Gently rolling through the Pla de Mallorca. Feed station at Arta (~285.2 km).

**Km 285-312: Arta to Platja de Muro** — Final push back to the coast. Flat.

### Key Climbs on the 312 Route

#### 1. Coll de Femenia (first climb of the day)
- **Distance**: 7.5-7.9 km
- **Elevation gain**: ~405-416 m
- **Average gradient**: 5.5-6%
- **Maximum gradient**: 8.5-10%
- **Summit altitude**: 519-575 m (sources vary; official route profile lists 519 m)
- **Category**: 2
- **Character**: Steady, well-graded warm-up climb. The first 2 km are the steepest (6-7%, with ramps to 10%). Shaded sections through pine forest. Beautiful views of the Tramuntana opening up.
- **Route position**: ~km 25-33

#### 2. Puig Major / Monnaber Tunnel (highest point)
- **Distance**: ~14 km from Soller side; ~5 km from Gorg Blau side (the 312 route approaches from the north/Lluc side, a shorter but still substantial climb)
- **Elevation gain**: ~260-400 m (from Gorg Blau reservoir approach on the 312 route)
- **Average gradient**: 5-6% (from north/Lluc approach)
- **Maximum gradient**: 8%
- **Summit altitude**: 864 m (Monnaber Tunnel — public road summit; true summit of Puig Major is 1,436 m but is a military base, inaccessible)
- **Category**: 1 (full climb from Soller); Cat 2-3 from the northern approach used in the 312
- **Character**: The route from Lluc passes the stunning Gorg Blau and Cuber reservoirs. Steady, relentless gradient. The Monnaber Tunnel is dark — lights mandatory. Spectacular descent on the other side toward Soller.
- **Route position**: ~km 45-55 (approach from Lluc side)

#### 3. Coll de Soller (from Soller / north side)
- **Distance**: 8.9 km (north side, from Soller)
- **Elevation gain**: 473 m
- **Average gradient**: 5.3%
- **Maximum gradient**: 9.5%
- **Summit altitude**: 506 m
- **Category**: 2
- **Hairpins**: 29 switchbacks, with 16 concentrated between km 4-6
- **Character**: Classic Mallorcan climb. Shady north side offers relief. Steady effort with no extreme gradients. The old road (pre-tunnel) with gorgeous views.
- **Route position**: ~km 85-94

#### 4. Coll d'en Claret
- **Distance**: 3.7 km
- **Elevation gain**: ~185 m
- **Average gradient**: 5%
- **Maximum gradient**: 8%
- **Summit altitude**: 550 m
- **Category**: 3
- **Character**: Short climb on the MA-10 heading north toward Valldemossa. Part of the spectacular coastal road section.
- **Route position**: ~km 90-93 area

#### 5. Coll d'Orient (from Alaro side)
- **Distance**: 6.1 km
- **Elevation gain**: 274 m
- **Average gradient**: 4.5%
- **Maximum gradient**: 8.5%
- **Summit altitude**: 498 m
- **Category**: 3
- **Character**: Steady approach through olive groves. First of the mid-route inland climbs.
- **Route position**: ~km 105-111

#### 6. Coll d'Honor (from Orient)
- **Distance**: 2.6 km
- **Elevation gain**: 127 m
- **Average gradient**: 4.9%
- **Maximum gradient**: 11.1%
- **Summit altitude**: 550 m
- **Category**: 3
- **Character**: Short but with a sting — the maximum gradient ramps come in concentrated bursts. Follows quickly after Coll d'Orient.
- **Route position**: ~km 112-115

#### 7. Galilea (from Puigpunyent)
- **Distance**: 4.2 km
- **Elevation gain**: 204 m
- **Average gradient**: 4.8%
- **Maximum gradient**: 8%
- **Summit altitude**: 413 m
- **Category**: 3
- **Character**: Short, sharp climb through the village. Part of the southern mountain loop after Andratx.
- **Route position**: ~km 135-139

#### 8. Es Grau / Grau de Superna (from Puigpunyent)
- **Distance**: 4.2 km
- **Elevation gain**: 255 m
- **Average gradient**: 6.1%
- **Maximum gradient**: 9%
- **Summit altitude**: 465 m
- **Category**: 3
- **Character**: The last significant climb of the day. Steeper than it looks on paper. Comes when legs are already carrying 140+ km. The summit marks the transition from mountains to flatlands.
- **Route position**: ~km 150-155

### Sa Calobra — NOT on the Mallorca 312 Route
Sa Calobra (Coll dels Reis) is Mallorca's most famous climb (10.2 km, 708 m gain, 7% avg, 12% max, 26 hairpins, the iconic 270-degree "tie knot" spiral) but it is NOT part of any Mallorca 312 route. The organizers run a separate multi-day tour that includes Sa Calobra. Some tour operators offer a guided Sa Calobra ride on Thursday before the event as an optional activity.

### Road Surface Quality
- **Excellent throughout**. Mallorca is renowned globally for its road quality, and the 312 route uses the island's finest tarmac.
- The MA-10 coastal road through the Serra de Tramuntana is particularly well-maintained — smooth, wide, well-marked.
- Mountain roads in the Tramuntana are good quality asphalt with clear lane markings. Some narrower sections in the Galilea/Puigpunyent area.
- Flat sections through the central plain are standard two-lane roads with good surfaces.
- **Fully closed roads** — the entire route is closed to motor traffic on race day, which is exceptional for a sportive of this scale and adds significantly to road surface safety.

### Descent Character
- Descents are technical but not extreme. The Puig Major descent to Soller is fast (60+ km/h possible) with sweeping bends and good sightlines. The Coll de Soller descent (south side to Bunyola) has tighter switchbacks. No unpaved or rough surfaces. All descents on excellent tarmac.
- Descent technicality is moderate — skilled descenders gain time but there are no truly treacherous sections.

## Logistics

### Nearest Airports
- **Palma de Mallorca Airport (PMI)**: The only commercial airport on the island. ~60 km / 43-minute drive from Platja de Muro. Massive international hub with direct flights from virtually all European capitals and many UK regional airports. Ryanair, easyJet, Eurowings, British Airways, Iberia, Vueling, Jet2, TUI all serve PMI. Budget flights from UK and northern Europe are plentiful in spring.
- No other airports are relevant — PMI is the single entry point.

### Lodging Strategy
- **Platja de Muro / Alcudia Bay**: The start/finish area. Enormous hotel capacity thanks to the resort infrastructure. Hotels range from budget package-holiday properties to cycling-specific accommodations. The VIVA Blue & Spa is a popular cycling-specific hotel with a dedicated cycling centre. Many hotels in Alcudia and Can Picafort are also within easy reach.
- **Book early**: Hotels near the start fill fast, especially cycling-friendly properties. The event selling out 6+ months before race day means accommodation pressure starts early. Many tour operators (Sports Tours International, Sportive Breaks, Stuart Hall Cycling, Huerzeler) offer package deals including accommodation, transfers, and race entry.
- **Alcudia old town** (5 km from start): More character, restaurants, boutique options. Short taxi/ride to start.
- **Pollenca** (15 km): Charming town, quieter, excellent dining. Requires early morning transfer.
- **Palma** (60 km): Only viable if you want a city experience and arrange very early transport.

### Race-Day Transport
- The start area at Platja de Muro is walkable from most nearby hotels.
- Parking at the event village is available but arrives early — with 8,500 riders, congestion is significant.
- Many tour operators provide shuttle buses from partner hotels.
- **Bike bags**: Riders must leave gear bags at the start; they are available at the finish. Mechanical support vehicles are on course.

### Feed Zones (312 km route)
1. **Km 55.2** — Top of Puig Major / Monnaber Tunnel
2. **Km 93.1** — Coll d'en Claret area
3. **Km 135.3** — Es Capdella
4. **Km 192.7** — Lloseta
5. **Km 241** — Ariany
6. **Km 285.2** — Arta

All feed stations are well-stocked with food, water, isotonic drinks, fruit, sandwiches, energy bars, and gels. Numerous volunteers distribute supplies. Medical and mechanical assistance at each station. The first feed station at km 55 means riders should carry sufficient food/water for the first 55 km, which includes the first two major climbs.

### Time Checkpoints
- **Km 97**: Must arrive by 11:15 (approximately 4h45m from start)
- **Km 220**: Must arrive by 16:30 (approximately 10h from start)
- **Finish (312 km)**: Must arrive by 20:40 (approximately 14h10m from start)
- **Finish (167/225 km)**: Must arrive by 17:00

### Registration / Entry Requirements
- Registration opens annually in early October, typically on a Tuesday at 11:00 CET. Sells out within minutes.
- A UCI sportif license or equivalent is NOT required; a medical certificate of fitness is recommended but the specific requirements have varied by year.
- Minimum age: 18 years
- Helmet mandatory. Front and rear lights mandatory (for tunnels).

## History

### Origin Story
The idea for the Mallorca 312 was born in 2008 in a bar in Arta, a small town in northeast Mallorca. Founder Xisco Lliteras — a sports management graduate from the Canary Islands and Barcelona who had worked as a tennis coach and later as a sports coordinator for the Government of the Balearic Islands — was discussing the idea of riding the entire island in a single day. The question "What if we rode around the whole island in one day?" became the seed.

Lliteras had the organizational expertise from his government role, where he had connections to sports federations and experience managing international events at Mallorca's MegaSport Centre. He spent two years planning the logistics.

### First Edition (June 2010)
- 199 participants (Lliteras had hoped for at least 500 and was "a little disappointed")
- Almost entirely local riders from Mallorca and mainland Spain
- The only international contingent: 13 Irishmen who arrived on a private jet
- Held in June (later moved to April to align with peak cycling tourism season)

### Growth Timeline
- **2010**: 199 riders (Edition I)
- **By mid-2010s**: Rapid international growth. The event moved to April and began attracting northern European cyclists who used Mallorca as a spring training destination.
- **2017**: Sold out. The event began selling out every year.
- **2022**: Sold out 8,000 entries in 10 hours (then a record).
- **2023**: Sold out 8,000 entries in 10 hours. Over 1,000 women participated for the first time. XIII edition. Route options expanded to include 167, 225, and 312 km.
- **2024**: Sold out 8,000 entries in 6 minutes — a new record. 8,500 riders participated in the XIV edition. Participants from 79 countries. Course record smashed by 20 minutes.
- **2025**: 8,500 capacity. Sold out "almost immediately." XV edition, April 26, 2025.
- **2026**: 8,500 capacity. XVI edition, April 25, 2026. Sold out. Pinarello announced as new Gold Sponsor for 3 years. OK Mobility continues as title sponsor.

### Notable Milestones
- Europe's longest one-day closed-road sportive (312 km)
- The event is widely cited as one of the top 3-5 gran fondos in the world
- From 199 riders in 2010 to 8,500 in 2024 — a 42x growth in 14 years
- Regularly described as "more than a gran fondo" — it has become Mallorca's signature sporting event

### Pro Cyclist Appearances
The event has attracted numerous retired professional cycling legends as ambassadors and participants:
- **Alberto Contador** (3x Grand Tour winner) — completed the 225 km route in 7h 12m 58s (2021 edition)
- **Jan Ullrich** (1997 Tour de France winner) — completed the 312 km in 10h 59m 56s (2021)
- **Oscar Freire** (3x World Champion) — completed the 167 km in 6h 40m 41s (2021)
- **Miguel Indurain** (5x Tour de France winner) — led out the start in 2022
- **Alejandro Valverde** (World Champion, Vuelta winner) — special guest with bib #312
- **Ivan Basso** (2x Giro d'Italia winner)
- **Joseba Beloki** (3x Tour de France podium finisher)
- **Sean Kelly** (legend of the classics)
- **Pedro Horrillo**, **Haimar Zubeldia**, **Maurizio Fondriest**

### Tragic Incident
In 2025, a British cyclist died in a crash during the event — a reminder of the inherent risks in high-speed descending on mountain roads with large fields.

## Climate

### Typical Race-Day Weather (Late April in Mallorca)
- **Temperature**: Daytime highs 19-22C (66-72F). Morning lows 12-14C (54-57F). Pre-dawn start at 06:30 will feel cool (12-15C), particularly at mountain altitude.
- **Mountain temperatures**: Summits at 500-870 m will be 5-8C cooler than sea level. Expect 10-15C on Puig Major at the Monnaber Tunnel in morning hours. Arm warmers and a gilet/windbreaker essential for descents.
- **Sunshine**: ~8 hours of sunshine per day in April. The sun rises around 07:00 and sets around 20:30, so the entire race takes place in daylight (even the 312 km with 14-hour limit).
- **Precipitation**: Average 39 mm over ~5 rainy days in April. Showers are typically brief and sporadic. The probability of a completely dry race day is ~80%. However, April can be unpredictable — cold, wet, and windy days are possible though increasingly rare in recent years.
- **Wind**: Average wind speed ~13 km/h in April. Probability of strong winds is low. The flat sections through the central plain (km 160-285) are the most wind-exposed; a headwind here can be demoralizing on tired legs.
- **Sea temperature**: ~16C — not warm enough for post-race swimming for most.
- **Summary**: Conditions are generally excellent for endurance cycling. Cool mornings warm to pleasant afternoons. Rain is a minor risk. The main climate challenge is cold on mountain descents in the early hours and potential wind on the flats in the afternoon.

## Community & Culture

### Atmosphere
The Mallorca 312 has developed a festival atmosphere that extends well beyond race day. The event village at Platja de Muro opens several days before the race with expo, pasta party, bike demos, and social events. The Friday evening pasta party is a major social gathering. The start-line atmosphere at dawn — 8,500 riders in corrals as the sun rises over Alcudia Bay — is routinely described as electric and emotional.

The post-race celebration includes music, food, massage tents, and a strong sense of accomplishment. The "finisher" culture is strong — this is not a race for most participants but a personal challenge. The digital diploma and official jersey reinforce the achievement.

### International Character
With participants from 79-80 countries and roughly 60-70% coming from outside Spain, the event is one of the most internationally diverse sportives in the world. The UK contingent (18-30%) is particularly large, reflecting Mallorca's popularity as a British cycling destination. German and Northern European participation is also substantial. This international mix creates a multilingual, cosmopolitan atmosphere unusual for a sporting event.

### Mallorca as a Cycling Destination
Mallorca is arguably the number one cycling destination in Europe:
- **Pro team training**: Virtually every WorldTour team trains in Mallorca in January-March. Team Sky/INEOS Grenadiers, Movistar, Bora-Hansgrohe, and others are regular visitors.
- **100,000+ cyclists per year** visit the island for training and holidays.
- **Infrastructure**: Extensive cycling-specific hotels, bike rental shops, guided tours, cycling cafes, and repair services. The cycling economy is a major contributor to the island's tourism industry.
- **Roads**: World-renowned road quality. The Serra de Tramuntana provides 50+ categorized climbs. The flat coastal roads and rolling interior offer recovery routes. It is the "complete package" for road cyclists.
- **Climate**: Rideable 10+ months per year. Peak cycling season is February-May and September-November. Summers are too hot for most (35C+).
- **Accessibility**: PMI airport has direct flights from 150+ European cities. Flight times: 2h from London, 2h from Frankfurt, 2.5h from Scandinavia.
- **UNESCO World Heritage**: The Serra de Tramuntana is a UNESCO Cultural Landscape, adding prestige to any ride through these mountains.

### Media Coverage
The event generates significant media coverage in cycling media (Rouleur, Cyclist, CyclingWeekly, GranFondoGuide, road.cc) and Mallorcan/Spanish general media. The Mallorca Daily Bulletin and Palma Weekly cover it as a major island event. The event's social media presence is strong, with participants generating enormous user-generated content.

### Community Tension
The event's scale (8,500 riders, extensive road closures for up to 7 hours) has generated some local controversy. Residents in affected villages have complained about "abusive" road closures that prevent them from entering or leaving their homes, with some calling for the event to be cancelled or scaled back. The organizers and local government have worked to mitigate these concerns, but it remains a point of tension between the cycling tourism economy and local quality of life.

### Economic Impact
The Mallorca 312 is a major economic driver for the island, particularly valuable because it falls in April — before the main summer tourism season. 8,500 riders plus their companions, staying multiple days, spending on accommodation, dining, bike services, and tourism, provide significant off-peak revenue.

## Scoring Dimension Notes

### Distance (5/5)
312 km is one of the longest one-day sportives in the world. Even the shortest option (167 km) is a substantial ride. The 312 km route is genuinely extreme distance.

### Climbing (4-5/5)
5,050 m of elevation gain is substantial, though the climbs individually are moderate in gradient (mostly 5-6% average). The volume of climbing is the challenge, not extreme steepness. Comparable to or exceeding most gran fondos.

### Descent Technicality (3/5)
Technical descents exist (Puig Major, Coll de Soller) with good road surfaces. Fast but not treacherous. Hairpins are well-sighted. Closed roads reduce risk significantly.

### Road Surface (5/5)
World-class tarmac. Mallorca is universally regarded as having some of the best cycling road surfaces in Europe. Fully closed roads on race day.

### Climate Risk (2/5 - low risk)
Late April in Mallorca is near-ideal for cycling. Warm days, cool mornings, low precipitation risk. Occasional rain possible but rarely severe. Wind on the flats is the main variable.

### Altitude (2/5)
Highest point is 864 m (Monnaber Tunnel). No meaningful altitude effects. Start/finish near sea level.

### Logistics (4/5)
Single airport (PMI) with excellent European connectivity. Resort infrastructure means abundant accommodation. 60 km transfer from airport. Event is impeccably organized with feed stations, mechanical support, medical support, and closed roads. The main logistics challenge is that it sells out in minutes, requiring advance planning.

### Prestige (5/5)
One of the most prestigious gran fondos in the world. Sells out in 6 minutes. 79-80 countries represented. Pro cycling legends attend as ambassadors. Pinarello as gold sponsor. Featured in every major cycling publication. The "bucket list" sportive for road cyclists.

### Organization (5/5)
Exceptional. Closed roads for the entire 312 km route (unprecedented at this scale). Six well-stocked feed stations. Medical and mechanical support throughout. Time checkpoints. Digital timing and results. Pasta party. Post-race massage. Jersey included. The event has scaled from 199 to 8,500 riders while maintaining quality.

### Scenic Experience (5/5)
The Serra de Tramuntana is a UNESCO World Heritage Cultural Landscape. The route traverses some of the most beautiful cycling roads in Europe: dramatic limestone mountains, Mediterranean coast, pine forests, ancient olive groves, turquoise reservoirs, charming villages. The MA-10 coast road section and the Puig Major / Gorg Blau section are world-class scenic cycling.

### Community & Culture (5/5)
8,500 riders from 79-80 countries. Festival atmosphere. Strong finisher culture. Deep integration with Mallorca's cycling identity. Pro cycling legends as ambassadors. Post-race celebrations. The event has become Mallorca's signature sporting event and a symbol of the island's cycling culture. Over 1,000 women participants.

### Field Depth (4/5)
While primarily a mass-participation sportive (not a race), the field includes serious amateur racers, ex-professionals, and strong club riders from across Europe. The presence of retired Grand Tour winners as participants adds depth. Course records are competitive.

### Value (4/5)
EUR 159.99 includes race entry, official jersey, feed station food/drink, pasta party, massage service, and digital diploma. For a one-day event of this scale and quality, this represents good value. The jersey alone would retail for EUR 60-80.

### Expenses (3/5 - moderate)
Flights to Mallorca are generally affordable from European origins (budget airlines serve PMI extensively). Accommodation in Platja de Muro is resort-priced but not extreme (EUR 80-150/night for decent hotels). Bike transport/rental adds cost. Tour operator packages range from EUR 500-1,500+ depending on duration and inclusions. Overall, a 4-5 day trip with flights, hotel, entry, and dining would run EUR 800-1,500 for a European participant.
